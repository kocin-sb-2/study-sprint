S1.1 — Introduction to the Particulate Nature of Matter
Elements, compounds, mixtures. Kinetic molecular theory. States of matter and phase changes. Separation techniques.
▸
📄 Past Papers & Resources
IB Chemistry past papers — Smashing Science ↗ IB Chemistry revision — ibchemistry.com ↗

Key exam patterns: stoichiometry calculations appear every session. Practice mole calculations, limiting reagent, and percentage yield.

Key Concepts
Elements, compounds, mixtures: An element consists of one type of atom. A compound is two or more elements chemically bonded in fixed ratios. A mixture contains two or more substances not chemically bonded, with variable composition.
Kinetic molecular theory: All matter consists of particles in constant motion. Temperature is a measure of average kinetic energy. Higher temperature = faster particles. This explains properties of solids, liquids, and gases.
States of matter: Solids — particles vibrate in fixed positions, strong forces, fixed shape/volume. Liquids — particles can move past each other, moderate forces, fixed volume but variable shape. Gases — particles far apart, negligible forces, variable shape and volume.
Phase changes: Melting (s→l), freezing (l→s), boiling/evaporation (l→g), condensation (g→l), sublimation (s→g), deposition (g→s). During phase changes, temperature remains constant as energy is used to break/form intermolecular bonds.
Separation techniques: Filtration (insoluble solid from liquid), distillation (different boiling points), chromatography (different affinities for stationary/mobile phases), evaporation (solute from solution), fractional distillation (similar boiling points).
Key Terms & Definitions
Element: A pure substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. All atoms have the same atomic number.
Compound: A substance formed from two or more elements chemically combined in a fixed ratio.
Mixture: Two or more substances physically combined, not chemically bonded, that can be separated by physical methods.
Homogeneous mixture: A mixture with uniform composition throughout (e.g., a solution).
Heterogeneous mixture: A mixture with non-uniform composition — different parts are visible (e.g., sand and water).
Exam Focus
Classify substances as elements, compounds, or mixtures from descriptions or particle diagrams.
Use kinetic molecular theory to explain observations about states of matter, pressure, and diffusion.
Choose an appropriate separation technique for a given mixture and explain how it works.
🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

Distinguish between an element, a compound, and a mixture.

Element: A pure substance made of only one type of atom (same atomic number). Cannot be broken down by chemical means. [1]
Compound: A pure substance made of two or more elements chemically bonded in a fixed ratio. Has different properties from its constituent elements. Mixture: two or more substances not chemically bonded, in variable proportions, retaining individual properties. [1]
Common error: Saying a compound is "mixed elements". IB requires "chemically bonded in a fixed ratio" — this distinguishes compounds from mixtures.
Application[3 marks]L2

Explain, using kinetic molecular theory, why a gas fills its container but a liquid does not.

Gas particles: Have high kinetic energy, move rapidly and randomly in all directions. Intermolecular forces are negligible, so particles spread to fill the entire container. [1]
Liquid particles: Have lower kinetic energy. Intermolecular forces are strong enough to keep particles close together but not in fixed positions — they can slide past each other. [1]
Key difference: In a gas, KE >> intermolecular PE, so particles escape each other. In a liquid, KE ≈ intermolecular PE, maintaining a definite volume but not shape. [1]
Common error: Saying gas particles "have no forces between them". They do have forces — the forces are just negligible compared to kinetic energy at normal conditions.
Analysis[3 marks]L2

A student heats a pure substance and records temperature vs time. The graph shows two flat (horizontal) regions. Explain what is happening at the molecular level during these flat regions.

Phase change: The flat regions represent phase changes (melting and boiling). Temperature remains constant despite continuous heating. [1]
Energy use: All energy input is used to overcome intermolecular forces (breaking bonds between molecules), not to increase kinetic energy. Since temperature measures average KE, it stays constant. [1]
Two plateaus: First plateau = solid→liquid (melting, latent heat of fusion). Second plateau = liquid→gas (boiling, latent heat of vaporisation). [1]
Common error: Saying "bonds are broken" without specifying intermolecular forces. In phase changes, intermolecular forces are overcome — covalent bonds within molecules remain intact.
S1.2 — The Nuclear Atom
Protons, neutrons, electrons. Isotopes. Mass spectrometry and relative atomic mass. HL Fragmentation patterns.
▸
Key Concepts
Subatomic particles: Protons (charge +1, mass 1 u, in nucleus), neutrons (charge 0, mass 1 u, in nucleus), electrons (charge −1, mass ~1/1836 u, in electron shells). Atomic number (Z) = number of protons. Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons.
Isotopes: Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. Same chemical properties (same electron configuration) but different physical properties (mass, rate of diffusion, nuclear stability).
Mass spectrometry: An instrument that measures the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) of ions. Steps: vaporisation → ionisation → acceleration → deflection → detection. The mass spectrum shows peaks at different m/z values, with height proportional to abundance.
Relative atomic mass (Ar): The weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element, measured relative to ¹²C = 12.000. Calculated from mass spectrum data.
HL: Fragmentation: In mass spectrometry, molecules can break apart (fragment) after ionisation. The fragmentation pattern helps identify the molecular structure. The peak with the highest m/z (excluding isotope peaks) is typically the molecular ion peak (M⁺).
Key Terms & Definitions
Atomic number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. Defines the element.
Mass number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.
Isotopes: Atoms of the same element (same Z) with different mass numbers (different number of neutrons).
Relative atomic mass (Ar): The weighted mean mass of an atom of an element relative to 1/12 the mass of a ¹²C atom.
Mass spectrum: A plot of relative abundance vs m/z ratio, showing the isotopes or fragments of a substance.
Molecular ion peak (M⁺): The peak in a mass spectrum corresponding to the intact molecule minus one electron.
Key Formulas
Ar = Σ (isotope mass × fractional abundance)
Relative atomic mass from mass spectrum data

🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

Define isotopes and explain why isotopes of the same element have identical chemical properties.

Definition: Isotopes are atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons (same Z, different A). [1]
Same chemistry: Chemical properties depend on electron configuration, which is determined by the number of protons (= number of electrons in a neutral atom). Since isotopes have the same number of protons, they have the same electron configuration and identical chemical behaviour. [1]
Common error: Saying isotopes have "different numbers of electrons". Neutral isotopes have the same number of electrons. They differ in neutrons only.
Calculation[3 marks]L2

Chlorine has two isotopes: ³⁵Cl (75.77%) and ³⁷Cl (24.23%). Calculate the relative atomic mass of chlorine.

Formula: Ar = Σ(isotope mass × fractional abundance) [1]
Calculation: Ar = (35 × 0.7577) + (37 × 0.2423) = 26.52 + 8.97 = 35.49 [1]
Answer: Ar(Cl) = 35.5 (to 3 s.f., matching the periodic table value) [1]
Common error: Using percentages (75.77) instead of fractions (0.7577) in the calculation. This gives an answer ~100× too large.
Analysis[3 marks]L2

A mass spectrum of an element shows peaks at m/z = 63 (69.2%) and m/z = 65 (30.8%). Identify the element and explain how mass spectrometry determines isotopic composition.

Ar: (63 × 0.692) + (65 × 0.308) = 43.60 + 20.02 = 63.6. This matches copper (Cu, Ar = 63.55). [1]
MS process: Atoms are ionised, accelerated, and deflected by a magnetic field. Lighter ions are deflected more. Each isotope produces a separate peak at its m/z value. [1]
Abundance: The height (or area) of each peak is proportional to the relative abundance of that isotope in the sample. [1]
Common error: Confusing m/z with mass number. For singly charged ions (z = 1), m/z equals the mass number. For multiply charged ions, m/z is smaller.
S1.3 — Electron Configurations
Emission spectra, energy levels, sublevels (s, p, d, f). Orbital diagrams. HL Ionisation energies. PES.
▸
Key Concepts
Emission spectra: When atoms are excited (by heat or electricity), electrons absorb energy and jump to higher energy levels. When they fall back down, they emit photons of specific energies, producing a line emission spectrum. This proves energy levels are quantised.
Energy levels and sublevels: Main energy levels (n = 1, 2, 3...) contain sublevels: s (2e⁻), p (6e⁻), d (10e⁻), f (14e⁻). Each sublevel contains orbitals, each holding max 2 electrons with opposite spins.
Electron configuration rules: Aufbau principle (fill lowest energy first), Hund's rule (fill orbitals of the same sublevel singly before pairing), Pauli exclusion principle (max 2 electrons per orbital, opposite spins). Exception examples: Cr is [Ar]3d⁵4s¹, Cu is [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹.
HL: Successive ionisation energies: Removing electrons one at a time from an atom requires increasing energy. Large jumps in IE indicate crossing into a new (inner) energy level. Data from successive IEs reveals the number of electrons in each shell.
HL: Photoelectron spectroscopy (PES): Measures the binding energies of all electrons in an atom. Each peak corresponds to a sublevel. Peak height ∝ number of electrons in that sublevel. Provides direct experimental evidence for sublevel structure.
Key Terms & Definitions
Electron configuration: The arrangement of electrons in an atom's energy levels and sublevels (e.g., 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶).
Orbital: A region of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron. Each orbital holds max 2 electrons.
Aufbau principle: Electrons fill the lowest available energy level first.
Hund's rule: Electrons occupy orbitals of the same sublevel singly (with parallel spins) before pairing up.
Pauli exclusion principle: No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers (max 2 per orbital, opposite spins).
Ionisation energy (IE): The energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms.
Exam Focus
Write full and condensed electron configurations for atoms and ions (including d-block elements and their ions).
Explain the relationship between emission spectra and electron energy levels.
HL: Interpret PES spectra to determine electron configuration. Analyse successive IE data to deduce the number of electrons in each shell.
🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

Write the full electron configuration of iron (Fe, Z = 26) and its Fe³⁺ ion.

Fe: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 3d⁶ 4s² (or [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s²). Note: 4s fills before 3d. [1]
Fe³⁺: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 3d⁵ (or [Ar] 3d⁵). Electrons are removed from 4s first, then 3d. Fe³⁺ has a half-filled 3d subshell, which is particularly stable. [1]
Common error: Removing electrons from 3d before 4s when forming ions. Although 4s fills first, 4s electrons are removed first when forming cations (4s has higher energy in the ion).
Application[3 marks]L2

Explain why the first ionisation energy of oxygen (1314 kJ mol⁻¹) is lower than that of nitrogen (1402 kJ mol⁻¹), despite oxygen having a higher nuclear charge.

N configuration: 2p³ — three electrons in separate 2p orbitals (half-filled, all parallel spins). This is a particularly stable arrangement. [1]
O configuration: 2p⁴ — one 2p orbital contains a pair of electrons. The electron-electron repulsion in this paired orbital makes it easier to remove one electron. [1]
Conclusion: The extra stability of nitrogen's half-filled 2p subshell outweighs the effect of oxygen's higher nuclear charge, making N's IE₁ higher. [1]
Common error: Only discussing nuclear charge without mentioning electron-electron repulsion in the paired orbital. Both factors must be addressed for full marks.
S1.4 — Counting Particles by Mass: The Mole
Mole concept, Avogadro's constant. Molar mass, empirical/molecular formulae. HL Molar volume. Real vs ideal gases.
▸
Key Concepts
The mole: One mole contains exactly 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's constant, Nₐ). It is the amount of substance containing as many particles as there are atoms in 12 g of ¹²C.
Molar mass (M): The mass of one mole of a substance, numerically equal to its relative formula mass in g/mol. n = m/M.
Empirical vs molecular formula: Empirical formula = simplest whole-number ratio of atoms (e.g., CH₂O). Molecular formula = actual number of atoms in one molecule (e.g., C₆H₁₂O₆). To find molecular from empirical: divide molar mass by empirical formula mass.
Concentration: c = n/V, where c is in mol/dm³ (M), n in mol, V in dm³. Dilution: c₁V₁ = c₂V₂.
HL: Molar volume of gases: At STP (0°C, 100 kPa), one mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.7 dm³. This follows from the ideal gas law and is independent of the gas identity (Avogadro's law).
Key Terms & Definitions
Mole (mol): The SI unit of amount of substance. One mole = 6.022 × 10²³ entities.
Avogadro's constant (Nₐ): 6.022 × 10²³ mol⁻¹ — the number of particles in one mole.
Molar mass (M): The mass per mole of a substance. Unit: g/mol.
Empirical formula: The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound.
Molecular formula: The actual number of each type of atom in one molecule of a compound.
Concentration (c): Amount of solute per unit volume of solution. Unit: mol/dm³.
Key Formulas

Recall[2 marks]L1

Define the mole and state Avogadro's constant.

Mole: The amount of substance that contains exactly 6.022 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, ions, etc.). It is the SI unit of amount of substance. [1]
Avogadro's constant: Nₐ = 6.022 × 10²³ mol⁻¹. One mole of any substance contains this many particles. The molar mass in grams equals the relative formula mass. [1]

Calculation[4 marks]L2

What mass of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) is needed to produce 5.00 g of carbon dioxide (CO₂) by thermal decomposition? CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂. (Ar: Ca=40.08, C=12.01, O=16.00)

M(CO₂): 12.01 + 2(16.00) = 44.01 g mol⁻¹ [1]

S1.5 — Ideal Gases
Ideal gas model and assumptions. Gas laws. PV = nRT. HL Deviations from ideal behaviour.
▸
Key Concepts
Ideal gas assumptions: (1) Gas particles have negligible volume compared to the container. (2) No intermolecular forces between particles. (3) Collisions are perfectly elastic. (4) Particles are in constant, random motion. (5) Average KE is proportional to absolute temperature.
Gas laws: Boyle's law: P ∝ 1/V (constant T, n). Charles' law: V ∝ T (constant P, n). Gay-Lussac's: P ∝ T (constant V, n). Avogadro's: V ∝ n (constant T, P). All combined into PV = nRT.
Ideal gas equation: PV = nRT, where R = 8.314 J/(mol·K). T must be in Kelvin (K = °C + 273). P in Pa, V in m³ (or P in kPa, V in dm³).
HL: Real gas deviations: Real gases deviate from ideal behaviour at high pressure (particle volume becomes significant) and low temperature (intermolecular forces become important). Gas molecules with stronger IMFs and larger sizes deviate more.
Key Terms & Definitions
Ideal gas: A hypothetical gas that perfectly obeys PV = nRT under all conditions.
STP (Standard Temperature and Pressure): 0°C (273.15 K) and 100 kPa (IB definition).
Gas constant (R): 8.314 J/(mol·K) — the proportionality constant in the ideal gas equation.
Absolute zero: 0 K (−273.15°C) — the theoretical temperature at which all particle motion ceases.
Key Formulas
PV = nRT
Ideal gas equation
P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂
Combined gas law (constant n)
T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15
Celsius to Kelvin conversion

🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

State the ideal gas equation and define each variable.

Equation: PV = nRT, where P = pressure (Pa), V = volume (m³), n = amount (mol), R = gas constant (8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹), T = temperature (K). [1]
Conditions: Works best at high temperature and low pressure (where real gases behave most ideally). Deviations occur at high P and low T. [1]
Common error: Using °C instead of K, or cm³ instead of m³. PV = nRT requires SI units: Pa, m³, mol, K. Convert: 1 dm³ = 10⁻³ m³, 1 kPa = 10³ Pa.
Calculation[3 marks]L2

Calculate the volume occupied by 0.250 mol of an ideal gas at 25.0°C and 101.3 kPa. R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹.

Convert: T = 25.0 + 273.15 = 298.15 K. P = 101.3 × 10³ = 101300 Pa. [1]
V = nRT/P: V = 0.250 × 8.314 × 298.15 / 101300 = 619.8/101300 = 6.12 × 10⁻³ m³ [1]
Answer: V = 6.12 dm³ (= 6.12 × 10⁻³ m³). At STP, 1 mol occupies 22.7 dm³, so 0.25 mol ≈ 5.7 dm³ — our answer at slightly above STP is reasonable. [1]
Common error: Forgetting to convert kPa to Pa or °C to K. These unit errors are the most common source of wrong answers in gas calculations.

S2.1 — The Ionic Model
Ionic bonding, lattice structures, properties. HL Born-Haber cycles. Lattice enthalpy. Polarisation.
▸
Key Concepts
Ionic bonding: Transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal, forming cations (+) and anions (−). The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions forms the ionic bond. Ionic compounds form giant lattice structures.
Properties of ionic compounds: High melting/boiling points (strong electrostatic forces in lattice). Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (ions free to move), but not as solids (ions fixed). Generally soluble in water (polar solvent). Brittle (like charges repel when layers shift).
HL: Born-Haber cycles: An application of Hess's law to calculate lattice enthalpy indirectly using known enthalpy changes: atomisation, ionisation, electron affinity, formation enthalpy. A higher lattice enthalpy indicates a stronger ionic bond.
HL: Polarisation: Small, highly charged cations can distort the electron cloud of large anions, introducing covalent character to the bond (Fajans' rules). This means ionic and covalent bonding exist on a continuum.
Key Terms & Definitions
Ionic bond: The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
Lattice structure: A regular, repeating 3D arrangement of ions in an ionic compound.
Lattice enthalpy: The enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic compound is formed from its gaseous ions (exothermic) — or separated into gaseous ions (endothermic, depending on definition).
Electron affinity: The enthalpy change when one mole of electrons is added to one mole of gaseous atoms.
Fajans' rules: Rules predicting when an ionic bond will have significant covalent character (small cation, large anion, high charge).
Exam Focus
Explain properties of ionic compounds (melting point, conductivity, solubility) in terms of structure and bonding.
HL: Construct and use a Born-Haber cycle to calculate lattice enthalpy or another unknown enthalpy change.
HL: Compare theoretical and experimental lattice enthalpies to assess covalent character.
🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

Describe ionic bonding and explain why ionic compounds have high melting points.

Ionic bonding: The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions, formed by the transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal. [1]
High mp: Ionic compounds form a giant lattice structure with strong electrostatic forces in all directions. A large amount of energy is needed to overcome these forces, resulting in high melting points. [1]
Common error: Saying "ionic bonds are broken" when melting. The lattice structure is disrupted, but the ions themselves remain — they just become free to move.
Application[3 marks]L2

Explain why NaCl conducts electricity when molten or dissolved in water, but not as a solid.

Solid: Ions are held in fixed positions in the lattice. They cannot move, so they cannot carry charge. No conduction. [1]
Molten: The lattice breaks down. Ions are free to move and carry charge toward electrodes. Conducts electricity. [1]
Dissolved: Water molecules surround and separate the ions (hydration). Free ions in solution can move and carry charge. [1]
Common error: Saying "electrons flow" in ionic conduction. Ionic substances conduct via ion movement, not electron flow. This is a key distinction from metallic conduction.
S2.2 — The Covalent Model
Lewis structures. VSEPR theory. Polarity. HL Resonance. Formal charge. Hybridisation.
▸
Key Concepts
Covalent bonding: Sharing of electron pairs between non-metal atoms. A single bond shares 1 pair, double bond shares 2 pairs, triple bond shares 3 pairs. Bond strength increases and bond length decreases as bond order increases.
Lewis (dot) structures: Diagrams showing all valence electrons, bonding pairs, and lone pairs around atoms. Follow the octet rule (8 electrons around each atom, except H which gets 2). Count total valence electrons, form bonds, distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs.
VSEPR theory: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion — electron pairs (bonding and lone) around a central atom arrange themselves to minimise repulsion. This determines molecular geometry. Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs, compressing bond angles.
Common shapes: Linear (2 e⁻ domains, 180°), trigonal planar (3, 120°), tetrahedral (4, 109.5°), trigonal bipyramidal (5, 90°/120°), octahedral (6, 90°). With lone pairs: bent, trigonal pyramidal, T-shaped, seesaw, square planar, etc.
Polarity: A bond is polar if atoms have different electronegativities. A molecule is polar if it has polar bonds arranged asymmetrically (net dipole moment ≠ 0). Symmetric molecules (e.g., CO₂, CCl₄) are non-polar despite having polar bonds.
HL: Resonance: When a Lewis structure can be drawn in multiple valid ways (e.g., O₃, CO₃²⁻, benzene). The actual structure is a hybrid — an average of all resonance structures. Bond order is fractional.
HL: Hybridisation: The mixing of atomic orbitals to form new hybrid orbitals for bonding. sp³ (tetrahedral, 4 σ bonds), sp² (trigonal planar, 3 σ + 1 π), sp (linear, 2 σ + 2 π). The number of σ bonds + lone pairs on the central atom determines hybridisation.
Key Terms & Definitions
Covalent bond: A bond formed by the sharing of one or more pairs of electrons between atoms.
Lone pair: A pair of valence electrons not involved in bonding.
Electronegativity: The ability of an atom to attract a shared pair of electrons in a covalent bond.
VSEPR: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion — a model for predicting molecular geometry.
Dipole moment: A measure of the polarity of a molecule, determined by the magnitude and direction of bond dipoles.
Sigma (σ) bond: A bond formed by head-on overlap of orbitals (single bonds).
Pi (π) bond: A bond formed by sideways overlap of p-orbitals (part of double/triple bonds).
Formal charge: The charge on an atom in a Lewis structure = valence e⁻ − lone pair e⁻ − ½(bonding e⁻). Preferred structures minimise formal charges.
Exam Focus
Draw Lewis structures for molecules and ions. Predict the shape and bond angle using VSEPR. Determine polarity.
HL: Draw resonance structures and explain how resonance affects bond length and strength.
HL: Determine hybridisation of the central atom from the number of electron domains.
🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

Define a covalent bond and explain the difference between a sigma (σ) and a pi (π) bond.

Covalent bond: A shared pair of electrons between two non-metal atoms, resulting from the overlap of atomic orbitals. The electrostatic attraction between the shared electrons and both nuclei holds the atoms together. [1]
σ vs π: A σ bond is formed by head-on (axial) overlap of orbitals — electron density is concentrated along the internuclear axis. A π bond is formed by lateral (sideways) overlap of p orbitals — electron density is above and below the axis. A double bond = 1σ + 1π. [1]
Common error: Saying a double bond is "two covalent bonds". IB requires you to specify it is one sigma and one pi bond. A triple bond = 1σ + 2π.
Application[3 marks]L2

Draw the Lewis structure of SO₂ and predict its shape using VSEPR theory. Include any lone pairs and formal charges.

Lewis structure: S is central with 2 bonding pairs (double bonds to each O) and 1 lone pair. Total electron domains on S = 3. One resonance structure has S=O and S→O (dative bond). [1]
Shape: 3 electron domains → trigonal planar electron geometry. With 1 lone pair, molecular shape is bent/V-shaped. Bond angle ≈ 119° (slightly less than 120° due to lone pair repulsion). [1]
Polarity: The bent shape means the dipole moments do not cancel → SO₂ is a polar molecule. [1]
Common error: Drawing SO₂ as linear (like CO₂). SO₂ has a lone pair on S that CO₂ lacks, making it bent. Always count electron domains on the central atom.
S2.3 — The Metallic Model
Metallic bonding. Physical properties. HL Transition metals and d-electron delocalisation.
▸
Key Concepts
Metallic bonding: A lattice of positive metal cations surrounded by a "sea" of delocalised electrons. The electrostatic attraction between the cations and the delocalised electrons holds the structure together.
Properties explained: High melting points (strong attraction). Electrical conductivity (delocalised electrons can flow). Thermal conductivity (electrons transfer kinetic energy). Malleability and ductility (layers can slide past each other without breaking bonds — electrons adjust).
Factors affecting strength: More delocalised electrons per atom and smaller ionic radius → stronger metallic bonding → higher melting point. E.g., Na < Mg < Al (1, 2, 3 delocalised e⁻ respectively).
HL: Transition metals: Have partially filled d-orbitals. The d-electrons can also delocalise, contributing to very strong metallic bonding. This explains why transition metals generally have higher melting points than s-block metals.
Key Terms & Definitions
Metallic bond: The electrostatic attraction between a lattice of metal cations and delocalised electrons.
Delocalised electrons: Electrons not associated with a single atom but shared across the entire metallic lattice.
Alloy: A mixture of a metal with other metals or non-metals. Different-sized atoms disrupt regular layers, making alloys harder and less malleable.
Exam Focus
Explain properties of metals (conductivity, malleability, melting point) using the metallic bonding model.
Compare metallic bonding strength across elements (e.g., Na vs Mg vs Al) and explain trends.
Explain why alloys are harder than pure metals in terms of structure.
🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

Describe the metallic bonding model and explain why metals are good conductors of electricity.

Model: Metallic bonding is the electrostatic attraction between a lattice of positive metal cations and a "sea" of delocalised electrons. [1]
Conductivity: The delocalised electrons are free to move throughout the structure. When a potential difference is applied, these electrons flow as an electric current. [1]
Common error: Saying "metal atoms share electrons". In metallic bonding, electrons are delocalised across the entire structure, not shared between specific atoms (that would be covalent).
Analysis[3 marks]L2

Explain why sodium (Na) has a lower melting point than magnesium (Mg), despite both being metals.

Na: Has 1 delocalised electron per atom, ionic radius is larger, charge on cation is +1. Metallic bonding is relatively weak. [1]
Mg: Has 2 delocalised electrons per atom, smaller ionic radius, charge +2. More electrons in the sea and higher charge density → stronger metallic bonding. [1]
Conclusion: Stronger metallic bonding in Mg requires more energy to break → higher melting point (Na: 98°C, Mg: 650°C). [1]
Common error: Only mentioning "more electrons" without also discussing charge density and ionic radius. IB mark schemes typically require at least two factors.
S2.4 — From Models to Materials
Intermolecular forces: LDF, dipole-dipole, H-bonding. Allotropes of carbon. HL Polymers. Supramolecular chemistry.
▸
Key Concepts
Intermolecular forces (IMFs): Forces between molecules (NOT within them). Three types in order of increasing strength: London dispersion forces (LDF) — present in ALL molecules, stronger with more electrons/larger surface area. Dipole-dipole — between polar molecules. Hydrogen bonding — special strong dipole-dipole between H bonded to N/O/F and a lone pair on N/O/F.
Effect on physical properties: Stronger IMFs → higher boiling point, higher viscosity, lower vapour pressure. Water's unusually high BP for its size is due to extensive hydrogen bonding. Ice is less dense than liquid water (H-bonds create open lattice structure).
Allotropes of carbon: Diamond (giant covalent, each C bonded to 4 others, extremely hard, non-conductor). Graphite (layers of sp² carbon with delocalised electrons, soft/lubricant, conducts electricity). Fullerenes/graphene (molecular/2D forms with unique properties).
HL: Condensation polymers: Formed by combining monomers with the loss of small molecules (usually H₂O). Examples: polyesters (from diol + dicarboxylic acid), polyamides/nylon (from diamine + dicarboxylic acid), proteins (amino acids).
Key Terms & Definitions
London dispersion forces (LDF): Temporary, induced dipole-dipole forces caused by fluctuations in electron distribution. Present in all molecules.
Dipole-dipole forces: Attractions between the positive end of one polar molecule and the negative end of another.
Hydrogen bond: A strong intermolecular force between a hydrogen atom bonded to N, O, or F and a lone pair on another N, O, or F atom.
Allotrope: Different structural forms of the same element in the same physical state (e.g., diamond, graphite, fullerene for carbon).
Giant covalent structure: A structure where all atoms are connected by covalent bonds in a continuous network (e.g., diamond, SiO₂).

Application[3 marks]L2

Explain why water (H₂O, Mr = 18) has a much higher boiling point than hydrogen sulfide (H₂S, Mr = 34).

H₂O: Forms hydrogen bonds between molecules (O is highly electronegative with lone pairs, H is small and δ+). These are strong intermolecular forces. [1]

Key Concepts
Periodic table organisation: Arranged by increasing atomic number. Groups (columns) = same number of valence electrons = similar chemical properties. Electronegativity: The ability of an atom in a covalent bond to attract the shared pair of electrons toward itself (Pauling scale).
Electron affinity: The enthalpy change when an electron is added to a gaseous atom.
Transition metal: A d-block element that forms at least one ion with a partially filled d-sublevel.
Oxidation state: The charge an atom would have if all bonds were ionic. Transition metals can have multiple oxidation states.

R1.1 — Measuring Enthalpy Changes
Exo/endothermic reactions. Energy profiles. Calorimetry. Bond enthalpies. HL Hess's law.
▸
Key Concepts
Enthalpy (H): The heat content of a system at constant pressure. We measure changes in enthalpy (ΔH), not absolute values. Exothermic: ΔH < 0 (releases heat, products lower energy). Endothermic: ΔH > 0 (absorbs heat, products higher energy).
Calorimetry: Measuring enthalpy changes experimentally using Q = mcΔT. Then ΔH = −Q/n (per mole of limiting reagent). Assumptions: no heat loss, specific heat capacity of solution = water (4.18 J/g/K), density = 1 g/cm³.
Bond enthalpies: Energy required to break one mole of a specific bond in the gaseous state (always endothermic). ΔH ≈ Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds formed). This gives only an estimate because bond enthalpies are averages.
Energy profiles: Diagrams showing energy on the y-axis and reaction progress on the x-axis. Show reactant/product energy levels, activation energy (Ea), and ΔH. The peak represents the transition state.
HL: Hess's law: The total enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the route taken. ΔH = Σ ΔH_f(products) − Σ ΔH_f(reactants), or ΔH = Σ ΔH_c(reactants) − Σ ΔH_c(products). Used to calculate ΔH for reactions that cannot be measured directly.
Key Terms & Definitions
Standard enthalpy change (ΔH°): Measured under standard conditions: 298 K, 100 kPa, 1 mol/dm³ solutions.
Enthalpy of formation (ΔH_f°): The enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states.
Enthalpy of combustion (ΔH_c°): The enthalpy change when one mole of a substance undergoes complete combustion under standard conditions.
Activation energy (Ea): The minimum energy required for a reaction to occur.
Bond enthalpy: The energy needed to break one mole of a bond in the gaseous state, averaged across different molecules.

ΔH = −Q / n
Enthalpy change per mole

Enthalpy from bond enthalpies
ΔH = Σ ΔH_f(products) − Σ ΔH_f(reactants)

R1.2 — Energy Cycles in Reactions HL
Born-Haber cycles. Lattice enthalpy. Enthalpy of hydration, solution, atomisation.
▸
Key Concepts
Born-Haber cycle: An enthalpy cycle for the formation of an ionic compound from its elements, via gaseous atoms and ions. Uses Hess's law to find lattice enthalpy indirectly. Steps: atomisation → ionisation of metal → atomisation of non-metal → electron affinity → lattice formation.
Lattice enthalpy: The energy released when one mole of an ionic compound is formed from its gaseous ions. High lattice enthalpy = strong ionic bonding. Increases with higher ion charges and smaller ion sizes (Coulomb's law).
Enthalpy of solution: ΔH_sol = −ΔH_lattice + ΔH_hydration(cation) + ΔH_hydration(anion). Whether dissolving is exo- or endothermic depends on the balance between breaking the lattice (endothermic) and hydrating the ions (exothermic).
Comparing lattice enthalpies: If experimental lattice enthalpy (from Born-Haber) >> theoretical (from ionic model), the compound has significant covalent character.

Enthalpy of atomisation: The enthalpy change when one mole of gaseous atoms is formed from an element in its standard state.
Enthalpy of hydration: The enthalpy change when one mole of gaseous ions is dissolved in water.
Enthalpy of solution: The enthalpy change when one mole of a solute dissolves in excess solvent.

R1.4 — Entropy & Spontaneity HL
Entropy (S). Predicting entropy changes. Gibbs free energy: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. Spontaneity.
▸
Key Concepts
Entropy (S): A measure of the dispersal of energy and matter (the number of possible microstates). Greater disorder = higher entropy. S(gas) >> S(liquid) > S(solid). Entropy increases with temperature, number of particles, and complexity of molecules.
Predicting ΔS: Increase in number of gas molecules → positive ΔS. Phase change from solid to liquid to gas → positive ΔS. Dissolving a solid in water → usually positive ΔS. Decrease in gas moles → negative ΔS.
Gibbs free energy: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. Determines spontaneity: ΔG < 0 → spontaneous. ΔG > 0 → non-spontaneous. ΔG = 0 → at equilibrium. A reaction can be spontaneous even if endothermic, provided TΔS is large enough (entropy-driven).
Temperature dependence: When ΔH and ΔS have the same sign, spontaneity depends on temperature. ΔH < 0 and ΔS > 0 → always spontaneous. ΔH > 0 and ΔS < 0 → never spontaneous. Mixed signs → spontaneous above or below a certain T = ΔH/ΔS.
Key Terms & Definitions
Entropy (S): A thermodynamic quantity representing the degree of dispersal of energy and matter. Unit: J/(mol·K).
Gibbs free energy (G): The thermodynamic potential that determines whether a process is spontaneous at constant T and P.
Spontaneous reaction: A reaction that occurs without continuous external input of energy (ΔG < 0). Does not mean "fast."

R2.2 — How Fast? The Rate of Chemical Change
Collision theory. Factors affecting rate. Maxwell-Boltzmann. HL Rate equations. Arrhenius. Mechanisms.
▸
Key Concepts
Collision theory: For a reaction to occur, particles must (1) collide, (2) with sufficient energy (≥ Ea), and (3) with correct orientation. Rate depends on the frequency of successful collisions.
Factors affecting rate: Temperature ↑ → faster particles, more exceed Ea. Concentration/pressure ↑ → more frequent collisions. Surface area ↑ → more exposed particles. Catalyst → provides alternative pathway with lower Ea.
Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution: A graph showing the distribution of particle energies at a given temperature. At higher T, the curve shifts right and flattens — a greater proportion of particles have energy ≥ Ea. A catalyst lowers Ea, so more particles can react at the same temperature.
HL: Rate equations: Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n. The order with respect to each reactant (m, n) is determined experimentally, not from the balanced equation. Rate constant k depends on temperature (Arrhenius equation). Overall order = m + n.
HL: Arrhenius equation: k = Ae^(−Ea/RT). Plotting ln k vs 1/T gives a straight line with slope = −Ea/R. This allows experimental determination of Ea.
HL: Rate-determining step: The slowest step in a multi-step mechanism controls the overall rate. The rate equation reflects the species involved in the RDS (and any preceding fast equilibria).
Key Terms & Definitions
Rate of reaction: The change in concentration of reactants or products per unit time. Unit: mol/(dm³·s).
Activation energy (Ea): The minimum energy required for a successful collision (reaction to occur).
Catalyst: A substance that increases the rate of reaction without being consumed, by providing an alternative pathway with lower Ea.
Rate constant (k): The proportionality constant in the rate equation. Depends on temperature and Ea.
Order of reaction: The power to which a reactant's concentration is raised in the rate equation (determined experimentally).
Rate-determining step (RDS): The slowest elementary step in a reaction mechanism.

State two factors that increase the rate of a chemical reaction and explain each using collision theory.

Temperature: Higher temperature increases the kinetic energy of particles. More particles exceed the activation energy (Ea), so the frequency of successful collisions increases. [1]
Concentration: Higher concentration means more particles per unit volume. This increases the frequency of collisions, and therefore the frequency of successful collisions. [1]
Common error: Saying "particles collide harder" at higher temperature. The key point is that MORE particles have energy ≥ Ea, not just that collisions are harder. Draw a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution to show this.
Analysis[4 marks]L3

Explain how a catalyst increases the rate of reaction. Include a reference to activation energy and an energy profile diagram description.

Mechanism: A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy (Ea). [1]
Effect: With lower Ea, a greater proportion of particles have sufficient energy to react (shown by the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution — more area under the curve beyond the new, lower Ea). [1]
Energy profile: The profile diagram shows the same reactant and product energy levels (ΔH unchanged) but a lower "hump" (Ea). The catalyst does not change the enthalpy change of the reaction. [1]
Not consumed: The catalyst participates in the reaction mechanism but is regenerated — it is not used up and does not appear in the overall equation. [1]
Common error: Saying a catalyst "gives particles more energy". It does not — it lowers the energy barrier. The particles' energies are unchanged; the threshold they need to exceed is lowered.
R2.3 — How Far? The Extent of Chemical Change
Dynamic equilibrium. Le Chatelier's principle. Kc. HL Kp. ΔG° and K relationship.
▸
Key Concepts
Dynamic equilibrium: In a closed system, when the forward and reverse reaction rates are equal. Macroscopic properties remain constant, but reactions continue at the molecular level. Reached from either direction.
Le Chatelier's principle: When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to partially counteract the change. Temperature ↑ → shifts in endothermic direction. Concentration ↑ of a substance → shifts away from it. Pressure ↑ → shifts to side with fewer gas moles. Catalysts do NOT shift equilibrium (speed up both directions equally).
Equilibrium constant (Kc): For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: Kc = [C]^c[D]^d / [A]^a[B]^b. Large K → products favoured. Small K → reactants favoured. Only affected by temperature, not concentration, pressure, or catalysts.
HL: Kp: For gaseous reactions, Kp uses partial pressures instead of concentrations. Kp = (p_C)^c(p_D)^d / (p_A)^a(p_B)^b. Partial pressure: p_A = (mole fraction of A) × total pressure.
HL: ΔG° and K: ΔG° = −RT ln K. If K > 1, ΔG° < 0 (products favoured thermodynamically). If K < 1, ΔG° > 0 (reactants favoured).
Key Terms & Definitions
Dynamic equilibrium: A state where the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal and concentrations remain constant.
Le Chatelier's principle: A system at equilibrium will respond to a disturbance by shifting to partially oppose the change.
Equilibrium constant (K): The ratio of product to reactant concentrations (or pressures) at equilibrium, each raised to their stoichiometric coefficients.
ICE table: A method (Initial, Change, Equilibrium) for organising equilibrium calculations.

Recall[2 marks]L1

State Le Chatelier's principle.

Principle: If a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change in concentration, temperature, or pressure, the system will shift to partially counteract the change and establish a new equilibrium. [1]
Key word: "Partially" — the system does not fully reverse the change, it only partially opposes it. The equilibrium constant Kc only changes with temperature, not with concentration or pressure changes. [1]
Common error: Saying the system "restores" the original equilibrium. It establishes a NEW equilibrium position — the original conditions are not fully restored.
Calculation[4 marks]L3

For the reaction N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g), at equilibrium: [N₂] = 0.50 mol dm⁻³, [H₂] = 0.30 mol dm⁻³, [NH₃] = 0.20 mol dm⁻³. Calculate Kc and state its units.

Expression: Kc = [NH₃]²/([N₂][H₂]³) [1]
Substitution: Kc = (0.20)²/(0.50 × (0.30)³) = 0.040/(0.50 × 0.027) = 0.040/0.0135 [1]
Answer: Kc = 2.96 ≈ 3.0 [1]
Units: (mol dm⁻³)²/((mol dm⁻³)(mol dm⁻³)³) = (mol dm⁻³)²/(mol dm⁻³)⁴ = (mol dm⁻³)⁻² = mol⁻² dm⁶ [1]

R3.1 — Proton Transfer Reactions (Acids & Bases)
Brønsted-Lowry. pH scale. Strong vs weak. HL Lewis acids/bases. Ka, Kb, Kw. Buffers. pH curves.
▸
Key Concepts
Brønsted-Lowry theory: An acid is a proton (H⁺) donor; a base is a proton acceptor. In every acid-base reaction, a proton is transferred. Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by one H⁺.
pH scale: pH = −log₁₀[H⁺]. pH 7 = neutral (at 25°C), pH < 7 = acidic, pH > 7 = basic. Each unit change in pH = 10× change in [H⁺].
Strong vs weak: Strong acids/bases fully dissociate in water (HCl, NaOH). Weak acids/bases partially dissociate — equilibrium lies toward undissociated form (CH₃COOH, NH₃). Same concentration, same pH? No — weak acid has higher pH than strong acid.
HL: Ka and Kb: Ka = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA] for weak acids. Larger Ka = stronger acid. pKa = −log Ka. Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C. Ka × Kb = Kw for conjugate pairs.
HL: Buffer solutions: Resist changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. Made from a weak acid + its conjugate base (or weak base + conjugate acid). Buffer action: the weak acid neutralises added OH⁻; the conjugate base neutralises added H⁺.
HL: pH curves (titration curves): Plot pH vs volume of titrant added. Shape depends on strong/weak combinations. The equivalence point is where stoichiometric amounts have reacted. Indicator choice: pKa of indicator ≈ pH at equivalence point.
Key Terms & Definitions
Conjugate acid-base pair: Two species that differ by one proton (H⁺).
Amphiprotic / amphoteric: A substance that can act as both an acid and a base (e.g., water, amino acids).
Ka (acid dissociation constant): The equilibrium constant for the dissociation of a weak acid.
Kw (ionic product of water): [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C.
Buffer solution: A solution that resists pH changes upon addition of small amounts of acid or base.
Equivalence point: The point in a titration where the acid and base have reacted in stoichiometric proportions.
Key Formulas
pH = −log[H⁺] and [H⁺] = 10^(−pH)
pH calculations
Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ (at 25°C)
Ionic product of water
Ka = [H⁺][A⁻] / [HA]
HL: Acid dissociation constant
pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
HL: Henderson-Hasselbalch equation (buffers)

Calculation[4 marks]L3

Calculate the pH of a 0.10 mol dm⁻³ solution of ethanoic acid (Ka = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³).

Equilibrium: CH₃COOH ⇌ CH₃COO⁻ + H⁺. Ka = [H⁺][CH₃COO⁻]/[CH₃COOH] [1]
Approximation: Let [H⁺] = x. Since Ka is small, assume [CH₃COOH] ≈ 0.10 (x is negligible). Ka = x²/0.10 [1]
Solve: x² = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵ × 0.10 = 1.8 × 10⁻⁶. x = [H⁺] = 1.34 × 10⁻³ mol dm⁻³ [1]
pH: pH = −log[H⁺] = −log(1.34 × 10⁻³) = 2.87 ≈ 2.9 [1]
Common error: Using pH = −log(0.10) as if it were a strong acid. Weak acids only partially dissociate — you must use Ka to find [H⁺]. This is the most common pH calculation error.
R3.2 — Electron Transfer Reactions (Redox)
Oxidation numbers. Half-equations. Electrochemical cells. HL E° values. Faraday's laws.
▸
Key Concepts
Redox: Oxidation = loss of electrons (increase in oxidation number). Reduction = gain of electrons (decrease in oxidation number). OIL RIG. They always occur together — one substance is oxidised while another is reduced.
Oxidation numbers: Rules: elements = 0, monatomic ions = charge, O usually −2, H usually +1. Sum in a compound = 0; in a polyatomic ion = ion charge. Used to track electron transfer.
Voltaic (galvanic) cell: Two different metals in solutions of their ions, connected by a wire and salt bridge. Spontaneous redox reaction generates electricity. Anode = oxidation (−), cathode = reduction (+). Salt bridge completes the circuit.
Electrolytic cell: Uses electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous reaction. Anode = oxidation (+), cathode = reduction (−). Applications: electroplating, extraction of reactive metals (e.g., aluminium), electrolysis of brine.
HL: Standard electrode potentials (E°): Measured relative to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE, E° = 0.00 V). More positive E° = stronger oxidising agent. Cell EMF: E°_cell = E°_cathode − E°_anode. Positive E°_cell → spontaneous reaction.
HL: Faraday's laws: The amount of substance deposited/dissolved at an electrode is proportional to the charge passed. Q = It, n = Q/(zF), where z = electrons transferred per ion, F = 96,485 C/mol.
Key Terms & Definitions
Oxidation: Loss of electrons / increase in oxidation number.
Reduction: Gain of electrons / decrease in oxidation number.
Oxidising agent: A substance that causes oxidation (itself gets reduced).
Reducing agent: A substance that causes reduction (itself gets oxidised).
Salt bridge: A connection between two half-cells allowing ion flow to maintain electrical neutrality.
Standard electrode potential (E°): The potential of a half-cell under standard conditions relative to the SHE.
Faraday constant (F): 96,485 C/mol — the charge carried by one mole of electrons.
Key Formulas
E°_cell = E°_cathode − E°_anode
HL: Cell EMF from standard electrode potentials
Q = I × t
HL: Charge from current and time
n = Q / (z × F)
HL: Moles deposited in electrolysis
Exam Focus

Write and balance half-equations, then combine them into a full redox equation.
HL: Use E° values to predict spontaneity and calculate cell EMF. Perform Faraday's law calculations for electrolysis.

Calculation[4 marks]L2

Calculate the EMF of a galvanic cell made from Zn²⁺/Zn (E° = −0.76 V) and Cu²⁺/Cu (E° = +0.34 V). Write the overall cell equation and identify the anode and cathode.

EMF: E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode = +0.34 − (−0.76) = +1.10 V [1]
Anode: Zn (more negative E°, oxidised): Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ [1]

R3.3 — Electron Sharing Reactions (Radicals) HL
Radicals. Homolytic fission. Free radical substitution: initiation, propagation, termination.
▸
Key Concepts
Homolytic fission: A covalent bond breaks so that each atom gets one electron from the shared pair, forming two radicals (species with unpaired electrons). Denoted by a half-arrow (fishhook). Requires energy (UV light or heat).
Free radical substitution (e.g., CH₄ + Cl₂): Three stages: (1) Initiation: UV light causes homolytic fission of Cl₂ → 2Cl•. (2) Propagation: Cl• + CH₄ → CH₃• + HCl, then CH₃• + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + Cl• (chain reaction — radical regenerated). (3) Termination: two radicals combine (e.g., Cl• + Cl• → Cl₂).
Limitations: Free radical substitution produces a mixture of products (mono-, di-, tri-substituted) because substitution can continue. This makes it non-selective — low yield of a specific product.
Key Terms & Definitions
Radical (free radical): A species with one or more unpaired electrons. Highly reactive.
Homolytic fission: Breaking a covalent bond so each atom receives one electron, forming two radicals.
Initiation: The step that produces the first radicals (usually by UV light).
Propagation: Steps where a radical reacts with a molecule to form a product and regenerate a radical (chain continues).
Termination: Steps where two radicals combine, ending the chain.
Exam Focus
Write the three stages (initiation, propagation, termination) using correct curly arrow notation (half-arrows for radicals).

Recall[2 marks]L1

Define a free radical and explain why radical reactions are important in atmospheric chemistry.

Free radical: A species with one or more unpaired electrons. Highly reactive. Formed by homolytic fission of a covalent bond (each atom gets one electron). Denoted with a dot: Cl•. [1]
Atmospheric importance: Chlorine radicals from CFCs catalyse ozone destruction: Cl• + O₃ → ClO• + O₂, then ClO• + O → Cl• + O₂. One Cl• can destroy thousands of O₃ molecules because it is regenerated. [1]
Common error: Confusing homolytic fission (→ radicals) with heterolytic fission (→ ions). In homolytic fission, the bond breaks evenly; in heterolytic, one atom takes both electrons.
Analysis[3 marks]L2

Outline the three stages of a free radical substitution mechanism for the reaction of methane with chlorine (CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl).

Initiation: Cl₂ → 2Cl• (UV light causes homolytic fission of Cl−Cl bond, producing two chlorine radicals). [1]
Propagation: Cl• + CH₄ → CH₃• + HCl, then CH₃• + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + Cl•. The Cl• radical is regenerated — this is a chain reaction. [1]
Termination: Two radicals combine: Cl• + Cl• → Cl₂, or CH₃• + Cl• → CH₃Cl, or CH₃• + CH₃• → C₂H₆. Chain ends when radicals are consumed. [1]
Common error: Only writing one propagation step. IB requires both propagation steps to show the chain mechanism. Also: termination produces minor by-products like ethane (C₂H₆).
R3.4 — Electron-Pair Sharing Reactions HL
Nucleophiles, electrophiles. SN1, SN2 mechanisms. Electrophilic addition. Aromatic substitution. Condensation.
▸
Key Concepts
Nucleophiles and electrophiles: A nucleophile is an electron-pair donor (lone pair or π-electrons) — attacks positive/electron-deficient centres. An electrophile is an electron-pair acceptor — attacks negative/electron-rich centres.
SN2 mechanism: One-step process: nucleophile attacks the carbon simultaneously as the leaving group departs. Rate = k[substrate][nucleophile]. Favoured by primary substrates (less steric hindrance). Inversion of configuration at the carbon.
SN1 mechanism: Two-step process: (1) leaving group departs forming a carbocation, (2) nucleophile attacks the carbocation. Rate = k[substrate]. Favoured by tertiary substrates (stable carbocation). Racemisation (mixture of enantiomers).
Electrophilic addition: Alkenes react with electrophiles (HBr, Br₂, H₂O/H⁺). The π-bond attacks the electrophile, forming a carbocation intermediate, then a nucleophile attacks. Markovnikov's rule: H adds to the carbon with more H's already (more stable carbocation intermediate).
Electrophilic aromatic substitution: Benzene's delocalised π-system is attacked by an electrophile. A hydrogen is substituted (not added) to preserve aromaticity. Examples: nitration (HNO₃/H₂SO₄), halogenation (Br₂/FeBr₃ catalyst).
Condensation reactions: Two molecules join with the loss of a small molecule (usually H₂O). Examples: ester formation (alcohol + carboxylic acid → ester + H₂O), amide formation (amine + carboxylic acid → amide + H₂O).
Key Terms & Definitions
Nucleophile: A species that donates an electron pair to form a new covalent bond (e.g., OH⁻, NH₃, CN⁻, H₂O).
Electrophile: A species that accepts an electron pair to form a new covalent bond (e.g., H⁺, NO₂⁺, Br⁺).
Leaving group: An atom or group that departs with the bonding electrons during a substitution reaction.
Carbocation: A positively charged carbon intermediate. Stability: tertiary > secondary > primary.
Heterolytic fission: Breaking a bond so that both electrons go to one atom, forming a cation and an anion.
Markovnikov's rule: In HX addition to an alkene, H adds to the carbon with more hydrogens (forming the more stable carbocation).
Exam Focus
Draw full curly-arrow mechanisms for SN1, SN2, electrophilic addition, and electrophilic aromatic substitution.
Predict whether SN1 or SN2 is more likely based on the substrate (primary vs tertiary).
Apply Markovnikov's rule to predict the major product of addition to unsymmetrical alkenes.

Application[4 marks]L3

Describe the SN2 mechanism for the reaction of bromoethane (CH₃CH₂Br) with hydroxide ions (OH⁻). Include the role of the nucleophile, the transition state, and the stereochemical outcome.

Nucleophilic attack: OH⁻ attacks the δ+ carbon (bonded to Br) from the opposite side to the leaving group. This is a backside attack. [1]
Transition state: A single step — bond formation (C−OH) and bond breaking (C−Br) occur simultaneously. The transition state has 5 groups around carbon (trigonal bipyramidal). [1]
Products: CH₃CH₂OH + Br⁻. The Br⁻ is the leaving group. [1]
Stereochemistry: Backside attack causes inversion of configuration (Walden inversion). If the substrate is chiral, the product has opposite configuration. Rate = k[substrate][nucleophile] (second order). [1]
Common error: Drawing the nucleophile attacking from the same side as the leaving group. SN2 always involves backside attack. Also: SN2 is one step (concerted), not two steps.

Experimental Techniques SKILL
Titrations, calorimetry, distillation, chromatography, gravimetric analysis, spectrophotometry.
▸
Key Concepts
Titration: A volumetric technique to determine concentration. Add titrant from a burette to analyte in a flask until the equivalence point (detected by indicator colour change or pH meter). Back-titration used when the analyte is insoluble or slow-reacting.
Calorimetry: Measuring enthalpy changes using Q = mcΔT. Key: insulate to minimise heat loss. Use a graph (temperature vs time) to extrapolate the maximum temperature if heat loss occurs.
Chromatography: Separating mixtures based on different affinities for stationary and mobile phases. Rf = distance moved by spot / distance moved by solvent front. Different types: paper, TLC, column, gas chromatography.
Spectrophotometry / colorimetry: Measuring absorption of light by coloured solutions. Beer-Lambert law: A = εcl. Calibration curve method: measure absorbance of known concentrations, then read unknown concentration from the curve.

Data Processing & Analysis SKILL
Uncertainty, error propagation, graphical analysis, significant figures.
▸
Key Concepts
Uncertainty: Every measurement has uncertainty. Analogue instruments: ± half the smallest division. Digital instruments: ± the smallest displayed digit. Percentage uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty / measured value) × 100%.
Error propagation: Addition/subtraction: add absolute uncertainties. Multiplication/division: add percentage uncertainties. Powers: multiply percentage uncertainty by the power.
Graphical analysis: Use error bars to show uncertainty. Best-fit line should pass through error bars. Max/min gradient lines give uncertainty in gradient. Linearisation of data (e.g., plotting 1/[A] vs t for second-order) allows determination of rate constants and other parameters.
