Sequences and Series
Arithmetic and geometric sequences. Sigma notation. Convergence and sum to infinity. Series formulas.
▸

Key exam patterns: sequences/series appear in Paper 1 and Paper 2 every session. Practice sum formulas, convergence tests, and sigma notation.

Key Concepts
Arithmetic sequences: Sequences where consecutive terms differ by a constant d. General term: uₙ = u₁ + (n−1)d. Sum: Sₙ = n/2(2u₁ + (n−1)d).
Geometric sequences: Sequences where consecutive terms have constant ratio r. General term: uₙ = u₁ · r^(n−1). Sum: Sₙ = u₁(1−rⁿ)/(1−r).
Convergence: A geometric series converges when |r| < 1. Sum to infinity: S∞ = u₁/(1−r).
Sigma notation: Σ notation used to represent series concisely. Σ(n=1 to N) uₙ represents the sum of terms from n=1 to N.
Key Terms & Definitions
Sequence: An ordered list of numbers following a pattern or rule.
Series: The sum of the terms of a sequence.
Common difference (d): The constant difference between consecutive terms in an arithmetic sequence.
Common ratio (r): The constant factor between consecutive terms in a geometric sequence.
Convergent: A series that approaches a finite limit as n → ∞.
Divergent: A series that does not approach a finite limit.
Key Formulas
uₙ = u₁ + (n−1)d
Arithmetic sequence general term
Sₙ = n/2(u₁ + uₙ) = n/2(2u₁ + (n−1)d)
Arithmetic series sum
uₙ = u₁ · r^(n−1)
Geometric sequence general term
Sₙ = u₁(1−rⁿ)/(1−r) = u₁(rⁿ−1)/(r−1)
Geometric series sum (r ≠ 1)
S∞ = u₁/(1−r), |r| < 1
Sum to infinity (geometric)
Σ(k=1 to n) k = n(n+1)/2
Sum of first n natural numbers
Exam Focus
Paper 1: Finding uₙ or Sₙ given initial terms. Determining convergence of geometric series. Using sigma notation to evaluate sums.
Paper 2: Modeling real-world situations with arithmetic/geometric sequences. Proving properties using induction or algebraic methods.
Paper 3: Analysis of infinite series. Finding which terms satisfy given conditions. Proof-based problems on series properties.

Recall[2 marks]L1

State the formula for the sum of the first n terms of a geometric series and the condition for convergence of an infinite geometric series.

Sum: Sₙ = a(1 − rⁿ)/(1 − r) for r ≠ 1, where a = first term, r = common ratio. [1]
Convergence: An infinite geometric series converges when |r| < 1. The sum to infinity is S∞ = a/(1 − r). [1]
Common error: Using the arithmetic series formula Sₙ = n/2(2a + (n−1)d) for a geometric series. Always identify whether the sequence is arithmetic (constant difference) or geometric (constant ratio) first.
Calculation[4 marks]L2

The 3rd term of a geometric sequence is 12 and the 6th term is 96. Find the first term and the common ratio. Hence find the sum of the first 10 terms.

Setup: u₃ = ar² = 12, u₆ = ar⁵ = 96. Dividing: ar⁵/ar² = r³ = 96/12 = 8 → r = 2. [1]
First term: ar² = 12 → a(4) = 12 → a = 3. [1]
S₁₀: S₁₀ = 3(1 − 2¹⁰)/(1 − 2) = 3(1 − 1024)/(−1) = 3 × 1023 = 3069. [2]
Common error: Using u₃ = ar³ instead of u₃ = ar². The nth term is uₙ = arⁿ⁻¹, not arⁿ. This off-by-one error is extremely common.
Exponents and Logarithms
Laws of exponents. Logarithmic functions and properties. Change of base. Natural logarithm. Solving exponential and logarithmic equations.
▸
Key Concepts
Exponent laws: aᵐ · aⁿ = a^(m+n); aᵐ/aⁿ = a^(m−n); (aᵐ)ⁿ = a^(mn); a⁰ = 1; a^(−n) = 1/aⁿ; a^(1/n) = ⁿ√a.
Logarithm definition: logₐ(x) = y iff aʸ = x. Logarithm is the inverse of exponential. Domain: x > 0.
Natural logarithm: ln(x) = logₑ(x). Inverse of eˣ. Crucial in calculus and modeling.
Change of base: logₐ(x) = ln(x)/ln(a) = log(x)/log(a). Allows converting between different bases for calculation.
Key Terms & Definitions
Exponent: The power to which a base is raised. In aⁿ, n is the exponent.
Base: The number being raised to a power. In aⁿ, a is the base.
Logarithm: The exponent to which a base must be raised to produce a given number.
Natural log (ln): Logarithm with base e (≈2.718), denoted ln(x).
Common log (log): Logarithm with base 10, denoted log(x).

logₐ(xⁿ) = n·logₐ(x)

Recall[2 marks]L1

State the three laws of logarithms.

Laws: log(ab) = log a + log b; log(a/b) = log a − log b; log(aⁿ) = n log a. [1]
Also: Change of base: logₐb = ln b/ln a = log b/log a. And: logₐa = 1, logₐ1 = 0. [1]

Solve: 3²ˣ⁺¹ = 5ˣ. Give your answer in the form x = ln a/ln b.

Take ln: (2x + 1)ln 3 = x ln 5. [1]
Expand: 2x ln 3 + ln 3 = x ln 5 → x(2 ln 3 − ln 5) = −ln 3. [1]

Binomial Theorem HL
Binomial expansion. Pascal's triangle. General term. Fractional and negative exponents for HL.
▸
Key Concepts
Binomial theorem: (a+b)ⁿ = Σ C(n,r) aⁿ⁻ʳ bʳ where C(n,r) = n!/(r!(n−r)!). Works for non-negative integer n.
General term: Tᵣ₊₁ = C(n,r) aⁿ⁻ʳ bʳ (the (r+1)th term in expansion). Useful for finding specific terms without full expansion.
HL: Fractional/negative exponents: Binomial theorem extended to (a+b)ᵏ where k is any real number. For |x| < 1: (1+x)ᵏ = 1 + kx + k(k−1)x²/2! + ... (infinite series).
Pascal's triangle: Triangular array of binomial coefficients. Row n contains C(n,0), C(n,1), ..., C(n,n). Each entry is sum of two entries above it.
Key Terms & Definitions
Binomial coefficient C(n,r): The number of ways to choose r items from n items. Equals n!/(r!(n−r)!).
Factorial (n!): Product of all positive integers up to n. 0! = 1 by definition.
Expansion: The full polynomial form of (a+b)ⁿ after applying the binomial theorem.
Key Formulas
(a+b)ⁿ = Σ(r=0 to n) C(n,r) aⁿ⁻ʳ bʳ
Binomial theorem (integer n ≥ 0)
Tᵣ₊₁ = C(n,r) aⁿ⁻ʳ bʳ
General term (position r+1)
C(n,r) = n!/(r!(n−r)!)
Binomial coefficient
(1+x)ᵏ = 1 + kx + k(k−1)x²/2! + k(k−1)(k−2)x³/3! + ..., |x| < 1
Binomial series for any real k [HL]
Exam Focus
Paper 1: Finding specific coefficients in (a+b)ⁿ. Identifying middle term or term containing xᵏ.
Paper 2: Proving binomial identities. Using binomial expansion in algebraic proofs.
Paper 3 [HL]: Binomial series for fractional/negative exponents. Convergence analysis. Using series for approximations.

Find the coefficient of x³ in the expansion of (2 + 3x)⁷.

The constant term in the expansion of (x² + k/x)⁶ is 240. Find the value of k.

General term: C(6,r)(x²)⁶⁻ʳ(k/x)ʳ = C(6,r)kʳ × x¹²⁻²ʳ⁻ʳ = C(6,r)kʳ × x¹²⁻³ʳ. [1]

Proof by Induction HL
Principle of mathematical induction. Base case, inductive step. Divisibility, series, inequalities proofs.
▸
Key Concepts
Principle of mathematical induction: To prove P(n) true for all n ≥ n₀: (1) Prove P(n₀) is true (base case); (2) Assume P(k) true for some k ≥ n₀ (inductive hypothesis); (3) Prove P(k+1) is true (inductive step). Then P(n) is true for all n ≥ n₀.
Divisibility proofs: Show that some expression is divisible by a number for all n. Example: Prove that 3ⁿ − 1 is divisible by 2 for all n ≥ 1.
Series sum proofs: Prove formulas like Σ k = n(n+1)/2 or Σ k² = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6. Use inductive hypothesis Σ(k=1 to m) = formula(m), then add (m+1)th term.
Inequality proofs: Prove inequalities like 2ⁿ > n for n ≥ 1. Show P(1) true, then assume 2ᵏ > k and prove 2^(k+1) > k+1.
Key Terms & Definitions
Base case: The initial step where you verify the statement is true for the first value (usually n=1).
Inductive hypothesis: The assumption that the statement is true for n=k, used to prove it for n=k+1.
Inductive step: The logical argument showing that if P(k) is true, then P(k+1) must also be true.
Key Formulas
Σ(k=1 to n) k = n(n+1)/2
Sum of first n natural numbers
Σ(k=1 to n) k² = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6
Sum of first n squares
Σ(k=1 to n) k³ = [n(n+1)/2]²
Sum of first n cubes
Σ(k=1 to n) r^(k−1) = (1−rⁿ)/(1−r), r ≠ 1
Geometric series sum (induction proof)
Exam Focus
Paper 1: Complete proofs by induction for divisibility, series sums, or simple inequalities given statement.
Paper 2: Construct inductive proofs. Identifying which type of induction problem (divisibility, sum, inequality).
Paper 3: Proving complex identities and inequalities. Derangement, permutation problems via induction.

Analysis[5 marks]L3

Prove by mathematical induction that Σᵣ₌₁ⁿ r² = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6 for all positive integers n.

Base case (n=1): LHS = 1² = 1. RHS = 1(2)(3)/6 = 1. LHS = RHS ✓ [1]
Assume true for n=k: Σᵣ₌₁ᵏ r² = k(k+1)(2k+1)/6. [1]
Prove for n=k+1: Σᵣ₌₁ᵏ⁺¹ r² = k(k+1)(2k+1)/6 + (k+1)² = (k+1)[k(2k+1)/6 + (k+1)] = (k+1)[k(2k+1) + 6(k+1)]/6 = (k+1)(2k² + 7k + 6)/6. [1]
Factor: 2k² + 7k + 6 = (k+2)(2k+3) = (k+1+1)(2(k+1)+1). So the expression = (k+1)(k+2)(2k+3)/6, which is the formula with n = k+1. [1]
Conclusion: Since the statement is true for n=1, and if true for n=k then true for n=k+1, by the principle of mathematical induction it is true for all n ∈ ℤ⁺. [1]
Common error: Omitting the conclusion statement. IB requires you to explicitly state the principle of induction in the final step. Without it, the proof is incomplete and loses the final mark.
Complex Numbers HL
Arithmetic operations. Argand diagram. Modulus and argument. Polar form. Euler form. De Moivre's theorem. Roots of polynomials.
▸
Key Concepts
Complex numbers: z = a + bi where i² = −1. a is real part (Re(z)), b is imaginary part (Im(z)). Arithmetic: add/subtract/multiply like binomials; division uses conjugate.
Argand diagram: Complex number z = a + bi plotted as point (a, b) in 2D plane. Real axis (horizontal), imaginary axis (vertical).
Modulus and argument: |z| = √(a² + b²) is distance from origin. arg(z) = θ is angle from positive real axis. Usually −π < θ ≤ π.
Polar and Euler forms: z = r(cosθ + i·sinθ) = r·e^(iθ) where r = |z|, θ = arg(z). Useful for multiplication, division, powers.
De Moivre's theorem: (cosθ + i·sinθ)ⁿ = cos(nθ) + i·sin(nθ) or (r·e^(iθ))ⁿ = rⁿ·e^(inθ). Simplifies powers and roots of complex numbers.
Roots of polynomials: Complex roots of polynomials with real coefficients appear in conjugate pairs. HL includes finding nth roots of complex numbers.
Key Terms & Definitions
Imaginary unit (i): Defined by i² = −1. Allows extension of real numbers to complex numbers.
Complex conjugate: For z = a + bi, conjugate is z* = a − bi. Properties: z·z* = |z|², (z+w)* = z*+w*.
Modulus: |z| = √(a² + b²), the magnitude or distance of z from origin.
Argument: arg(z) = θ, the angle from positive real axis to z, measured counterclockwise.
Polar form: z = r(cosθ + i·sinθ) where r = |z|, θ = arg(z).
Euler form: z = r·e^(iθ), compact notation for polar form.
Key Formulas

Calculation[4 marks]L3

Let z = 2 + 2i. Express z in polar form. Hence find z⁵ using De Moivre's theorem.

Modulus: |z| = √(2² + 2²) = √8 = 2√2. [1]
Argument: arg(z) = arctan(2/2) = π/4 (first quadrant). So z = 2√2 cis(π/4). [1]
De Moivre: z⁵ = (2√2)⁵ cis(5π/4) = (2√2)⁵ cis(5π/4). (2√2)⁵ = 2⁵ × (√2)⁵ = 32 × 4√2 = 128√2. [1]
Rectangular: z⁵ = 128√2(cos(5π/4) + i sin(5π/4)) = 128√2(−√2/2 − i√2/2) = −128 − 128i. [1]
Common error: Forgetting to raise the modulus to the power as well as multiplying the argument. De Moivre: (r cis θ)ⁿ = rⁿ cis(nθ). Both r and θ change.
Analysis[3 marks]L3

Find all cube roots of −8 in the form a + bi.

Polar form: −8 = 8 cis(π). Cube roots: z = 8^(1/3) cis((π + 2kπ)/3) for k = 0, 1, 2. [1]
k=0: 2 cis(π/3) = 2(cos 60° + i sin 60°) = 1 + i√3. k=1: 2 cis(π) = −2. k=2: 2 cis(5π/3) = 1 − i√3. [1]
Verify: (−2)³ = −8 ✓. The three roots are equally spaced at 120° intervals on a circle of radius 2 in the Argand diagram. [1]
Common error: Only finding the real root (−2) and missing the two complex roots. An nth degree equation always has exactly n roots (counting multiplicity) in ℂ.
Systems of Linear Equations
2×2 and 3×3 systems. Matrix methods [HL]. Row reduction. Consistency and uniqueness of solutions.
▸
Key Concepts
Linear systems: Set of linear equations in multiple variables. Example: 2x + 3y = 5, x − y = 1. Solution is values of variables satisfying all equations simultaneously.
2×2 systems: Two equations in two unknowns. Can be solved by substitution, elimination, or graphical method. Each equation represents a line; solution is intersection point (if exists).
3×3 systems: Three equations in three unknowns. Each equation represents a plane. Solution is intersection of three planes (if unique).
HL: Matrix methods: Write system as Ax = b where A is coefficient matrix, x is variable vector, b is constants. Solve via Gaussian elimination, row reduction, or matrix inversion (x = A⁻¹b if A invertible).
Consistency: System is consistent if at least one solution exists. Inconsistent if no solution. Underdetermined if infinitely many solutions (dependent equations).
Key Terms & Definitions
Linear equation: Equation of form a₁x₁ + a₂x₂ + ... + aₙxₙ = b. Variables appear to first power only.
Solution set: All values of variables satisfying every equation in the system.
Consistent system: Has at least one solution.
Inconsistent system: Has no solution.
Coefficient matrix: Matrix containing coefficients of variables. Denoted A in Ax = b.
Key Formulas
ax + by = c; dx + ey = f
2×2 linear system
ax + by + cz = d; ex + fy + gz = h; ix + jy + kz = l

Calculation[4 marks]L2

Solve the system: x + 2y − z = 3, 2x − y + 3z = 1, 3x + y + 2z = 4.

Eliminate x: (2)−2×(1): −5y + 5z = −5 → y − z = 1. (3)−3×(1): −5y + 5z = −5 → y − z = 1. [1]
Observation: Both eliminations give the same equation y − z = 1. This means the system has infinitely many solutions (the three planes intersect in a line). [1]
Parametric: Let z = t. Then y = 1 + t. From (1): x = 3 − 2(1+t) + t = 1 − t. [1]
Solution: (x, y, z) = (1 − t, 1 + t, t) for t ∈ ℝ. This is a line in 3D space. [1]
Common error: Concluding "no solution" when two equations reduce to the same thing. This indicates infinitely many solutions (dependent system), not inconsistency. Express the answer parametrically.

Function Concepts and Properties
Domain, range, inverse functions. Composition. Transformations of graphs: translations, reflections, stretches.
▸
Key Concepts
Function definition: A relation where each input has exactly one output. f: X → Y means function f maps set X (domain) to set Y. Notation: f(x) = expression.
Domain and range: Domain is set of all possible input values. Range is set of all possible output values. Domain may be restricted (e.g., cannot divide by zero, cannot take square root of negative in ℝ).
Inverse functions: f⁻¹ reverses the action of f. If f(a) = b, then f⁻¹(b) = a. For inverse to exist, f must be one-to-one (injective). Domain of f⁻¹ = range of f.
Function composition: (f ∘ g)(x) = f(g(x)). Apply g first, then apply f to the result. Order matters: f ∘ g ≠ g ∘ f in general.
Graph transformations: f(x) + k shifts up k units; f(x − k) shifts right k units; −f(x) reflects over x-axis; f(−x) reflects over y-axis; a·f(x) stretches vertically by factor a.
Key Terms & Definitions
One-to-one (injective): Each output corresponds to exactly one input. f(a) = f(b) implies a = b.
Onto (surjective): Every element of codomain is an output of f.
Bijection: Function that is both one-to-one and onto. Bijections have inverses.
Composition: (f ∘ g)(x) = f(g(x)). Combining functions by applying one to the output of another.
Translation: Shift of graph horizontally or vertically.
Stretch/compression: Vertical or horizontal scaling of graph.
Key Formulas
f: X → Y; domain is X, range ⊆ Y
Function notation and domain/range
(f ∘ g)(x) = f(g(x))
Function composition
If f(a) = b, then f⁻¹(b) = a
Inverse function relationship
y = f(x) + k shifts up k units; y = f(x−k) shifts right k units
Vertical and horizontal translations
y = a·f(x) stretches vertically by |a|; y = f(bx) compresses horizontally by |b|
Vertical and horizontal stretches
Exam Focus
Paper 1: Finding domains/ranges. Determining if function is one-to-one. Sketching inverse functions and compositions.
Paper 2: Applying transformations to graphs. Finding equations of transformed functions. Proving properties of compositions.
Paper 3: Complex compositions and inverses. Analyzing behavior of composed functions near discontinuities.

Recall[2 marks]L1

Define the domain and range of a function. State the condition for a function to have an inverse.

Domain: The set of all possible input values (x-values). Range: the set of all possible output values (y-values). [1]
Inverse: A function has an inverse if and only if it is one-to-one (injective) — each output corresponds to exactly one input. Graphically: passes the horizontal line test. [1]

Given f(x) = 2x + 3 and g(x) = x² − 1, find (f∘g)(x) and (g∘f)(x). State the value of x for which (f∘g)(x) = (g∘f)(x).

(f∘g)(x): f(g(x)) = 2(x² − 1) + 3 = 2x² + 1. [1]
(g∘f)(x): g(f(x)) = (2x + 3)² − 1 = 4x² + 12x + 9 − 1 = 4x² + 12x + 8. [1]

Quadratic Functions
Vertex form. Discriminant. Completing the square. Zeros and graphing. Applications.
▸
Key Concepts
Quadratic function: f(x) = ax² + bx + c where a ≠ 0. Parabola: opens upward if a > 0, downward if a < 0. Vertex at x = −b/(2a).
Vertex form: f(x) = a(x − h)² + k. Vertex is (h, k). h and k are easily read from vertex form. Easier to graph than standard form.
Completing the square: Rewrite f(x) = ax² + bx + c as vertex form by completing the square. Example: x² + 4x = (x+2)² − 4.
Discriminant: Δ = b² − 4ac determines nature of roots. If Δ > 0, two distinct real roots. If Δ = 0, one repeated root (vertex on x-axis). If Δ < 0, no real roots.
Zeros and roots: Zeros of f(x) = ax² + bx + c are solutions to ax² + bx + c = 0. Found by factoring, completing square, or quadratic formula.
Key Terms & Definitions
Vertex: The turning point of a parabola. For upward-opening, it is the minimum point.
Axis of symmetry: Vertical line through the vertex. For f(x) = ax²+bx+c, it is x = −b/(2a).
Parabola: U-shaped or ∩-shaped curve formed by a quadratic function.
Root (or zero): Value of x where f(x) = 0. Point where parabola crosses x-axis.
Key Formulas

General quadratic function

x = −b/(2a)

Quadratic formula (roots)

Polynomial Functions
Factor and remainder theorems. Zeros and multiplicities. Graphing. End behavior.
▸
Key Concepts
Polynomial function: f(x) = aₙxⁿ + aₙ₋₁xⁿ⁻¹ + ... + a₁x + a₀. Degree n is highest power. Leading coefficient aₙ determines end behavior.
Factor theorem: (x − a) is a factor of f(x) iff f(a) = 0. If (x−a)^m is a factor but (x−a)^(m+1) is not, then x = a is a zero of multiplicity m.

End behavior: For large |x|, polynomial behaves like leading term aₙxⁿ. If n even and aₙ > 0, both ends go up. If n odd and aₙ > 0, left end down, right end up.

Degree: The highest exponent of x in the polynomial.
Leading coefficient: The coefficient of the term with highest degree.

▸
Key Concepts
Vertical translation: y = f(x) + k shifts graph up (k > 0) or down (k < 0) by |k| units. Affects y-intercept and all y-coordinates.
Horizontal translation: y = f(x − h) shifts graph right (h > 0) or left (h < 0) by |h| units. Affects x-intercepts and domain shift.
Vertical reflection: y = −f(x) reflects over x-axis. Points (a,b) → (a,−b). Flips graph vertically.

Combined transformations: Apply transformations in correct order. Generally: horizontal transformations first (stretches/reflections), then translations, then vertical transformations.

Reflection: Flip of graph across a line (usually x-axis or y-axis).

Limits and Continuity [HL]
Limit definition and evaluation. L'Hôpital's rule [HL]. Squeeze theorem. Continuity. Differentiability.
▸
Key Concepts
Limit definition: lim(x→a) f(x) = L means f(x) gets arbitrarily close to L as x approaches a (from either side). Written: For all ε > 0, there exists δ > 0 such that |x − a| < δ implies |f(x) − L| < ε.
Limit evaluation: Direct substitution if f continuous at a. Otherwise factor, rationalize, or use algebraic tricks. One-sided limits: x→a⁻ (from left), x→a⁺ (from right). Limit exists iff left and right limits equal.
HL: L'Hôpital's rule: If lim f(x)/g(x) is indeterminate (0/0 or ∞/∞), then lim f(x)/g(x) = lim f'(x)/g'(x) (if limit on right exists). Differentiate numerator and denominator separately.
HL: Squeeze theorem: If h(x) ≤ f(x) ≤ g(x) near a, and lim h(x) = lim g(x) = L, then lim f(x) = L. Useful for oscillating functions.
Continuity: f continuous at a if lim(x→a) f(x) = f(a). Function continuous on interval if continuous at every point. Polynomials, exponentials, logarithms continuous on their domains.
Differentiability: f differentiable at a if f'(a) = lim(h→0) [f(a+h) − f(a)]/h exists. If f differentiable at a, then f continuous at a (converse false).

Integration by parts: ∫u·dv = uv − ∫v·du. Choose u and dv strategically. LIATE order: Logarithmic, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exponential (for choosing u).
Partial fractions [HL]: Decompose rational function into simpler fractions. Example: (x+5)/(x²−1) = A/(x−1) + B/(x+1). Solve for A, B, then integrate term by term.
Integration by recognition: Recognize integrand as derivative of known function. Example: ∫eˣsinxdx requires clever manipulation or repeated integration by parts.

Evaluate ∫x² eˣ dx using integration by parts.

First IBP: u = x², dv = eˣ dx → du = 2x dx, v = eˣ. ∫x²eˣ dx = x²eˣ − ∫2xeˣ dx. [1]
Second IBP: u = 2x, dv = eˣ dx → du = 2 dx, v = eˣ. ∫2xeˣ dx = 2xeˣ − ∫2eˣ dx = 2xeˣ − 2eˣ. [1]
Combine: ∫x²eˣ dx = x²eˣ − 2xeˣ + 2eˣ + C = eˣ(x² − 2x + 2) + C. [1]
Check: d/dx[eˣ(x²−2x+2)] = eˣ(x²−2x+2) + eˣ(2x−2) = eˣ(x²) = x²eˣ ✓ [1]
Common error: Choosing u and dv incorrectly. For ∫(polynomial)(eˣ) dx, always let u = polynomial (it reduces with each differentiation) and dv = eˣ dx.

Find the area enclosed between y = x² and y = 2x for x ≥ 0.

Intersections: x² = 2x → x² − 2x = 0 → x(x−2) = 0 → x = 0 and x = 2. [1]
Which is on top: At x = 1: y = 2(1) = 2 > 1 = 1². So y = 2x is above y = x² on [0, 2]. [1]
Area: ∫₀² (2x − x²) dx = [x² − x³/3]₀² = 4 − 8/3 = 4/3. [1]
Answer: Area = 4/3 square units ≈ 1.33. [1]

Differential Equations [HL]
Separable ODEs. Homogeneous equations. Slope fields. Euler's method. Modeling with differential equations.
▸
Key Concepts
Differential equation: Equation involving derivatives. ODE (ordinary differential equation) has one independent variable. General solution contains constants. Particular solution satisfies initial condition.
Separable ODEs: Rewrite as f(y)dy = g(x)dx. Integrate both sides: ∫f(y)dy = ∫g(x)dx + C. Example: dy/dx = y/x separates to (1/y)dy = (1/x)dx.
First-order linear ODEs: Form dy/dx + P(x)y = Q(x). Solution method: integrating factor μ(x) = e^∫P(x)dx. Multiply both sides by μ(x), integrate.
Homogeneous equations: Form dy/dx = F(y/x). Substitute v = y/x, so y = vx, dy/dx = v + x(dv/dx). Converts to separable equation in v and x.
Slope fields (direction fields): Graphical representation of ODE. At each point (x,y), draw small line segment with slope = f(x,y) [from dy/dx = f(x,y)]. Solution curves follow these directions.
Euler's method: Numerical approximation. Start at (x₀, y₀). Iterate: xₙ₊₁ = xₙ + h, yₙ₊₁ = yₙ + h·f(xₙ, yₙ). Step size h determines accuracy.
Modeling with ODEs: Write equation describing rate of change. Example: dP/dt = kP − m (logistic with harvesting). Solve to find population behavior.
Key Terms & Definitions
Differential equation: Equation with derivatives as unknowns.
ODE (Ordinary differential equation): Single independent variable.
Initial condition: Value y(x₀) = y₀ specifying particular solution.
Separable: Variables can be separated to opposite sides of equation.
Slope field: Graphical display of directions at each point (x,y).

Solve the differential equation dy/dx = 2xy with initial condition y(0) = 3.

Separate: dy/y = 2x dx. [1]
Integrate: ln|y| = x² + C. [1]
Initial condition: ln 3 = 0 + C → C = ln 3. So ln|y| = x² + ln 3. [1]
Solution: y = 3e^(x²). (Taking exponentials: |y| = e^(x²+ln3) = 3e^(x²). Since y(0) = 3 > 0, y = 3e^(x²).) [1]
Common error: Forgetting the constant of integration C, or forgetting to apply the initial condition. Without the IC, the answer is a general solution with an arbitrary constant — not a particular solution.
Analysis[3 marks]L3

A population P grows according to dP/dt = 0.05P with P(0) = 1000. Find P(t) and the doubling time.

Solve: dP/P = 0.05 dt → ln P = 0.05t + C. P(0) = 1000 → C = ln 1000. P = 1000e^(0.05t). [1]
Doubling: 2000 = 1000e^(0.05t) → 2 = e^(0.05t) → t = ln 2/0.05 = 13.86 ≈ 13.9 time units. [1]
General: For exponential growth dP/dt = kP, the doubling time is always T₂ = ln 2/k, independent of initial population. [1]
Common error: Using log₁₀ instead of ln when solving exponential equations. Since the model uses e, you must use natural logarithm.
