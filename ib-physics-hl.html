A.1 — Kinematics
Displacement, velocity, acceleration. Equations of motion. Graphs of motion. Projectile motion.
▸
Key Concepts
Displacement vs distance: Displacement is the straight-line distance with direction from start to finish (vector). Distance is the total path length (scalar). An object can travel a large distance but have zero displacement (e.g., circular path).
Velocity and acceleration: Velocity = rate of change of displacement (vector). Speed = rate of change of distance (scalar). Acceleration = rate of change of velocity. All three can be read from graphs: slope of s-t = v, slope of v-t = a, area under v-t = displacement.
SUVAT equations: For uniform acceleration: v = u + at, s = ut + ½at², v² = u² + 2as, s = ½(u+v)t. Only valid when acceleration is constant. Choose the equation that uses the three known quantities plus the one unknown.
Projectile motion: Horizontal component: constant velocity (aₓ = 0). Vertical component: constant acceleration (aᵧ = g = 9.81 m/s²). The two components are independent. Decompose initial velocity: vₓ = v₀cosθ, vᵧ = v₀sinθ.
Key Terms & Definitions
Displacement (s): The change in position of an object; a vector quantity with magnitude and direction. SI unit: m.
Instantaneous velocity: The velocity at a specific moment in time; the gradient of a displacement-time graph at that point.
Uniform acceleration: Acceleration that remains constant in magnitude and direction over time.
Free fall: Motion under gravity alone, with no air resistance. All objects experience the same acceleration g regardless of mass.
Key Formulas
v = u + at
Final velocity
s = ut + ½at²
Displacement
v² = u² + 2as
Velocity-displacement relation
s = ½(u + v)t
Displacement using average velocity
Exam Focus
Extract information from motion graphs: gradient of s-t = velocity, gradient of v-t = acceleration, area under v-t = displacement.
Projectile problems: always decompose into x and y, solve separately, link through time.
State assumptions clearly: "air resistance is negligible", "acceleration is constant".

🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

Define acceleration and state its SI unit. Distinguish between uniform and non-uniform acceleration.

Definition: Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity: a = Δv/Δt. SI unit: m s⁻². It is a vector quantity. [1]
Distinction: Uniform acceleration means a is constant (straight line on v-t graph). Non-uniform means a varies with time (curved v-t graph). [1]
Common error: Writing "change in speed over time" — IB requires "change in velocity" since acceleration is a vector. This distinction costs marks every session.
Calculation[4 marks]L2

A stone is dropped from rest from the top of a cliff. It hits the sea 3.2 s later. Calculate (a) the height of the cliff and (b) the speed at which the stone hits the water. Assume g = 9.81 m s⁻² and air resistance is negligible.

Given: u = 0 (dropped from rest), t = 3.2 s, a = g = 9.81 m s⁻².
(a) Height: s = ut + ½at² = 0 + ½(9.81)(3.2²) = ½(9.81)(10.24) = 50.2 m ≈ 50 m [2]
(b) Speed: v = u + at = 0 + 9.81 × 3.2 = 31.4 m s⁻¹ ≈ 31 m s⁻¹ [1]
Check: v² = u² + 2as = 0 + 2(9.81)(50.2) = 985.9 → v = 31.4 m s⁻¹ ✓ [1]
Common error: Forgetting u = 0 for "dropped from rest" and using a non-zero initial velocity. Also: IB expects you to state the assumption "air resistance negligible" even when the question says it — write it in your answer for the communication mark.
Application[4 marks]L2

A ball is launched horizontally at 12 m s⁻¹ from a table 1.5 m above the floor. Determine (a) the time to reach the floor and (b) the horizontal distance from the edge of the table where the ball lands.

Key principle: Horizontal and vertical motions are independent. Horizontal: constant velocity (aₓ = 0). Vertical: free fall from rest (uᵧ = 0, a = 9.81 m s⁻²). [1]
(a) Time: Vertical: s = ½gt² → 1.5 = ½(9.81)t² → t² = 0.3058 → t = 0.553 s ≈ 0.55 s [1]
(b) Range: Horizontal: x = vₓt = 12 × 0.553 = 6.64 m ≈ 6.6 m [1]
Note: The horizontal velocity does not affect the fall time — a ball dropped from the same height would hit the floor at the same time. [1]
Common error: Using the horizontal velocity in the vertical equation. The two components must be solved separately and linked only through time t.
Analysis[5 marks]L3

The velocity-time graph below shows the motion of a skydiver from the moment they jump until they reach the ground.

Phase A: velocity increases rapidly (0–10 s)
Phase B: velocity increases slowly then becomes constant (10–40 s)
Phase C: velocity suddenly decreases (40–43 s)
Phase D: velocity is small and constant (43–120 s)

Explain the motion in each phase in terms of the forces acting on the skydiver.

Phase A: Weight >> drag (low speed = low drag). Net downward force is large, so acceleration is large. As speed increases, drag increases. [1]
Phase B: Drag increases with speed until drag = weight. Net force = 0, acceleration = 0. The skydiver reaches terminal velocity (~55 m s⁻¹). [1]
Phase C: Parachute opens — drag increases dramatically (larger surface area). Drag >> weight, so there is a large net upward force causing rapid deceleration. [1]
Phase D: At the new (lower) terminal velocity (~5 m s⁻¹), drag again equals weight. Net force = 0, constant velocity until landing. [1]
Key principle: Terminal velocity occurs whenever drag = weight, not at a single fixed speed. The terminal velocity depends on the cross-sectional area and shape of the falling object. [1]
Common error: Saying "there is no gravity" or "no forces act" at terminal velocity. Both weight and drag still act — they are equal and opposite, giving zero NET force. IB mark schemes penalise this heavily.
A.2 — Forces and Momentum
Newton's laws. Free-body diagrams. Friction, drag. Impulse. Momentum conservation. Collisions.
▸
Key Concepts
Newton's three laws: 1st: An object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted on by a net external force (inertia). 2nd: F_net = ma — net force equals mass times acceleration. 3rd: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction (forces act on different objects).
Free-body diagrams: Show ALL forces acting on a single object as arrows from the centre of mass. Include weight (mg down), normal force (perpendicular to surface), friction (opposing motion along surface), tension, applied forces, drag.
Linear momentum: p = mv (vector). The law of conservation of momentum: in an isolated system (no external forces), total momentum before = total momentum after. Applies to all collisions and explosions.
Impulse: J = FΔt = Δp. The area under a force-time graph equals the change in momentum. Airbags and crumple zones increase Δt to reduce F for the same Δp.
Types of collision: Elastic: both momentum and KE conserved. Inelastic: momentum conserved, KE not conserved (some converted to heat/sound). Perfectly inelastic: objects stick together — maximum KE loss.
Key Terms & Definitions
Net force (resultant force): The vector sum of all forces acting on an object.
Momentum (p): The product of an object's mass and velocity. A vector quantity. Unit: kg·m/s or N·s.
Impulse (J): The change in momentum of an object, equal to force × time. Unit: N·s.
Friction: A contact force opposing relative motion between surfaces. Static friction ≤ μₛN; kinetic friction = μₖN.
Key Formulas

Newton's second law

Linear momentum

Σp_before = Σp_after

Exam Focus
Always draw a free-body diagram first. Resolve forces into components along convenient axes.
Collision problems: use conservation of momentum. Check if elastic by comparing KE before and after.
Explain safety features (airbags, seatbelts, helmets) using impulse: increasing time reduces force.

🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

State Newton's third law and give one example involving contact forces.

Law: When body A exerts a force on body B, body B exerts an equal and opposite force on body A. The forces are the same type, act on different bodies, and act simultaneously. [1 mark]
Example: When you push a wall, the wall pushes back on your hand with an equal force in the opposite direction (both are normal contact forces). [1 mark]
Common error: Saying weight and normal force are a Newton's third law pair — they are not. They act on the same body and are different types of force.
Application[3 marks]L2

A 1200 kg car travelling at 15 m/s collides with a stationary 800 kg car. After the collision, the two cars move together. Determine whether this collision is elastic or inelastic, justifying your answer.

Momentum before: p = 1200 × 15 + 800 × 0 = 18000 kg·m/s [1 mark]
Velocity after: By conservation of momentum: (1200 + 800)v = 18000, so v = 9.0 m/s [1 mark]
Classification: KE before = ½(1200)(15²) = 135000 J. KE after = ½(2000)(9²) = 81000 J. KE is not conserved (54000 J lost), so the collision is inelastic. Since the objects stick together, it is perfectly inelastic. [1 mark]
Common error: Assuming momentum is not conserved in inelastic collisions. Momentum is always conserved in all collisions (when no external forces act). Only kinetic energy is not conserved.
Calculation[4 marks]L2

A 5.0 kg block on a horizontal surface is pulled by a rope at 30° above the horizontal with a force of 40 N. The coefficient of kinetic friction is μₖ = 0.25. Calculate the acceleration of the block. Take g = 9.81 m/s².

Resolve applied force: Fₓ = 40 cos 30° = 34.6 N, Fᵧ = 40 sin 30° = 20.0 N [1 mark]
Normal force: N = mg − Fᵧ = (5.0 × 9.81) − 20.0 = 49.05 − 20.0 = 29.05 N [1 mark]
Friction: f = μₖN = 0.25 × 29.05 = 7.26 N [1 mark]
Acceleration: a = (Fₓ − f) / m = (34.6 − 7.26) / 5.0 = 27.34 / 5.0 = 5.5 m/s² [1 mark]
Common error: Using N = mg without subtracting the vertical component of the applied force. When pulling upward at an angle, the normal force is reduced, which reduces friction.
Analysis[4 marks]L3

Explain why a passenger in a car feels pushed backward when the car accelerates forward, using Newton's laws. Discuss whether a real force acts on the passenger in the backward direction.

Newton's first law: The passenger's body tends to remain at rest (inertia) while the car accelerates forward beneath them. [1 mark]
Real force: The seat pushes the passenger forward (friction/normal force from seat back). There is no real backward force — the sensation is due to the non-inertial reference frame of the car. [1 mark]
Inertial vs non-inertial: In the ground (inertial) frame, the passenger accelerates forward due to the seat force. In the car's (non-inertial) frame, a fictitious force appears to push the passenger backward. [1 mark]
Conclusion: The "backward push" is a pseudo-force that only exists in the accelerating reference frame. Newton's laws in their standard form only apply in inertial frames. [1 mark]
Common error: Claiming there is a real backward force on the passenger. The IB mark scheme requires distinguishing between real forces and effects of inertia in non-inertial frames.
A.3 — Work, Energy and Power
Work done. Kinetic and potential energy. Conservation of energy. Power. Efficiency.
▸
Key Concepts
Work done: W = Fs cosθ, where θ is the angle between force and displacement. Work is a scalar. Only the component of force parallel to displacement does work. If force is perpendicular to displacement, W = 0 (e.g., centripetal force).
Kinetic and potential energy: KE = ½mv². Gravitational PE = mgh (near Earth's surface). Elastic PE = ½kx². The work-energy theorem: net work done on an object = change in its kinetic energy.
Conservation of energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or transformed. In an isolated system: total energy is constant. In practice, some energy is always dissipated as thermal energy (friction, drag).
Power and efficiency: Power = energy transferred / time = W/t = Fv. Efficiency = useful energy output / total energy input × 100%. Always less than 100% due to energy dissipation.
Key Formulas

P = W/t = Fv

Efficiency
Exam Focus
Energy conservation problems: set up E_total(initial) = E_total(final), including all forms (KE, PE, work done by friction).
Sankey diagrams: width of arrows represents energy. Useful output + wasted = total input.

🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

State the principle of conservation of energy and define efficiency in terms of energy.

Conservation: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred from one form to another. The total energy of an isolated system remains constant. [1 mark]
Efficiency: η = useful energy output / total energy input × 100%. Alternatively, η = useful power output / total power input × 100%. [1 mark]
Common error: Forgetting to multiply by 100% when expressing efficiency as a percentage, or stating efficiency can exceed 100%.
Application[3 marks]L2

A roller coaster car of mass 500 kg starts from rest at the top of a 30 m hill. Assuming no friction, determine its speed at the bottom. Then explain qualitatively how friction would affect this result.

Energy conservation: mgh = ½mv² → v = √(2gh) = √(2 × 9.81 × 30) = √588.6 = 24.3 m/s [1 mark]
Note: Mass cancels — the speed is independent of the car's mass (same as free fall). [1 mark]
With friction: Some GPE is converted to thermal energy via friction, so less KE is available at the bottom. The actual speed would be less than 24.3 m/s. [1 mark]
Common error: Including mass in the final calculation when it cancels. Also: stating friction "destroys" energy — it converts KE to thermal energy, not destroys it.
Calculation[4 marks]L2

A crane lifts a 250 kg steel beam vertically through 12 m in 8.0 s at constant velocity. Calculate the power output of the crane. Take g = 9.81 m/s².

Given: m = 250 kg, h = 12 m, t = 8.0 s, g = 9.81 m/s².
Work done: W = mgh = 250 × 9.81 × 12 = 29430 J [1 mark]
Power: P = W/t = 29430 / 8.0 = 3679 W [1 mark]
Answer: P ≈ 3.7 kW (2 s.f.) [1 mark]
Check: Constant velocity means net force = 0, so crane force = weight = mg. P = Fv = 250 × 9.81 × (12/8) = 3679 W ✓ [1 mark]
Common error: Forgetting that "constant velocity" means the crane force exactly equals the weight — no net acceleration. Some students add an extra force for acceleration.
Analysis[4 marks]L3

A student claims: "A ball thrown upward has zero energy at the top of its path because it has stopped moving." Evaluate this claim using the concepts of kinetic and potential energy.

KE at top: The student is correct that KE = ½mv² = 0 at the top since v = 0. [1 mark]
GPE at top: However, the ball has maximum gravitational potential energy (GPE = mgh) at the highest point. Energy has been transferred from KE to GPE during the ascent. [1 mark]
Total energy: By conservation of energy, total mechanical energy (KE + GPE) remains constant throughout the flight (ignoring air resistance). The total energy is never zero. [1 mark]
Evaluation: The claim is false. The ball has zero kinetic energy but maximum potential energy. The student has confused "not moving" with "no energy" — a fundamental misconception about energy storage. [1 mark]
Common error: Only discussing KE without mentioning GPE. Full marks require explicit reference to energy transformation and conservation.
A.4 — Rigid Body Mechanics HL
Torque. Rotational equilibrium. Moment of inertia. Angular momentum. Rotational kinetic energy.
▸
Key Concepts
Torque (τ): The rotational equivalent of force. τ = Fr sinθ = F × moment arm. Causes angular acceleration. Net torque = 0 for rotational equilibrium. Unit: N·m.
Moment of inertia (I): The rotational equivalent of mass — resistance to angular acceleration. Depends on mass and how it is distributed relative to the axis. τ_net = Iα (Newton's 2nd law for rotation).
Angular momentum: L = Iω (analogous to p = mv). Conservation: in the absence of external torques, total angular momentum is constant. Explains why a spinning figure skater speeds up when pulling arms in (I decreases, ω increases).
Rotational kinetic energy: E_rot = ½Iω². A rolling object has both translational KE (½mv²) and rotational KE (½Iω²). Total KE = ½mv² + ½Iω².
Key Formulas

Torque
τ_net = Iα
Newton's 2nd law for rotation
L = Iω

E_rot = ½Iω²
Rotational kinetic energy
Exam Focus
Rolling problems: use energy conservation with E_total = ½mv² + ½Iω² + mgh. Remember v = ωr for rolling without slipping.
Conservation of angular momentum: identify when external torque is zero and apply L_before = L_after.
🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

Define torque and state its SI unit.

Definition: Torque is the product of force and the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force: τ = Fr sin θ. [1]
Unit: N m (newton metre). It is not the same as a joule, even though the dimensions are equivalent. [1]
Common error: Writing the unit as "Nm" or "J". IB requires "N m" (with a space) and explicitly penalises using joules for torque.
Calculation[4 marks]L2

A uniform rod of mass 2.0 kg and length 1.2 m is pivoted at one end. A force of 5.0 N is applied perpendicular to the rod at the free end. The moment of inertia of a uniform rod about one end is I = ⅓ML². Calculate (a) the moment of inertia and (b) the angular acceleration of the rod.

Given: M = 2.0 kg, L = 1.2 m, F = 5.0 N, I = ⅓ML².
(a) I: I = ⅓ × 2.0 × (1.2)² = ⅓ × 2.0 × 1.44 = 0.96 kg m² [1]
Torque: τ = F × L = 5.0 × 1.2 = 6.0 N m (force is perpendicular, so sin 90° = 1) [1]
(b) α: τ = Iα → α = τ/I = 6.0/0.96 = 6.25 rad s⁻² ≈ 6.3 rad s⁻² [2]
Common error: Forgetting to use the correct moment of inertia formula. A rod pivoted at the end uses I = ⅓ML², not I = 1/12 ML² (which is for the centre).
Analysis[4 marks]L3

An ice skater spins with arms extended at 2.0 rev s⁻¹. She then pulls her arms in, reducing her moment of inertia to 60% of its original value. Explain, using conservation of angular momentum, what happens to her angular velocity and rotational kinetic energy.

Conservation: No external torque acts, so angular momentum L = Iω is conserved: I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂. [1]
New ω: I₂ = 0.60 I₁, so ω₂ = I₁ω₁/(0.60 I₁) = ω₁/0.60 = 2.0/0.60 = 3.3 rev s⁻¹. She spins faster. [1]
KE change: KE = ½Iω². KE₂/KE₁ = (0.60)(3.3/2.0)² = 0.60 × 2.78 = 1.67. KE increases by ~67%. [1]
Energy source: The extra KE comes from the work done by the skater's muscles as she pulls her arms inward against centripetal acceleration. [1]
Common error: Claiming KE is conserved — it is not. Only angular momentum is conserved. The skater does internal work, increasing KE.
A.5 — Galilean and Special Relativity HL
Reference frames. Lorentz factor. Time dilation. Length contraction. Relativistic energy.
▸
Key Concepts
Einstein's postulates: (1) The laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames. (2) The speed of light in a vacuum (c ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s) is the same for all observers regardless of their motion or the motion of the source.
Lorentz factor: γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²). Always ≥ 1. Approaches 1 at low speeds. Approaches infinity as v → c. This factor appears in all relativistic equations.
Time dilation: A moving clock runs slower: Δt = γΔt₀, where Δt₀ is the proper time (measured in the rest frame of the clock). Muon experiments confirm this — muons survive longer than expected because their "clocks" run slow.
Length contraction: A moving object is shortened in the direction of motion: L = L₀/γ, where L₀ is the proper length (measured at rest). Only affects the direction of motion.
Relativistic energy: Total energy E = γmc². Rest energy E₀ = mc². Kinetic energy E_k = (γ−1)mc². The mass-energy equivalence means mass is a form of energy.
Key Formulas
γ = 1 / √(1 − v²/c²)
Lorentz factor
Δt = γΔt₀

L = L₀ / γ

E = γmc² , E₀ = mc² , E_k = (γ−1)mc²
Relativistic energy
p = γmv
Relativistic momentum
Exam Focus
Identify proper time (Δt₀) and proper length (L₀) — always measured in the object's rest frame.
Calculate γ first, then apply to time dilation, length contraction, or energy equations.
Explain muon decay as evidence for time dilation (or equivalently, length contraction from the muon's frame).
🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

State the two postulates of Einstein's special theory of relativity.

Postulate 1: The laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames. [1]
Postulate 2: The speed of light in a vacuum is the same for all observers, regardless of the motion of the source or observer (c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹). [1]
Common error: Stating "the speed of light is constant" without specifying "in a vacuum" and "for all inertial observers". Both qualifiers are needed for full marks.
Calculation[4 marks]L3

A muon is created in the upper atmosphere travelling at 0.98c. Its proper lifetime (rest frame) is 2.2 μs. Calculate (a) the Lorentz factor, (b) the dilated lifetime in the Earth frame, and (c) the distance the muon travels before decaying as measured by an Earth observer.

(a) γ: γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²) = 1/√(1 − 0.98²) = 1/√(1 − 0.9604) = 1/√0.0396 = 1/0.199 = 5.03 ≈ 5.0 [1]
(b) Dilated lifetime: Δt = γΔt₀ = 5.0 × 2.2 = 11.0 μs [1]
(c) Distance: d = vΔt = 0.98 × 3.0 × 10⁸ × 11.0 × 10⁻⁶ = 3.2 × 10³ m = 3.2 km [1]
Significance: Without time dilation, d = 0.98c × 2.2 μs = 647 m — the muon would not reach the surface. Time dilation explains why muons are detected at ground level. [1]
Common error: Using the proper lifetime directly to calculate distance in the Earth frame. The Earth observer measures the dilated time, not the proper time.
Analysis[4 marks]L3

Explain how both time dilation (Earth frame) and length contraction (muon frame) account for muons reaching Earth's surface. Why are both explanations equally valid?

Earth frame: The muon's lifetime is dilated by factor γ, so it lives long enough to travel the full distance to the surface. [1]
Muon frame: The muon lives for its proper lifetime (2.2 μs), but the distance to Earth's surface is length-contracted by factor γ, so the surface reaches the muon in time. [1]
Equivalence: Both frames give the same physical result (muon reaches surface). This is required by the first postulate — no inertial frame is preferred. [1]
Key point: Time dilation and length contraction are not separate phenomena but two aspects of the same spacetime geometry, viewed from different frames. [1]
Common error: Treating one frame as "correct" and the other as an approximation. Both are equally valid — this is the core of relativity.

The Particulate Nature of Matter

B.1 — Thermal Energy Transfers
Temperature. Internal energy. Specific heat capacity. Phase changes. Latent heat. Thermal conduction.
▸
Key Concepts
Temperature vs internal energy: Temperature measures the average KE of particles. Internal energy is the total KE + PE of all particles. Two objects can have the same temperature but different internal energies (different masses or states).
Specific heat capacity (c): The energy needed to raise 1 kg of a substance by 1 K. Q = mcΔT. Water has a high c (4180 J/kg·K), which is why it's used as a coolant and moderates climate.
Phase changes and latent heat: During a phase change, temperature stays constant — energy goes into breaking/forming intermolecular bonds, not increasing KE. Q = mL, where L is specific latent heat (fusion for melting, vaporisation for boiling).
Methods of heat transfer: Conduction (through solids, particle vibrations), convection (bulk movement of fluids), radiation (EM waves, no medium needed). Rate of conduction: Q/t = kAΔT/d.
Key Formulas
Q = mcΔT
Heat for temperature change
Q = mL
Heat for phase change
Q/t = kAΔT/d
Rate of thermal conduction
Exam Focus
Heating curve problems: calculate energy for each stage (heating, phase change, heating) separately then sum.
Explain why temperature remains constant during a phase change in terms of breaking intermolecular bonds.
🎯 Practice Questions
Recall[2 marks]L1

Define specific heat capacity and state its SI unit.

Definition: Specific heat capacity is the energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C). Symbol: c. [1]
Unit: J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹. From Q = mcΔT → c = Q/(mΔT). [1]
Common error: Writing "heat capacity" instead of "specific heat capacity". Heat capacity (C) is for the whole object; specific (c) is per unit mass. IB distinguishes these.
Calculation[4 marks]L2

0.50 kg of water at 20°C is heated to 100°C and then completely converted to steam at 100°C. Calculate the total energy required. Data: c_water = 4180 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹, L_v = 2.26 × 10⁶ J kg⁻¹.

Heating: Q₁ = mcΔT = 0.50 × 4180 × (100 − 20) = 0.50 × 4180 × 80 = 167200 J [1]
Vaporisation: Q₂ = mL = 0.50 × 2.26 × 10⁶ = 1130000 J [1]
Total: Q = Q₁ + Q₂ = 167200 + 1130000 = 1297200 J ≈ 1.3 × 10⁶ J = 1.3 MJ [1]
Note: The vaporisation energy (1.13 MJ) is ~87% of the total — far more energy goes into breaking intermolecular bonds than raising temperature. [1]
Common error: Forgetting to include both the heating stage AND the phase change. Many students calculate only Q = mcΔT and miss the latent heat entirely.
Analysis[3 marks]L2

Explain why a burn from steam at 100°C is more severe than a burn from liquid water at 100°C.

Latent heat: When steam condenses on skin, it releases latent heat of vaporisation (2.26 × 10⁶ J kg⁻¹) in addition to the heat released as it cools. [1]
Water comparison: Liquid water at 100°C can only transfer energy as it cools (Q = mcΔT). It has no latent heat to release. [1]
Conclusion: Steam delivers significantly more energy per kg to the skin than water at the same temperature, causing a more severe burn. [1]
Common error: Saying steam is "hotter" than water at 100°C. They are at the same temperature — the difference is the additional latent heat energy stored in steam.
B.2 — Greenhouse Effect
Albedo, emissivity, solar constant. Greenhouse gases. Energy balance models.
▸
Key Concepts
Solar constant and albedo: Solar constant S ≈ 1361 W/m². Albedo α = fraction of incident radiation reflected. Earth's average α ≈ 0.3. Power absorbed = S(1−α)πR². For energy balance, Earth must radiate as much as it absorbs.
Greenhouse effect: Earth's surface emits IR radiation. Greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, H₂O, N₂O) absorb and re-emit IR in all directions, trapping energy. This raises surface temperature above what it would be without an atmosphere (~255 K → ~288 K).
Enhanced greenhouse effect: Human activities increase greenhouse gas concentrations, intensifying the natural greenhouse effect and causing global warming. This is distinct from the natural greenhouse effect, which makes Earth habitable.
Key Formulas

Define albedo and state its range of values.

Calculation[4 marks]L2

Earth receives a solar constant S = 1361 W m⁻². Earth's albedo is 0.30. Using the Stefan-Boltzmann law (σ = 5.67 × 10⁻⁸ W m⁻² K⁻⁴), calculate the expected equilibrium surface temperature of Earth.

Absorbed power: P_in = S(1 − α)πR² = 1361 × 0.70 × πR² [1]
Emitted power: P_out = σT⁴ × 4πR² (radiates from full sphere) [1]
Equilibrium: S(1−α)πR² = σT⁴(4πR²) → T⁴ = S(1−α)/(4σ) = 1361 × 0.70/(4 × 5.67 × 10⁻⁸) = 952.7/2.268 × 10⁻⁷ = 4.20 × 10⁹ [1]
Answer: T = (4.20 × 10⁹)^(1/4) = 255 K ≈ −18°C [1]
Common error: Using 4πR² for both absorption and emission. Earth absorbs as a disc (πR²) but emits as a sphere (4πR²). This factor of 4 is critical.
Analysis[3 marks]L2

The calculated equilibrium temperature (255 K) is significantly lower than Earth's actual average temperature (288 K). Explain this discrepancy.

Greenhouse effect: Greenhouse gases (CO₂, H₂O, CH₄) in the atmosphere absorb outgoing infrared radiation from Earth's surface and re-emit it in all directions, including back toward the surface. [1]
Energy balance: The surface receives both solar radiation AND back-radiation from the atmosphere, so it must be warmer than the simple equilibrium calculation predicts to radiate enough energy to maintain balance. [1]
Temperature difference: The 33 K difference (288 − 255 = 33 K) is entirely due to the natural greenhouse effect. Without it, Earth would be frozen. [1]
Common error: Saying greenhouse gases "trap heat" — they absorb and re-emit IR radiation. The mechanism is absorption and re-emission, not trapping.
B.3 — Gas Laws
Ideal gas model. PV = nRT. Kinetic molecular theory. Pressure-volume-temperature relationships.

Key Concepts

Analysis[4 marks]L3

Explain why real gases deviate from ideal gas behaviour at (a) high pressures and (b) low temperatures.

(a) High pressure: At high pressure, molecules are forced close together. Their actual volume becomes significant compared to the container volume, violating the assumption of negligible molecular volume. [1]
Also at high P: Intermolecular forces become significant at small separations, violating the assumption of no forces between molecules. [1]
(b) Low temperature: At low T, molecules move slowly and have low KE. Intermolecular attractive forces become comparable to KE, so molecules spend more time interacting rather than moving freely. [1]
Consequence: At sufficiently low T, the gas condenses into a liquid — the ultimate deviation from ideal behaviour. Ideal gas law cannot predict phase changes. [1]
Common error: Only discussing one factor (volume or forces) at high pressure. IB mark schemes typically award separate marks for each factor.
B.4 — Thermodynamics HL

▸
Key Concepts
First law of thermodynamics: Q = ΔU + W. Heat added to a system either increases internal energy or does work on surroundings (or both). This is energy conservation applied to thermal systems.
P-V diagrams: Work done BY a gas = area under the curve on a P-V diagram. Isothermal (constant T): PV = const, hyperbola. Isobaric (constant P): horizontal line. Isovolumetric (constant V): vertical line. Adiabatic (Q = 0): steeper than isothermal.

Carnot cycle: The most efficient possible heat engine operating between two temperatures. η_Carnot = 1 − T_cold/T_hot. All real engines are less efficient than Carnot.

Analysis[4 marks]L3

Explain, using the second law of thermodynamics, why no heat engine can ever be 100% efficient. Reference the Carnot efficiency in your answer.

Second law: It is impossible to convert heat completely into work in a cyclic process. Some heat must always be rejected to a cold reservoir. [1]
Entropy: Total entropy of the universe must increase (or remain constant for reversible processes). 100% efficiency would require zero entropy increase, which is impossible for any real process. [1]

Practical implication: Even an ideal (reversible) engine cannot reach 100%. Real engines are even less efficient due to friction, irreversible heat transfer, etc. [1]
Common error: Stating "friction causes inefficiency" as the main reason. While true for real engines, the fundamental limit is thermodynamic (second law), not mechanical.

Calculation[4 marks]L2

A battery of EMF 12 V and internal resistance 0.50 Ω is connected to two resistors: 4.0 Ω and 6.0 Ω in parallel. Calculate (a) the total external resistance, (b) the current from the battery, and (c) the terminal potential difference.

Analysis[3 marks]L2

Explain why the terminal potential difference of a battery decreases as the current drawn from it increases.

Internal resistance: The battery has internal resistance r. When current I flows, there is a voltage drop across r equal to Ir (the "lost volts"). [1]
Terminal PD: V = EMF − Ir. As I increases, the lost volts (Ir) increase, so V decreases. [1]
Limit: When the external resistance approaches zero (short circuit), I → EMF/r and V → 0. All energy is dissipated internally. [1]
Common error: Saying the EMF decreases. EMF is a property of the battery's chemistry and does not change with current. Only the terminal PD changes.

C.3 — Wave Phenomena

▸
Key Concepts

▸
Key Concepts
Types of radiation: Alpha (α): ⁴₂He nucleus, +2 charge, most ionising, least penetrating (stopped by paper/skin). Beta-minus (β⁻): electron emitted from nucleus (neutron → proton + electron + antineutrino), moderate ionisation/penetration (stopped by aluminium). Gamma (γ): EM radiation, least ionising, most penetrating (reduced by thick lead/concrete).
Nuclear equations: Conservation of mass number (A) and atomic number (Z) on both sides. α decay: Z decreases by 2, A by 4. β⁻ decay: Z increases by 1, A unchanged. γ: no change in Z or A (energy released only).
Half-life (t½): The time for half the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. After n half-lives: N = N₀(½)ⁿ. Activity A = λN = A₀e^(−λt), where λ = ln2/t½.
Random and spontaneous: Radioactive decay is random (cannot predict which nucleus decays next) and spontaneous (not affected by external conditions like temperature or pressure).
Key Formulas
N = N₀(½)^(t/t½) = N₀e^(−λt)
Radioactive decay law
λ = ln2 / t½
Decay constant
A = λN = A₀e^(−λt)
Activity
Exam Focus
Write balanced nuclear equations for α and β decay. Ensure A and Z are conserved.
Half-life calculations: find remaining amount, time elapsed, or number of half-lives.
Compare α, β, γ in terms of charge, mass, ionising ability, penetrating power, and what stops them.
E.4 — Fission and Fusion
Binding energy. Mass defect. Fission chain reactions. Fusion in stars. E = mc².
▸
Key Concepts
Mass defect and binding energy: The mass of a nucleus is less than the sum of its individual nucleons. This "missing" mass (mass defect, Δm) has been converted to binding energy: E_b = Δmc². Higher binding energy per nucleon = more stable nucleus.
Binding energy per nucleon curve: Peaks at Fe-56 (most stable). Lighter elements can fuse (moving right up the curve). Heavier elements can undergo fission (moving left up the curve). Both processes release energy because products are more tightly bound.
Nuclear fission: A heavy nucleus (e.g., U-235) splits into two lighter nuclei + neutrons + energy. Chain reaction: released neutrons cause more fissions. Controlled in reactors (moderator slows neutrons, control rods absorb excess neutrons).
Nuclear fusion: Light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus + energy. Requires extreme temperatures (~10⁷ K) to overcome electrostatic repulsion. Powers the Sun (hydrogen → helium). Very difficult to achieve controlled fusion on Earth.
Key Formulas
E = mc² = Δmc²
Mass-energy equivalence
1 u = 931.5 MeV/c²
Atomic mass unit to energy conversion
Δm = Zm_p + Nm_n − m_nucleus
Mass defect
Exam Focus
Calculate mass defect and binding energy from nuclear masses. Convert between u and MeV using 1 u = 931.5 MeV/c².
Use the binding energy per nucleon curve to explain why fission and fusion release energy.
Describe the conditions needed for a sustained fission chain reaction and how a nuclear reactor controls it.
E.5 — Energy Production
Fossil fuels. Nuclear power. Renewable energy sources. Sankey diagrams. Efficiency.
▸
Key Concepts
Fossil fuels: Coal, oil, natural gas. High energy density. Reliable and transportable. Problems: CO₂ emissions (climate change), SO₂/NOₓ (acid rain), finite resource. Thermal power stations: fuel → heat → steam → turbine → generator.
Nuclear power: Very high energy density. No direct CO₂ emissions during operation. Concerns: radioactive waste (long half-lives), meltdown risk, weapons proliferation, decommissioning costs. Provides baseload power.
Renewable sources: Solar (photovoltaic and thermal), wind, hydroelectric, tidal, geothermal, biomass. Advantages: sustainable, low emissions. Disadvantages: intermittent (except geothermal), low energy density, large land area, weather-dependent.
Energy density and efficiency: Specific energy = energy per unit mass (J/kg). Energy density = energy per unit volume (J/m³). Sankey diagrams show energy flow: width = energy, useful output + losses = input. No process is 100% efficient.
Exam Focus
Compare energy sources: advantages, disadvantages, efficiency, environmental impact, reliability.
Draw and interpret Sankey diagrams for power stations. Calculate overall efficiency from input and useful output.
Discuss the energy mix needed for sustainable development — why one source alone is insufficient.
