Rörelse och Krafter — Motion & Forces
▸
2D Motion in Gravitational Fields
Projectile motion: horizontal launch and angled launch. Decomposing velocity into x/y components. Trajectory equations, max height, range.
▸
Key Concepts
Independence of horizontal and vertical motion: In projectile motion, the horizontal component of velocity remains constant (no air resistance), while the vertical component is affected by gravitational acceleration g = 9.81 m/s². These two components act independently of each other.
Horizontal launch (horisontellt kast): Object launched horizontally from a height. Initial vertical velocity = 0. Horizontal distance depends on launch speed and fall time. The path is a parabola.
Angled launch (snett kast): Object launched at angle θ to the horizontal. Initial velocity is decomposed into v₀ₓ = v₀·cos θ and v₀ᵧ = v₀·sin θ. Maximum height occurs when vertical velocity = 0. Range is maximised at θ = 45° (no air resistance).
Symmetry of trajectory: For level ground, time to reach max height = time to fall back. The trajectory is symmetric about the highest point. Total flight time T = 2v₀ sinθ / g.
Key Terms & Definitions
Projectile (projektil): An object moving under the influence of gravity alone after being launched.
Trajectory (bana): The curved path followed by a projectile through space — parabolic in uniform gravity.
Range (räckvidd): The horizontal distance covered by a projectile from launch to landing.
Component (komponent): The projection of a vector along an axis (x or y). Used to break velocity into horizontal and vertical parts.
Free fall (fritt fall): Motion under gravity alone, with no air resistance. Acceleration = g downward.

Exam Focus
Calculate time of flight, max height, or range given initial speed and angle — decompose first, then use kinematics separately for x and y.
Determine the velocity (magnitude and direction) at any point during flight — find vₓ and vᵧ, then use Pythagoras and tan⁻¹.
Horizontal launch from a table/cliff: given height and horizontal speed, find where the object lands.
Sketch or interpret trajectory graphs: x(t) is linear, y(t) is parabolic, vᵧ(t) is linear.
2D Motion in Electric Fields
Charged particle deflection in uniform electric fields. Parabolic trajectories. Comparison with gravitational projectile motion.
▸
Key Concepts
Analogy with gravity: A charged particle entering a uniform electric field perpendicular to its velocity follows a parabolic path — exactly analogous to projectile motion, but with electric force replacing gravity.
Uniform electric field: Between parallel plates with potential difference V and separation d, the field E = V/d is uniform. The force on a charge q is F = qE, giving acceleration a = qE/m.
Deflection of charged particles: A positive charge is deflected toward the negative plate; a negative charge toward the positive plate. The horizontal velocity remains constant while the vertical velocity changes due to the electric force.
Applications: Cathode ray tubes, ink-jet printers, mass spectrometers, and particle accelerators all use electric field deflection.
Key Terms & Definitions
Electric field (elektriskt fält), E: A region where a charged particle experiences a force. Measured in N/C or V/m.
Uniform field (homogent fält): A field with the same strength and direction at every point — produced between parallel plates.
Deflection (avböjning): The change in path of a charged particle due to an electric (or magnetic) field.
Potential difference (spänning), V: The energy per unit charge between two points. Measured in volts (V).
Key Formulas

Relate motion in electric field to projectile motion — both have constant horizontal velocity and uniformly accelerated motion perpendicular to initial velocity.
Given mass, charge, voltage, and plate separation, calculate deflection at a point or find plate length needed to achieve a target deflection.
Determine direction of deflection based on signs of charge and field direction.
Circular Motion & Centripetal Force
Uniform circular motion. Centripetal acceleration and force. Angular velocity, period, frequency. Real forces: friction, tension, gravity, normal force.
▸
Key Concepts
Uniform circular motion: Motion at constant speed in a circle. Although speed is constant, velocity is not (direction changes). Therefore there is acceleration toward the centre (centripetal acceleration).
Centripetal acceleration (centripetal acceleration): Always points toward the centre of the circle. Magnitude: aₒ = v²/r = ω²r, where v is speed, r is radius, ω is angular velocity.
Centripetal force: The net force toward the centre that causes centripetal acceleration. Fₒ = mv²/r = mω²r. This is not a new type of force — it's the resultant of real forces (friction, tension, gravity, normal force, etc.) that point toward the centre.
Real forces providing centripetal force: Car turning on flat road — friction. Satellite orbiting → gravity. Object swinging on string → tension. Ball rolling on inside of circular track → normal force. Identify the real force(s), then set it equal to mv²/r.
Angular quantities: Angular velocity ω = Δθ/Δt (rad/s). Period T = time for one revolution. Frequency f = 1/T = revolutions per second. Relationships: v = ωr, ω = 2π/T = 2πf.
Key Terms & Definitions
Centripetal (centripetal): Directed toward the centre; always used for acceleration and force in circular motion.
Angular velocity, ω (vinkelhastighet): Rate of change of angle. Unit: rad/s. Related to period: ω = 2π/T.
Period, T (period): Time for one complete revolution. Unit: seconds.
Frequency, f (frekvens): Number of revolutions per unit time. Unit: Hz (s⁻¹). f = 1/T.
Radian (radian): Unit of angle. One complete circle = 2π radians = 360°.
Key Formulas

Exam Focus
Identify the real force(s) providing centripetal force in a given scenario (car on curve → friction, satellite → gravity, etc.).
Calculate speed, radius, or centripetal force for objects in circular paths — always draw a free-body diagram first.
Banking of roads: explain why inclined curves reduce reliance on friction (component of normal force provides centripetal force).
Vertical circles: at the top, both gravity and normal/tension point inward; at the bottom, they oppose each other.
Torque & Rotational Equilibrium (Vridmoment)
Torque = F × d. Clockwise vs counterclockwise moments. Conditions for static equilibrium. Lever problems, beams, and balancing.
▸
Key Concepts
Torque (vridmoment, τ): The rotational effect of a force. Depends on the force magnitude, the distance from the pivot, and the angle. τ = F · d · sinθ, where d is the distance from the pivot to the point where the force is applied.
Moment arm (momentarm): The perpendicular distance from the line of action of the force to the pivot point. When force is perpendicular to the lever arm, moment arm = lever arm length.
Conditions for static equilibrium: (1) Net force = 0 (translational equilibrium). (2) Net torque = 0 about any pivot point (rotational equilibrium). Both must hold simultaneously.
Centre of mass / gravity: The point where the entire weight of an object can be considered to act. For a uniform beam, this is the geometric centre.
Key Terms & Definitions
Torque (vridmoment, τ): The measure of a force's tendency to cause rotation about a pivot. Unit: N·m.
Pivot / fulcrum (vridpunkt): The fixed point around which rotation occurs.
Lever arm (hävarm): The distance from the pivot to the point of force application.
Static equilibrium (statisk jämvikt): A state where an object is at rest with zero net force and zero net torque.
Couple (kraftpar): Two equal and opposite parallel forces that produce pure rotation (net force = 0 but net torque ≠ 0).
Key Formulas

τ = F · d⊥

Exam Focus
Calculate torque given force and distance; be careful with the perpendicular distance (moment arm).
Solve equilibrium problems (lever systems, beams, suspended objects) by setting net force = 0 and net torque = 0.
Choose an appropriate pivot point to simplify torque calculations (often the point with unknown forces).
Distinguish between torque and force; understand that a system can be in rotational equilibrium but not translational (or vice versa).
02
Oscillationer och Vågor — Oscillations & Waves
▸
Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)
Defining SHM: F = -kx, restoring force. Equilibrium and displacement. Period, amplitude, energy.
▸
Key Concepts
Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM): Motion where the acceleration is proportional to displacement but opposite in direction: a = -ω²x. Results in a restoring force F = -kx (Hooke's Law). The motion is periodic, oscillating around equilibrium.
Restoring force: The force that always acts toward equilibrium. Magnitude increases with distance from equilibrium. For a spring: F = -kx, where k is the spring constant (stiffness). For a pendulum: F = -mg sinθ ≈ -mg(θ) for small angles.
Equilibrium and displacement: Equilibrium is where the net force is zero. Displacement x is measured from equilibrium. Amplitude A is the maximum displacement (where velocity = 0).
Energy in SHM: Total mechanical energy E = ½kA² is constant. At equilibrium: all kinetic. At maximum displacement: all potential. Continuous exchange between kinetic and potential energy.
Period and frequency: Period T = 2π√(m/k) for a spring. T = 2π√(L/g) for a simple pendulum. Both independent of amplitude (isochronous). Frequency f = 1/T.
Key Terms & Definitions
Amplitude, A: Maximum displacement from equilibrium. Unit: meters.
Period, T: Time for one complete oscillation. Unit: seconds.
Frequency, f: Number of oscillations per second. Unit: Hz. f = 1/T.
Angular frequency, ω: ω = 2πf = 2π/T. Unit: rad/s. Related to SHM equation: a = -ω²x.
Spring constant, k: Measures stiffness of spring. Larger k → stiffer spring → higher frequency. Unit: N/m.
Key Formulas

Exam Focus
Determine if a motion is SHM: acceleration must be proportional to displacement and opposite in direction (a = -ω²x).
Calculate period or frequency from mass and spring constant (or length and gravity for pendulum).
Use energy conservation: total E = ½kA² is constant; at any position, E = ½kx² + ½mv².
Sketch displacement, velocity, and acceleration graphs for SHM; note that v leads x by 90°, and a leads v by 90°.
Damping & Resonance
Damped oscillations: amplitude decay. Damping constants and energy loss. Resonance: natural frequency, driving frequency, amplitude at resonance.
▸
Key Concepts
Damping: A resistive force (e.g., air resistance, friction) proportional to velocity: F_damp = -bv. Opposes motion, removing mechanical energy from the system. Amplitude decreases exponentially: A(t) = A₀e^(-t/τ), where τ is the time constant.
Types of damping: Underdamped (oscillates as it decays), critically damped (fastest return to equilibrium without oscillating), overdamped (slow return without oscillating). For exam purposes, usually underdamped.
Resonance: When a system is driven by an external periodic force at its natural frequency f₀, amplitude grows significantly. The natural frequency is f₀ = √(k/m) / (2π) for a spring-mass system, independent of damping.
Resonance condition: Amplitude is maximum when the driving frequency equals the natural frequency (f_drive = f₀). If damping is small, resonance amplitude can be very large. Larger damping → lower peak amplitude, broader resonance curve.
Applications and examples: Bridges: wind at natural frequency → large oscillations → failure. Radio tuning: select station by setting LC circuit to resonant frequency. Mechanical vibration isolation: choose damping to minimize vibration transmission.
Key Terms & Definitions
Damping (dämpning): Resistance to motion (e.g., friction, air resistance) that removes energy from oscillations.
Damping constant, b (dämpningskonstant): Coefficient in damping force F = -bv. Larger b → stronger damping.
Natural frequency, f₀ (egenfrekvens): Frequency at which a system oscillates freely (no driving force). For spring: f₀ = √(k/m)/(2π).
Driving frequency, f_drive: Frequency of external periodic force applied to the system.
Resonance (resonans): Large amplitude oscillations when f_drive = f₀.

Exam Focus
Sketch amplitude vs. time for damped oscillation; understand that amplitude decays exponentially while frequency remains constant.
Relate natural frequency to spring constant and mass; explain why lighter objects or stiffer springs oscillate faster.
Explain resonance qualitatively: when driving frequency matches natural frequency, the system is continuously "pushed" in phase with its motion → amplitude builds up.
Discuss real-world examples: bridge resonance, radio tuning, car suspension design.
Wave Properties & Wave Equation
Wavelength λ, frequency f, speed v. Transverse vs. longitudinal waves. Sinusoidal wave equation: y = A sin(kx - ωt).
▸
Key Concepts
Wave definition: A disturbance that propagates through a medium (or vacuum for EM waves) transferring energy. Characterized by wavelength λ (distance between adjacent crests/troughs or compressions/rarefactions), frequency f (oscillations per second), and speed v.
Wave speed relation: v = fλ. Speed is determined by the medium (e.g., sound speed in air ≈ 340 m/s; in water ≈ 1480 m/s; light speed c = 3×10⁸ m/s in vacuum). Speed is independent of amplitude or frequency.
Transverse waves: Oscillation perpendicular to direction of propagation (e.g., waves on a string, light). Particles move up and down while wave travels left/right.
Longitudinal waves: Oscillation parallel to direction of propagation (e.g., sound, seismic P-waves). Particles compress and rarefy along the direction of wave travel.
Wave equation: y(x,t) = A sin(kx − ωt), where A is amplitude, k = 2π/λ is wave number, ω = 2πf is angular frequency. Describes the displacement of any point on the medium at any time.
Key Terms & Definitions
Wavelength, λ: Distance between two adjacent points in phase (e.g., crest to crest). Unit: meters.
Frequency, f: Number of complete oscillations per second. Unit: Hz. f = v/λ.
Wave speed, v: Speed at which the wave pattern travels. v = fλ. Unit: m/s.
Amplitude, A: Maximum displacement of a particle from equilibrium. Unit: meters.
Wave number, k: k = 2π/λ (rad/m). Related to wavelength inversely.
Key Formulas

Exam Focus
Use v = fλ to find any unknown (speed, frequency, or wavelength) given the other two.
Distinguish between wave speed (determined by medium) and particle speed (depends on amplitude and frequency).
Interpret and sketch the wave equation y = A sin(kx − ωt): at fixed time, it shows spatial pattern; at fixed position, it shows temporal oscillation.
Determine the direction of wave travel from the wave equation: if it's sin(kx − ωt), wave travels in +x direction; sin(kx + ωt) travels in −x direction.
Interference & Standing Waves
Superposition. Constructive/destructive interference. Standing waves on strings: fixed ends, harmonics, nodes, antinodes.
▸
Key Concepts
Superposition principle: When two waves travel through the same medium, the net displacement at any point is the sum of displacements from each wave. This allows constructive and destructive interference to occur.
Constructive interference: Waves are in phase (path difference = nλ, n = 0,1,2,...). Amplitudes add. Resultant amplitude = A₁ + A₂. Occurs when wave crests/troughs align.
Destructive interference: Waves are out of phase (path difference = (n+½)λ). Amplitudes subtract. If A₁ = A₂, complete cancellation (amplitude = 0). Occurs when crest of one meets trough of another.
Standing waves: Result when two waves of equal frequency travel in opposite directions on a string with fixed ends. Appears stationary (nodes and antinodes don't move along the string).
Nodes and antinodes: Nodes are points of zero displacement (destructive interference). Antinodes are points of maximum displacement (constructive interference). On a string of length L with fixed ends: L = n(λ/2), n = 1,2,3,... giving allowed harmonics.
Key Terms & Definitions
Superposition (superposition): Principle that displacement is the vector sum of individual wave displacements.
Interference (interferens): The result of superposition when two waves combine (constructive or destructive).
Node (nod): A point on a standing wave where displacement is always zero.
Antinode (antinod): A point on a standing wave where displacement is maximum (amplitude = A₁ + A₂).
Harmonic (överton): A mode of vibration at a specific allowed frequency. First harmonic (fundamental) has one half-wavelength on string; second has two half-wavelengths, etc.
Key Formulas
Path difference for constructive interference: Δ = nλ (n = 0, 1, 2, ...)
Path difference for destructive interference: Δ = (n+½)λ

f_n = nv / (2L)

Exam Focus
Determine constructive vs. destructive interference from path difference: if Δ is integer multiple of λ → constructive; if (n+½)λ → destructive.
On a string or pipe with fixed ends, standing waves occur at wavelengths where L = n(λ/2). Calculate wavelength, frequency, or length given the harmonic number.
Sketch standing waves: mark nodes (zero displacement) and antinodes (maximum displacement). Fundamental mode has 1 half-wavelength, second harmonic has 2, etc.
Connect standing waves to musical instruments: longer strings → lower frequencies (larger wavelengths). Harmonics determine timbre.
03
Elektromagnetism — Electromagnetism
▸
Electromagnetic Induction & Faraday's Law
Magnetic flux. EMF and induced currents. Lenz's Law. Changing flux through coil.
▸
Key Concepts
Magnetic flux, Φ: Φ = B·A·cosθ, where B is magnetic field strength, A is area, θ is angle between B and normal to area. Unit: Weber (Wb) = T·m². Higher flux means stronger field or larger area perpendicular to field.
Faraday's Law: EMF induced in a coil is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux: ε = −N(dΦ/dt), where N is number of turns. Negative sign is Lenz's Law — induced EMF opposes the change.
Lenz's Law: The direction of induced current is such that its magnetic field opposes the change in flux. If flux through coil increases, induced current creates a field opposite to the external field (and vice versa). This reflects conservation of energy — you must do work against the induced magnetic force to change flux.
Sources of changing flux: (1) Moving a magnet in/out of a coil. (2) Moving a coil in/out of a magnetic field. (3) Rotating a coil in a constant field. (4) Changing current in a nearby coil (mutual inductance). (5) Changing the area of a coil.
Self-inductance, L: A coil induces an EMF in itself when current changes: ε = −L(dI/dt). L depends on coil geometry (number of turns, area, length). Unit: Henry (H) = Wb/A.
Key Terms & Definitions
Magnetic flux, Φ (magnetisk flöde): Product of magnetic field and perpendicular area. Unit: Weber (Wb).
EMF (electromotive force) (elektromotorisk kraft): Potential difference that drives current. Generated by changing flux. Unit: volt (V).
Induced current (inducerad ström): Current flowing in a conductor due to a changing magnetic field.
Self-inductance, L (självinduktans): Property of a coil that causes it to oppose changes in its own current.
Mutual inductance, M (ömsesidig induktans): The effect of current change in one coil on EMF in a nearby coil.

AC Circuits & Transformers
Alternating current (AC): V(t) = V₀ sin(ωt). RMS values. Transformers: voltage/current relationships, power transfer.
▸
Key Concepts
Alternating current (AC): Current or voltage that varies sinusoidally with time: V(t) = V₀ sin(ωt) or I(t) = I₀ sin(ωt). Frequency is typically 50 Hz (Europe) or 60 Hz (North America). AC can transmit power over long distances more efficiently than DC (lower power loss in transmission lines).
RMS (Root Mean Square) values: For a sinusoidal waveform, V_rms = V₀/√2 and I_rms = I₀/√2. RMS values are what an AC multimeter reads and what most AC power calculations use. For example, household "120 V AC" is actually 120 V_rms ≈ 170 V peak.
Transformers: Device using two coils and magnetic induction to change voltage and current. Ideal transformer: V_primary/V_secondary = N_primary/N_secondary = I_secondary/I_primary. Power is conserved: V_primary × I_primary = V_secondary × I_secondary.
Step-up vs. step-down: Step-up transformer (N_secondary > N_primary) increases voltage but decreases current. Step-down (N_secondary < N_primary) decreases voltage but increases current. Used in power distribution: step-up near power plant (high V, low I) to reduce transmission losses, then step-down near homes.
Transformer losses: Ideal transformers have 100% efficiency. Real transformers have losses due to coil resistance (I²R heating) and core losses (hysteresis). Efficiency typically 95–99%.
Key Terms & Definitions
Alternating current, AC (växelström): Current that reverses direction periodically. Sine wave is most common.
RMS value (effektivvärde): Root mean square; equivalent DC value that delivers same power. V_rms = V₀/√2 ≈ 0.707 V₀.
Peak value (toppvärde): Maximum instantaneous value of AC voltage or current. Also called amplitude.
Transformer (transformer): Device that changes voltage/current of AC using electromagnetic induction.
Turns ratio (lindningsförhållande): Ratio N_secondary/N_primary determines voltage and current transformation.

Electromagnetic Waves & Maxwell's Equations
Displacement current. Maxwell equations (overview). EM wave generation. Speed of light c = 1/√(ε₀μ₀).
▸
Key Concepts
Displacement current: A changing electric field acts like a current in generating a magnetic field. Maxwell added the term dE/dt to Ampère's Law, showing that a time-varying electric field produces a magnetic field even without physical current. This asymmetry was key to predicting EM waves.
Maxwell's Equations (overview): (1) Gauss's Law: electric charges create electric fields. (2) No magnetic monopoles: magnetic field lines form closed loops. (3) Faraday's Law: changing magnetic flux induces electric field. (4) Ampère-Maxwell Law: currents and changing electric flux create magnetic field. Together, these show that accelerating charges produce propagating EM waves.
EM wave generation: An oscillating (or accelerating) electric charge creates a time-varying electric field, which (by displacement current) creates a changing magnetic field. These two time-varying fields propagate together as a transverse wave perpendicular to each other and to the direction of travel.
Speed of light: Maxwell showed c = 1/√(ε₀μ₀) ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s. This theoretical prediction, matching the measured speed of light, provided evidence that light is an EM wave. All EM waves (radio, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-rays, gamma rays) travel at the same speed in vacuum.
EM spectrum: Classified by frequency/wavelength: radio (low frequency), microwaves, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-rays, gamma rays (high frequency). Different parts interact with matter differently.
Key Terms & Definitions
Displacement current (förskjutningsström): The effect of a changing electric field; acts like current in creating magnetic field.
Electromagnetic wave (elektromagnetisk våg): Transverse wave combining oscillating E and B fields, perpendicular to each other and direction of travel.
Speed of light, c (ljushastighet): 3×10⁸ m/s in vacuum. All EM waves travel at this speed in vacuum.
Wavelength and frequency: For all EM waves: c = fλ. Higher frequency → shorter wavelength.
Polarization (polarisering): Direction of the oscillating electric field. Can be linear, circular, or elliptical.
Key Formulas

Exam Focus
Understand that light is an EM wave: oscillating E and B fields perpendicular to each other and to propagation direction.
Use c = fλ to relate frequency and wavelength for any EM wave (radio, visible, X-ray, etc.).
Sketch an EM wave: E oscillating in one plane, B perpendicular to it, both perpendicular to direction of propagation.
Discuss evidence for EM waves: Maxwell's prediction of c from ε₀ and μ₀ matched measured light speed, supporting wave nature of light.

Atomfysik — Atomic Physics
▸
Photons & Photoelectric Effect
Photon energy E = hf. Einstein's photoelectric equation. Work function, kinetic energy of electrons, stopping potential.
▸
Key Concepts
Photons: Light and EM radiation consist of discrete packets of energy called photons. Energy of a photon: E = hf = hc/λ, where h is Planck's constant (6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s). Higher frequency (shorter wavelength) → higher energy photon.
Photoelectric effect: When light shines on a metal surface, electrons are ejected if the photon energy exceeds a threshold. The process is instantaneous and doesn't depend on light intensity (brightness), only on frequency — supporting the photon picture.
Einstein's photoelectric equation: E_photon = W + KE_max, where W is the work function (minimum energy to release an electron) and KE_max is the maximum kinetic energy of ejected electrons. Rearranging: KE_max = hf − W. Higher frequency → higher KE of electrons.
Threshold frequency: f_threshold = W/h. Below this frequency, no electrons are ejected, no matter how intense the light. This was unexplained by classical wave theory but natural in the photon model.
Stopping potential: The retarding potential difference that just stops the most energetic electrons: e·V_s = KE_max, so KE_max = eV_s. A graph of stopping potential vs. frequency is linear with slope h/e.
Key Terms & Definitions
Photon (foton): A quantum of EM radiation with energy E = hf.
Work function, W (arbetsfunktion): Minimum energy needed to release an electron from a metal surface. Unit: joules (J) or electron volts (eV).
Kinetic energy (rörelseenergi): Energy of motion. For electrons: KE = ½mv².
Stopping potential, V_s (stopppotential): Potential that decelerates the most energetic electrons to zero velocity.
Planck's constant, h (Plancks konstant): 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s. Fundamental constant relating energy and frequency.
Key Formulas

Bohr Model & Atomic Spectra
Electron energy levels. Transitions and photon emission/absorption. Series of lines: Balmer (visible), Lyman (UV), Paschen (IR).
▸
Key Concepts
Bohr model: Niels Bohr proposed that electrons orbit at specific energy levels (quantum numbers n = 1, 2, 3, ...). Electrons in hydrogen: E_n = −13.6 eV / n². Ground state (n=1): E₁ = −13.6 eV. Excited states are less negative (higher energy). Electrons don't radiate continuously; they jump between levels.
Energy transitions: An electron can absorb a photon and jump to a higher level (excited state) or emit a photon and fall to a lower level. Energy of photon: hf = |ΔE| = |E_final − E_initial|. Only specific energies are absorbed/emitted (discrete spectra).
Spectral series: Different series correspond to transitions ending at different levels: Lyman series (n_final = 1, transitions from n ≥ 2; UV), Balmer series (n_final = 2, transitions from n ≥ 3; visible), Paschen series (n_final = 3, transitions from n ≥ 4; IR).
Ionization energy: Energy needed to remove an electron from ground state to infinity (n = ∞). For hydrogen: 13.6 eV. Matches energy needed to excite from n=1 to n=∞.
Absorption and emission spectra: White light through a cool gas → continuous spectrum with dark absorption lines (atoms absorb specific frequencies). Hot gas → emission spectrum with bright lines on dark background (atoms emit after excitation). The lines match exactly.
Key Terms & Definitions
Quantum number, n (kvanttal): Integer (1, 2, 3, ...) labeling electron energy levels in an atom.
Energy level (energinivå): Allowed discrete energy value for an electron in an atom.
Ground state (grundtillstånd): Lowest energy state (n=1). Most stable; electrons prefer this.
Excited state (exciterat tillstånd): Higher energy state (n ≥ 2). Unstable; electron falls to lower level, emitting a photon.
Ionization energy (joniseringsenergi): Energy to remove an electron completely (n → ∞).
Key Formulas

Energy of electron at level n
h · f = ΔE = E_final − E_initial
Photon energy from energy transition
f = (13.6 eV / h) · (1/n₁² − 1/n₂²)
Frequency of emitted/absorbed photon (hydrogen)
Ionization energy = 13.6 eV (hydrogen, n=1)
Energy to remove electron to infinity

Quantum Numbers & Orbitals
Principal (n), orbital angular momentum (l), magnetic (m_l), spin (m_s). Shapes of s, p, d orbitals. Pauli exclusion principle.
▸
Key Concepts
Quantum numbers: Four quantum numbers fully describe an electron's state: (1) Principal quantum number n (1, 2, 3, ...) → energy level and distance from nucleus. (2) Orbital angular momentum quantum number l (0 to n−1; 0=s, 1=p, 2=d, 3=f, ...) → shape of orbital. (3) Magnetic quantum number m_l (−l to +l) → orientation of orbital in space. (4) Spin quantum number m_s (±½) → intrinsic angular momentum (spin-up or spin-down).
Orbital shapes: s-orbitals are spherical. p-orbitals are dumbbell-shaped (three per subshell: p_x, p_y, p_z). d-orbitals are more complex four-lobed shapes (five per subshell). f-orbitals are even more complex (seven per subshell). The shape reflects probability distribution of finding electron — not a classical orbit.
Subshells and capacity: Each subshell (n,l) can hold 2(2l+1) electrons. s-subshell: 2 electrons. p-subshell: 6 electrons. d-subshell: 10 electrons. f-subshell: 14 electrons.
Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers. Consequence: each orbital (defined by n, l, m_l) can hold at most 2 electrons (with opposite spins). This principle explains electron configurations and chemical properties.
Electron configuration: Written as 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p¹, etc. Describes how electrons fill orbitals. Follows Aufbau principle (filling lowest energy first) and Hund's rule (maximize parallel spins before pairing). Determines chemical behavior.
Key Terms & Definitions
Principal quantum number, n (huvudkvanttal): n = 1, 2, 3, ... Determines energy level and shell.
Orbital angular momentum, l (orbitalrörelsemängdsmoment): l = 0, 1, 2, 3, ... for s, p, d, f orbitals. Related to shape of orbital.
Magnetic quantum number, m_l (magnetisk kvanttal): m_l = −l to +l. Determines orientation in space (e.g., three p-orbitals: m_l = −1, 0, +1).
Spin quantum number, m_s (spinkvanttal): m_s = ±½. Intrinsic angular momentum; two values per orbital (spin-up and spin-down).
Orbital (orbital): Quantum state of one electron, defined by n, l, m_l. Graphically, a region where electron is likely found.

Nuclear Structure & Binding Energy
Protons, neutrons, isotopes. Mass defect and binding energy. Nuclear stability. Semi-empirical mass formula.
▸
Key Concepts
Nuclear composition: Nucleus contains protons (charge +e, mass ≈ 1 u) and neutrons (charge 0, mass ≈ 1 u). Atomic number Z = number of protons (defines element). Mass number A = Z + N (number of nucleons). Isotopes are atoms of same element with different N (same Z, different A).
Mass defect: The mass of a nucleus is slightly less than the sum of its constituent nucleons (protons + neutrons). The missing mass Δm = Z·m_p + N·m_n − m_nucleus is converted to binding energy B.E. = Δm·c².
Binding energy per nucleon: B.E. / A reflects nuclear stability. Plot of B.E./A vs. A shows: light nuclei and very heavy nuclei have lower B.E./A (less stable); nuclei near iron-56 have maximum B.E./A (most stable). This explains why fusion (combining light nuclei) and fission (splitting heavy nuclei) both release energy.
Strong nuclear force: Attractive force between nucleons, acts at very short range (~1 fm). Overcomes proton-proton electrostatic repulsion in nucleus. Not fully understood (unlike EM and gravity), but well-described by nuclear models.
Nuclear stability: For light nuclei (A < 20), stable nuclei have N ≈ Z. For heavier nuclei, stable nuclei have N > Z (extra neutrons stabilize against proton repulsion). Beyond bismuth-209, all nuclei are radioactive.
Key Terms & Definitions
Atomic number, Z (atomnummer): Number of protons. Defines element.
Mass number, A (masstall): Total nucleons (protons + neutrons). Z + N.
Nucleon (nukleon): Generic term for proton or neutron.
Isotope (isotop): Atoms of same element (same Z) with different mass number (different N).
Binding energy (bindningsenergi): Energy released when nucleons bind together. B.E. = Δm·c².
Key Formulas

Identify stable vs. unstable nuclei: light nuclei stable with N≈Z; heavy nuclei require N > Z. Bismuth-209 is heaviest stable nucleus.
Calculate Q-value (energy released) in nuclear reactions using mass-energy equivalence.
Radioactivity & Decay Modes
Alpha, beta, gamma decay. Decay equations. Half-life. Activity and decay constant.
▸
Key Concepts
Radioactivity: Spontaneous emission of particles/radiation from unstable nuclei to reach a more stable state. Three main types: alpha (α), beta (β), and gamma (γ) decay. Radioactive decay is random (probabilistic) and exponential.
Alpha decay: Nucleus emits an alpha particle (⁴He nucleus: 2 protons + 2 neutrons). Parent nucleus loses 2 protons and 2 neutrons: A → A−4, Z → Z−2. Example: ²³⁸U → ²³⁴Th + ⁴He. Alpha particles are highly ionizing but low range.
Beta-minus (β⁻) decay: A neutron converts to proton, electron, and antineutrino. Parent nucleus: A → A, Z → Z+1. Example: ¹⁴C → ¹⁴N + e⁻ + ν̄. Beta particles are electrons; moderately ionizing, longer range than alpha.
Beta-plus (β⁺) decay: A proton converts to neutron, positron, and neutrino. A → A, Z → Z−1. Example: ¹¹C → ¹¹B + e⁺ + ν. Less common than β⁻.
Gamma decay: Nucleus in excited state emits a gamma ray (high-energy photon) and drops to lower energy state. A and Z unchanged. Often accompanies alpha or beta decay. Gamma rays are highly penetrating.
Half-life, T₁/₂: Time for half of a sample to decay. After one half-life, 50% remains; after two, 25%; after three, 12.5%, etc. N(t) = N₀ · (½)^(t/T₁/₂) = N₀ · e^(−λt), where λ = ln(2)/T₁/₂ is decay constant. Half-life is independent of sample size and temperature.
Key Terms & Definitions
Alpha particle (alfapartikel): Helium-4 nucleus: ⁴He or ⁴₂He. Emitted in alpha decay.
Beta particle (betapartikel): Electron (β⁻) or positron (β⁺) emitted in beta decay.
Gamma ray (gammastrålning): High-energy photon emitted by excited nucleus.
Half-life, T₁/₂ (halveringstid): Time for radioactive sample to decay to half its original amount.
Decay constant, λ (sönderfallskonstant): Probability of decay per unit time. λ = ln(2)/T₁/₂.

Fission & Fusion
Nuclear fission: chain reactions, critical mass. Nuclear fusion: conditions, energy release. Applications in power generation.
▸
Key Concepts
Nuclear fission: Heavy nucleus (A > 230) splits into two smaller fragments, releasing energy and 2–3 neutrons. Example: ²³⁵U + n → ²³⁶U* → ⁹²Kr + ¹⁴¹Ba + 3n + energy. Energy released: typically 200 MeV per fission, due to binding energy increase (fragments have higher B.E./A than original).
Chain reaction: One fission releases neutrons that cause more fissions. If each fission triggers ≥1 additional fission on average, reaction is self-sustaining (critical). If <1 → reaction dies out. If >1 → exponential growth (nuclear explosion). Control rods absorb neutrons to maintain steady state (nuclear reactor).
Critical mass: Minimum mass of fissile material (e.g., U-235) needed for chain reaction. Depends on material shape and purity. For bare sphere: ~10 kg of U-235, ~15 kg of Pu-239.
Nuclear fusion: Two light nuclei combine to form heavier nucleus, releasing energy. Example: ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n + 17.6 MeV. Requires extreme temperature/pressure to overcome Coulomb barrier. Occurs naturally in stars (powers the sun). Controlled fusion in reactors (ITER, etc.) not yet achieved commercially.
Fusion vs. fission: Both release energy because products have higher B.E./A. Fusion: light → medium (higher B.E./A). Fission: heavy → medium. Fusion produces less radioactive waste and is theoretically cleaner, but is harder to control. Fission is commercially viable now.
Key Terms & Definitions
Fission (kärnklyvning): Splitting of heavy nucleus into two fragments.
Fusion (kärnfusion): Combination of two light nuclei into one heavier nucleus.
Chain reaction (kedjereaktion): Self-sustaining sequence where neutrons from one fission trigger more fissions.
Critical mass (kritisk massa): Minimum mass for self-sustaining chain reaction.
Coulomb barrier (Coulombbarriär): Electrostatic repulsion between positively charged nuclei, must be overcome for fusion.
Key Formulas
Q-value = (M_initial − M_final) · c²
Energy released in nuclear reaction
For fission: Q ≈ 200 MeV per ²³⁵U nucleus
For fusion (e.g., deuterium-tritium): Q ≈ 17.6 MeV
Power output = number of reactions per second × energy per reaction

Explain chain reactions: one fission triggers others. Control requires k (multiplication factor) = 1 (critical). k > 1 → exponential growth.
Compare fission and fusion: both release energy, but from opposite ends of B.E./A curve. Fusion more difficult but cleaner (theoretically).
Discuss applications: fission in nuclear power plants, fusion in stars and research reactors. Address safety and waste concerns.

Stellar Evolution & The HR Diagram
Hertzsprung-Russell diagram: luminosity vs. temperature. Main sequence. Red giants, white dwarfs, supergiants. Stellar lifecycles.
▸
Key Concepts
Hertzsprung-Russell (HR) diagram: Plot of stellar luminosity (brightness) vs. surface temperature (or spectral type). Most stars lie on the main sequence (diagonal band), fusing hydrogen in their cores. Upper right: cool, bright giants/supergiants. Lower left: hot, dim white dwarfs.
Main sequence: Longest phase of a star's life. Hydrostatic equilibrium: inward gravity balanced by outward pressure from fusion in core. Massive stars burn fuel faster → shorter main sequence life. Sun has ~10 billion year main sequence; will last ~5 billion more.
Red giants: Star exhausts core hydrogen, begins fusing hydrogen in shell around helium core. Expands and cools (moves up and right on HR diagram). Example: Betelgeuse. Later fuses helium in core → asymptotic giant branch (AGB).
White dwarfs: Remnant of low/medium-mass star after losing outer layers. Hot, Earth-sized, composed of degenerate matter (electrons packed so tightly they resist further compression via Pauli principle). No longer fusing. Slowly cools to become black dwarf (theoretical; universe too young for any yet).
Supergiants and neutron stars: Massive stars (M > 8 M_sun) end as supergiants, then core collapse. Either explode as supernova (leaving neutron star or black hole) or form black hole directly.
Key Terms & Definitions
Luminosity, L (luminositet): Total power radiated by a star. Unit: watts (W) or solar luminosities (L_sun ≈ 3.8 × 10²⁶ W).
Spectral type (spektralklass): Classification by temperature: O (hottest, blue), B, A, F, G, K, M (coolest, red). Sun is G-type (yellow).
Stefan-Boltzmann Law: L = 4πR² σT⁴, where R is radius, T is surface temperature, σ is Stefan-Boltzmann constant (5.67×10⁻⁸ W/(m²K⁴)). Hotter and larger stars are brighter.
Hydrostatic equilibrium (hydrostatisk jämvikt): Balance of inward gravity and outward pressure (thermal, radiation, degeneracy). Determines star's structure and stability.
White dwarf (vit dvärg): Dense, Earth-sized stellar remnant. Supported by electron degeneracy pressure, not fusion.
Key Formulas

Stefan-Boltzmann Law for luminosity
Luminosity ∝ M³ (on main sequence)
Mass-luminosity relation: more massive stars are brighter
Lifetime ∝ M / L ∝ M⁻² (main sequence)
Massive stars burn fuel faster and die sooner

Explain stellar lifecycle: main sequence → red giant → white dwarf (low/medium mass) or supernova → neutron star (high mass).
Cosmology & The Expanding Universe
Big Bang theory. Hubble expansion. Cosmic microwave background (CMB). Dark matter and dark energy. Age of universe.
▸
Key Concepts
Hubble expansion: Galaxies recede from us with velocities proportional to distance: v = H₀ · d, where H₀ ≈ 70 km/s/Mpc is the Hubble constant. Implies the universe is expanding and was once smaller and hotter (Big Bang).
Big Bang theory: The universe began as an infinitely hot, dense point (~13.8 billion years ago) and has been expanding ever since. Supported by: (1) Hubble expansion, (2) Cosmic microwave background radiation (ancient light from early universe), (3) abundance of light elements (¹H, ⁴He, etc.).
Cosmic microwave background (CMB): Relic radiation from ~380,000 years after Big Bang, when universe cooled enough for atoms to form (recombination). Temperature ~2.7 K. Remarkably uniform (isotropic), with tiny fluctuations that grew into galaxies. COBE/WMAP/Planck satellites mapped it precisely.
Dark matter: Invisible matter comprising ~85% of universe's matter (only ~15% is ordinary baryonic matter). Evidence: galaxy rotation curves (too fast without extra mass), gravitational lensing, CMB patterns. Nature unknown; candidates include WIMPs, axions.
Dark energy: Mysterious component causing accelerated expansion (~68% of universe's total energy density). Discovered in 1998 via observations of distant supernovae. Physical nature unknown; perhaps cosmological constant (Λ) or scalar field. Dominates late-time universe.
Key Terms & Definitions
Hubble constant, H₀ (Hubbles konstant): Rate of expansion: v = H₀ · d. Unit: km/s/Mpc. Current value ~70 km/s/Mpc.
Redshift, z (rödförskjutning): For receding objects, light is redshifted: z = Δλ/λ. At low velocities, z ≈ v/c.
Cosmic microwave background (kosmisk mikrovågsbakgrund): Ancient thermal radiation from early universe, now ~2.7 K.
Dark matter (mörk materia): Non-luminous matter making up ~85% of matter.
Dark energy (mörk energi): Component causing accelerated expansion (~68% of total energy density).
