Carbon Chemistry Fundamentals
Hybridisation (sp³, sp², sp), chain structures, functional groups, structural and molecular formulas.
▸
Key Concepts
Hybridisation: sp³ (tetrahedral, 109.5°), sp² (trigonal planar, 120°), and sp (linear, 180°). Determines molecular geometry and chemical properties. sp³ single bonds, sp² double bonds, sp triple bonds.
Carbon chains: Straight-chain (alkan), branched-chain (isoalkanes), and cyclic structures. Chain length and branching affect boiling point, melting point, and solubility.
Functional groups: Characteristic atom groups that define reactivity (e.g., –OH hydroxyl, –C=O carbonyl, –COOH carboxyl, –NH₂ amino). Same functional group → similar chemical behaviour regardless of rest of molecule.
Isomerism origin: Carbon's ability to form four bonds and chain flexibly creates multiple arrangements (isomers) with same molecular formula but different properties.
Key Terms & Definitions
Hybridisation (hybridisering): Mixing of atomic orbitals to form new hybrid orbitals with specific geometry.
Functional group (funktionell grupp): An atom or group of atoms responsible for the characteristic reactions of an organic compound.
Structural isomer (strukturisomer): Molecules with the same molecular formula but different connectivity (different structures).
Saturated hydrocarbon (mättad kolväte): Contains only C–C and C–H single bonds (alkanes).
Unsaturated hydrocarbon (omättad kolväte): Contains C=C double bonds (alkenes) or C≡C triple bonds (alkynes).
Key Formulas
CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
Alkanes (saturated acyclic)
CₙH₂ₙ
Alkenes or cycloalkanes
CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
Alkynes or dienes
Exam Focus
Identify hybridisation state of each carbon in a structure — predict geometry and reactivity.
Draw and name structural isomers given a molecular formula.
Recognise functional groups in complex molecules and predict reactivity.

Förklara vad som menas med att kol kan bilda fyra kovalenta bindningar. Varför är kol unikt i sin förmåga att bilda långa kedjor?

Fyra bindningar: Kol har 4 valenselektroner och kan bilda 4 kovalenta bindningar (enkla, dubbla eller trippla). [1]
Kedjor: C-C-bindningen är stark (346 kJ/mol) och stabil. Kol kan binda till sig själv i långa kedjor, grenade kedjor och ringar — detta kallas katenering. Inget annat grundämne gör detta lika effektivt. [1]
Vanligt fel: Att säga att kol har 4 elektroner totalt. Kol har 6 elektroner totalt men 4 valenselektroner.
IUPAC Nomenclature & Naming
Systematic naming of alkanes, alkenes, alkynes, alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids. Swedish convention (IUPAC).
▸
Key Concepts
IUPAC naming rules: (1) Identify the longest carbon chain (parent name). (2) Number the chain to give functional groups lowest numbers. (3) Name and number substituents. (4) Arrange substituents alphabetically in the name.
Prefixes: Indicate position and number of substituents: di-, tri-, tetra-, penta-, etc. Alphabetise by substituent name (not prefix).
Suffixes for functional groups: –ane (alkane), –en (alkene), –yn (alkyne), –ol (alcohol), –al (aldehyde), –one (ketone), –oic acid (carboxylic acid).
Numbering priority: Functional groups take precedence (carboxylic acid > aldehyde > ketone > alcohol > amine > alkene/alkyne). Number to give these groups the lowest numbers.
Key Terms & Definitions
Parent chain (huvudkedja): The longest continuous carbon chain that includes the principal functional group.
Substituent (substitutent): An atom or group replacing a hydrogen on the parent chain (e.g., methyl, ethyl, chloro).
Principal functional group (huvudfunktionell grupp): The most reactive group, which determines the suffix and numbering priority.
IUPAC: International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry — system for systematic chemical nomenclature.
Exam Focus
Name organic molecules from structural formulas using correct IUPAC rules.
Draw structural formulas from IUPAC names — test understanding of nomenclature priorities.
Identify errors in incorrect IUPAC names and correct them.

Alkanes, Alkenes, Alkynes
Saturated vs unsaturated hydrocarbons. Structure, properties, combustion, addition and substitution reactions.
▸
Key Concepts
Alkanes (alkaner): CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Single C–C bonds only. Non-polar, hydrophobic. Boiling point increases with chain length. Combustion: CₓHᵧ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O. Substitution reactions with halogens (free-radical mechanism).
Alkenes (alkener): CₙH₂ₙ. C=C double bond (sp² carbons). Polar, more reactive than alkanes. Addition reactions across the double bond (electrophilic addition): H₂, X₂, HX, H₂O. Markovnikov's rule: H adds to C with more H atoms already attached.
Alkynes (alkyner): CₙH₂ₙ₋₂. C≡C triple bond (sp carbons). Even more reactive. Addition reactions (two equivalents can add). Can undergo coupling reactions. Less common in Kemi 2, but important for completeness.
Branching effect: Branching lowers boiling point compared to straight-chain isomers (less surface contact). Increases stability of carbocations (tertiary > secondary > primary).
Key Terms & Definitions
Saturated (mättad): All available bonds filled with atoms (typically H); only single bonds.
Unsaturated (omättad): Contains C=C or C≡C bonds; can undergo addition reactions.
Combustion (förbränning): Complete oxidation in oxygen producing CO₂ and H₂O; exothermic.
Addition reaction (additionsreaktion): Two molecules combine to form one product; occurs across double/triple bonds.
Substitution reaction (substitutionsreaktion): One atom/group replaces another; typical of saturated hydrocarbons.
Key Formulas
CₓHᵧ + (x + y/4)O₂ → xCO₂ + (y/2)H₂O
General combustion equation
Exam Focus
Balance combustion equations for hydrocarbons.
Write equations for addition of H₂, Br₂, HBr, H₂O to alkenes (apply Markovnikov's rule).
Distinguish between addition and substitution reactions in terms of mechanism and conditions.

Alcohols, Aldehydes, Ketones, Carboxylic Acids
Functional groups with oxygen: structure, nomenclature, preparation, oxidation reactions, properties.
▸
Key Concepts
Alcohols (alkoholer, –OH): Hydroxyl group bonded to sp³ carbon. Primary (1°), secondary (2°), tertiary (3°) based on substitution. H-bonding → higher boiling points, soluble in water. Oxidation: primary → aldehyde → carboxylic acid; secondary → ketone; tertiary → no oxidation.
Aldehydes (aldehyder, –CHO): Carbonyl at chain end. Product of primary alcohol oxidation. Readily oxidised to carboxylic acid. Reduced back to primary alcohol by LiAlH₄ or NaBH₄. React with nucleophiles (addition).
Ketones (ketoner, –CO–): Carbonyl group internal (not at chain end). From secondary alcohol oxidation. Resistant to further oxidation (no strong oxidising agent breaks C–C bonds adjacent to C=O). Undergo nucleophilic addition and condensation reactions.
Carboxylic acids (karboksylsyror, –COOH): Highest oxidation state for organic compounds. Weak acids (partially ionised in water). React with alcohols (esterification) and bases (salt formation). Decarboxylation possible under strong heating.
Key Terms & Definitions
Hydroxyl group (hydroxylgrupp): –OH functional group; indicates alcohol.
Carbonyl (karbonyl): C=O functional group; found in aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, esters.
Oxidation state (oxidationstillstånd): Reflects degree of oxidation; alcohols → aldehydes/ketones → carboxylic acids (increasing oxidation).
Nucleophilic addition (nukleofil addition): Attack by electron-rich nucleophile on electron-poor carbonyl carbon.
Key Formulas
RCH₂OH + [O] → RCHO + H₂O
Primary alcohol → aldehyde (mild oxidation)
RCHO + [O] → RCOOH
Aldehyde → carboxylic acid (strong oxidation)
R₂CHOH + [O] → R₂CO
Secondary alcohol → ketone
RCOOH + R'OH ⇌ RCOOR' + H₂O
Esterification (Fischer esterification, reversible)
Exam Focus
Classify alcohols as 1°, 2°, or 3° and predict oxidation products.
Write equations for oxidation of alcohols using [O] notation or specific reagents (K₂Cr₂O₇, KMnO₄).
Esterification reactions: balance equations and understand reversibility, conditions (acid catalyst, heat).

Organic Reaction Types
Substitution, addition, elimination, condensation, and oxidation mechanisms and examples.
▸
Key Concepts
Substitution (substitution): One group replaces another. SN1 (unimolecular, carbocation intermediate, favoured by 3° and polar solvents) or SN2 (bimolecular, backside attack, inversion, favoured by 1° and aprotic solvents). Alkanes: free-radical substitution (Cl₂ or Br₂ under UV).
Addition (addition): Two groups add across a double or triple bond, reducing unsaturation. Electrophilic addition (alkenes + HX, H₂O, X₂). Nucleophilic addition (carbonyl + CN⁻, NH₃, etc.).
Elimination (elimination): Removal of a small molecule (H₂O, HX) from adjacent carbons, creating a double bond. E1 (unimolecular, carbocation) or E2 (bimolecular, concerted). Often competes with substitution.
Condensation (kondensation): Two molecules join with loss of a small molecule (usually H₂O). Esterification, amidation, and polymerisation are condensation reactions.
Oxidation (oxidation): Loss of electrons (or H, or gain of O). Alcohols → carbonyl → carboxylic acid. Alkenes → epoxides or diols. Alkanes → less common.
Key Terms & Definitions
SN1: Unimolecular nucleophilic substitution; two-step with carbocation intermediate; first-order kinetics.
SN2: Bimolecular nucleophilic substitution; one-step backside attack; second-order kinetics; inversion of configuration.
Carbocation (karbokatjon): Positively charged carbon with six electrons (sp² hybridised). Stability: 3° > 2° > 1° > methyl.
Elimination: Removal of a group from adjacent atoms, creating a multiple bond.
Free radical (fri radikal): Species with unpaired electron; formed by homolytic cleavage (e.g., Cl•).
Exam Focus
Predict products of SN1 vs SN2 based on alkyl halide type, nucleophile, and solvent.
Write mechanisms (arrow-pushing) for simple substitution and elimination reactions.
Explain why certain conditions favour certain reaction types (e.g., heat favours elimination).

Polymers & Plastics
Addition polymers (monomers, chains), condensation polymers, polymerisation reactions, properties, environmental impact.
▸
Key Concepts
Addition polymers (additionspolymerer): Formed by joining monomers (e.g., alkenes) without loss of atoms. Examples: polyethylene (PE), polypropylene (PP), polyvinyl chloride (PVC). Monomer units repeat in backbone; double bonds convert to single bonds during polymerisation.
Condensation polymers (kondensationspolymerer): Formed by joining monomers with loss of a small molecule (H₂O). Examples: polyesters (PET), polyamides (nylon), polyurethanes. Functional groups (–OH, –COOH, –NH₂) on monomers interact to form linking bonds.
Cross-linking and properties: Linear polymers are flexible (low melting point, soluble). Cross-linked polymers are rigid, durable, insoluble. Heat and pressure affect properties. Branching affects density and properties.
Environmental impact: Most synthetic plastics are non-biodegradable, persist in environment for centuries. Burning releases CO₂ (greenhouse gas) and toxic fumes. Recycling (melting, re-forming) extends useful life but degrades polymer chains. Bioplastics (from plant material) are biodegradable alternatives.
Key Terms & Definitions
Monomer (monomer): A small molecule that can join with others to form a polymer.
Polymer (polymer): A large molecule made by joining many monomers; has repeating units.
Polymerisation (polymerisation): Chemical process of linking monomers to form polymers.
Cross-link (tvärning): Chemical bonds between polymer chains, increasing rigidity and strength.
Biodegradable: Capable of being broken down by biological processes (e.g., bacteria, enzymes).
Exam Focus
Write polymerisation equations for alkene monomers (show repeating unit in brackets with subscript n).
Explain the difference between addition and condensation polymerisation with examples.
Discuss properties (melting point, solubility, flexibility) in relation to polymer structure (linear, cross-linked, branched).
Evaluate environmental impacts of plastics and the merits of recycling vs bioplastics.

Isomerism
Structural isomers, geometric (cis-trans) isomers, stereoisomers, optical activity.
▸
Key Concepts
Structural isomers (strukturisomerer): Same molecular formula but different connectivity. Examples: butane vs 2-methylpropane (isobutane); propanol (1° alcohol) vs 2-propanol (2° alcohol). Different chemical and physical properties.
Geometric isomers (geometriska isomerer): Non-rotating double bonds or ring junctions prevent free rotation. Cis (same side) vs trans (opposite sides) configurations for C=C bonds. Example: but-2-ene (cis and trans forms). Different melting points, boiling points, and chemical reactivity.
Stereoisomers (stereoisomerer): Broader term: includes geometric isomers and optical isomers. Identical connectivity but different 3D arrangement.
Optical isomers (optiska isomerer): Arise from chiral carbon atoms (4 different groups attached). D/L or R/S notation. Rotate plane-polarised light in opposite directions (enantiomers). Important in biochemistry and pharmaceuticals.
Key Terms & Definitions
Isomer (isomer): Molecule with same molecular formula but different structure or arrangement.
Chiral (kiral): A carbon with four different groups (asymmetric); has no plane of symmetry.
Enantiomer (enantiomer): Pair of optical isomers that are non-superimposable mirror images.
Cis-trans (cis-trans): Geometric isomerism across a double bond (cis = same side; trans = opposite sides).
Plane-polarised light (planpolariserat ljus): Light vibrating in one plane; rotated by optically active compounds.
Exam Focus
Identify structural isomers given a molecular formula — draw and name each.
Draw and label cis and trans isomers of alkenes.
Identify chiral carbons and explain why optical isomerism arises.
Predict properties (boiling point, reactivity) differences between isomers.

Proteins & Amino Acids
20 amino acids, peptide bonds, primary/secondary/tertiary/quaternary structure, enzymes as proteins.
▸
Key Concepts
Amino acids: 20 standard amino acids (in living systems). All have amino (–NH₂) and carboxyl (–COOH) groups attached to the same carbon (α-carbon). The R-group (side chain) varies: polar, non-polar, charged. Solubility, reactivity, and interactions depend on R-group.
Peptide bond (peptidösa bindning): Condensation reaction between –COOH of one amino acid and –NH₂ of another, forming –CO–NH– link with loss of H₂O. Directionality: N-terminus (free –NH₂) to C-terminus (free –COOH).
Primary structure (primärstruktur): Sequence of amino acids in the polypeptide chain. Determined by genetic code. Any change in sequence can drastically alter protein function (e.g., sickle cell disease from one amino acid change).
Secondary structure (sekundärstruktur): Regular, repeating local structures stabilised by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms. Common types: α-helix (coiled spring), β-sheet (extended, zigzag), random coil.
Tertiary structure (tertiärstruktur): Overall 3D shape of single polypeptide chain. Stabilised by interactions between R-groups: hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, disulfide bridges (–S–S–), hydrophobic interactions, van der Waals forces.
Quaternary structure (kvaternärstruktur): Only for multi-subunit proteins. Interactions between separate polypeptide chains. Example: haemoglobin (4 subunits). Crucial for cooperative binding and function.
Key Terms & Definitions
Amino acid (aminosyra): Organic compound with amino and carboxyl functional groups; monomers of proteins.
Peptide bond (peptidösa bindning): C–N bond formed between carboxyl of one amino acid and amino of the next.
Polypeptide: Chain of amino acids linked by peptide bonds (can also be called protein if folded and functional).
Denaturalisation (denaturering): Disruption of protein structure (secondary, tertiary, quaternary) by heat, pH, or chemicals; loses function.
Enzyme (enzym): Protein catalyst that speeds up biological reactions by lowering activation energy.
Exam Focus
Draw a peptide bond formation between two amino acids; label the bond and identify the new N-terminus and C-terminus.
Explain the different levels of protein structure and the forces stabilising each.
Predict the effect of denaturation (heat, pH, chemical) on protein function.
Relate amino acid structure (R-group polarity) to protein folding and function.

Carbohydrates
Monosaccharides, disaccharides, polysaccharides, glycosidic bonds, structural and functional roles.
▸
Key Concepts
Monosaccharides (monosackarider): Simple sugars (C, H, O in ratio roughly 1:2:1). Glucose (6-carbon, hexose), fructose (isomer of glucose), ribose (5-carbon, pentose). Exist as linear form (aldehyde group) or cyclic forms (hemiacetal, most stable in aqueous solution). Ring forms (α and β anomers) differ in configuration at anomeric carbon.
Disaccharides (disackarider): Two monosaccharide units joined by glycosidic bond. Examples: sucrose (glucose + fructose), lactose (glucose + galactose), maltose (glucose + glucose). Glycosidic bond formation is condensation reaction; hydrolysis reverses it.
Polysaccharides (polysackarider): Many monosaccharide units joined. Starch (plants, storage, linear amylose and branched amylopectin), glycogen (animals, storage, highly branched), cellulose (structural, rigidity). Different branching and linkage types (α vs β) affect digestibility and properties.
Glycosidic bonds (glykosidiska bindningar): C–O–C linkages between monosaccharides. α-linkages (e.g., in starch) can be hydrolysed by human enzymes (amylase); β-linkages (e.g., in cellulose) cannot (no cellulase in humans).
Key Terms & Definitions
Monosaccharide (monosackärid): Simple sugar; single polyhydroxy aldehyde or ketone.
Reducing sugar (reducerande sockrar): Contains free aldehyde or ketone group; can be oxidised (tests positive with Fehling's or Benedict's reagent).
Glycosidic linkage (glykosidisk bindning): Bond between atoms of two monosaccharides.
Hydrolysis (hydrolys): Reaction with water that breaks bonds (e.g., polysaccharides → monosaccharides).
Exam Focus
Draw a disaccharide formation (condensation) showing glycosidic bond formation and the water molecule released.
Explain why starch is digestible but cellulose is not, in terms of enzyme specificity and glycosidic bond types.
Compare properties of starch, glycogen, and cellulose in relation to their structure and biological roles.
Predict products of hydrolysis of di- or polysaccharides.

Lipids
Fatty acids, triglycerides, saturated vs unsaturated, phospholipids, membranes, properties.
▸
Key Concepts
Fatty acids (fettsyror): Long hydrocarbon chains (typically 16–18 carbons) with –COOH at end. Saturated (single bonds, straight chain, solid at room temp, e.g., palmitic acid) vs unsaturated (C=C double bonds, kinked shape, liquid at room temp, e.g., oleic acid). Double bond configuration (cis, more kinked) vs trans (more straight, less natural).
Triglycerides (triacylglycerols): Glycerol + 3 fatty acids via ester linkages. Energy storage (fats and oils). Saturated fats are more stable but associated with health risks; unsaturated fats are healthier. Hydrolysis produces glycerol + fatty acids.
Phospholipids (fosfolipider): Glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate group. Amphipathic: hydrophobic tail (fatty acids) and hydrophilic head (phosphate). Self-assemble into bilayers in water (cell membrane structure). Head faces aqueous environment, tails face each other.
Cell membrane structure: Phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins. Hydrophobic barrier prevents passage of polar molecules (ions, glucose). Selective permeability via transport proteins. Fluidity affected by cholesterol and degree of unsaturation in fatty acids.
Key Terms & Definitions
Lipid (lipid): Hydrophobic or amphipathic organic compound; includes fats, oils, waxes, phospholipids.
Saturated fatty acid (mättad fettsyra): No C=C double bonds; straight chain; higher melting point.
Unsaturated fatty acid (omättad fettsyra): Contains C=C double bonds; kinked structure; lower melting point; more fluid.
Amphipathic: Molecule with both hydrophobic and hydrophilic regions.
Ester linkage (esterbindning): C–O bond formed between carboxylic acid and alcohol groups.
Exam Focus
Draw the structure of a triglyceride showing ester linkages; explain hydrolysis to glycerol + fatty acids.
Explain phospholipid structure and its role in cell membranes (bilayer formation, selective permeability).
Compare properties of saturated and unsaturated fatty acids; relate structure to health implications.
Explain why phospholipids are amphipathic and how this enables membrane structure.

Beskriv lipiders (fetters) kemiska struktur och förklara skillnaden mellan mättade och omättade fetter.

Struktur: Fetter (triglycerider) bildas genom kondensation av glycerol (3 OH-grupper) med tre fettsyror → tre esterbindningar + 3 H₂O. [1]
Mättade: Bara enkelbindningar i fettsyrakedjan → raka kedjor → kan packas tätt → fast vid rumstemperatur (smör). Omättade: Dubbelbindningar → böjda kedjor → kan inte packas lika tätt → flytande (olja). [1]
Vanligt fel: Att säga att omättade fetter är "ohälsosamma". Omättade fetter (t.ex. olivolja) är generellt hälsosammare än mättade.
Nucleic Acids & DNA
Nucleotides, base pairing, double helix structure, replication, genetic code basics.
▸
Key Concepts
Nucleotides (nukleotider): Building blocks of DNA and RNA. Three components: nitrogenous base (purine or pyrimidine), pentose sugar (ribose in RNA, deoxyribose in DNA), phosphate group. Connected by phosphodiester bonds (sugar-phosphate backbone).
DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid): Double-stranded molecule. Bases: adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), cytosine (C). Base pairing rules (complementarity): A–T (2 H-bonds), G–C (3 H-bonds). Double helix structure: two antiparallel sugar-phosphate strands twisted around each other.
Complementary base pairing: Enables replication. Template strand directs synthesis of new strand. Each new DNA molecule = original strand + newly synthesised complementary strand (semiconservative replication).
Genetic code: Triplet codons (3 bases) code for amino acids. Redundancy: multiple codons for same amino acid. mRNA carries code from nucleus to ribosomes. tRNA brings correct amino acids. Start codon (AUG), stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA).
Key Terms & Definitions
Nucleotide (nukleotid): Monomer of nucleic acids; contains sugar, phosphate, and nitrogenous base.

Enzymes & Catalysis
Enzyme structure, mechanism, specificity, factors affecting activity (temperature, pH, substrate concentration), inhibition.
▸
Key Concepts
Enzyme (enzym): Biological catalyst (usually protein). Speeds up reaction without being consumed. Enzyme + substrate → enzyme–substrate complex → enzyme + product. Lowers activation energy by stabilising transition state.
Enzyme specificity (enzymspecificitet): Each enzyme catalyses one specific reaction (or type of reaction). Lock-and-key or induced-fit model: active site precisely shaped to bind substrate. Allows metabolism to be controlled and directed.
Temperature effect: Increasing temperature speeds up enzyme activity (more collisions) until denaturation temperature (~37°C for human enzymes). Beyond optimal temp, enzyme denatures and activity drops sharply.
pH effect: Each enzyme has optimal pH (neutral for most, acidic for pepsin, alkaline for trypsin). Changes in pH alter ionisation of active site and can denature protein.
Substrate concentration effect: At low [substrate], rate increases linearly (first-order). At high [substrate], rate plateaus (zero-order) when enzyme is saturated (Vmax). Enzyme concentration is directly proportional to reaction rate.
Inhibition: Competitive inhibition: inhibitor competes with substrate for active site (can be reversed by increasing [substrate]). Non-competitive inhibition: inhibitor binds elsewhere, reducing enzyme activity (cannot be reversed by increasing [substrate]).
Key Terms & Definitions
Active site (aktivt ställe): Pocket in enzyme where substrate binds and reaction occurs.
Substrate (substrat): Molecule that enzyme acts upon.
Cofactor (kofaktor): Non-protein helper (e.g., metal ion, vitamin) required for enzyme activity.
Denaturation (denaturering): Loss of enzyme structure and activity due to heat, pH, or chemical.
Vmax: Maximum velocity; rate when enzyme is saturated with substrate.

Chemical Equilibrium
Dynamic equilibrium, equilibrium constant Kc, Le Chatelier's principle, shifts in equilibrium.
▸
Key Concepts
Dynamic equilibrium (dynamisk jämvikt): Forward and reverse reactions occur simultaneously at equal rates. Macroscopic properties (concentrations, colour, pH) remain constant, but reactions continue at molecular level. Not a static state.
Equilibrium constant (jämviktskonstant), Kc: For reaction aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD, Kc = [C]^c[D]^d / [A]^a[B]^b at equilibrium. Dimensionless (in Kemi 2, assuming concentrations in mol/L). Kc independent of initial concentrations or pressure but depends on temperature. Large Kc (>1) favours products; small Kc (<1) favours reactants.
Le Chatelier's principle (Le Chateliers princip): If a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it shifts to counteract the disturbance. Increasing pressure/decreasing volume: shifts towards side with fewer moles of gas. Increasing temperature: shifts towards endothermic direction. Changing concentrations: shifts to reduce the changed substance.
Effect of catalysts: Catalysts speed up both forward and reverse reactions equally. Do not shift equilibrium position but allow equilibrium to be reached faster. Do not change Kc.
Key Terms & Definitions
Equilibrium (jämvikt): State where forward and reverse reaction rates are equal; macroscopic properties unchanged.
Kc: Equilibrium constant (concentration-based). Characteristic of each reaction at a given temperature.
Heterogeneous equilibrium: Equilibrium involving different phases (gas, liquid, solid). Pure solids and liquids excluded from Kc expression.
Reversible reaction (reversibel reaktion): Reaction that can proceed in both directions.
Key Formulas
Kc = [C]^c[D]^d / [A]^a[B]^b
Equilibrium constant expression (concentration-based)
Kp = (P_C)^c(P_D)^d / (P_A)^a(P_B)^b
Equilibrium constant for gases (partial pressures)

Reaction Rate & Kinetics
Collision theory, activation energy, factors affecting rate, rate equations, catalysts.
▸
Key Concepts
Collision theory (stötteorin): Reaction occurs when molecules collide with sufficient energy (≥ activation energy, Ea) and correct orientation. Reaction rate depends on frequency of collisions, fraction with sufficient energy, and orientation factor.
Activation energy (aktiveringsenegi), Ea: Energy barrier that reactants must overcome to form products. High Ea → slow reaction. Lowering Ea (e.g., with catalyst) increases rate exponentially (Arrhenius equation).
Temperature effect: Increasing temperature increases kinetic energy of molecules and collision frequency. Both effects increase reaction rate. Rule of thumb: 10°C increase → rate doubles (depends on Ea).
Concentration effect: Increasing reactant concentration increases collision frequency → faster rate. Often follows rate law: rate = k[A]^m[B]^n where m and n are reaction orders (determined experimentally, not from stoichiometry).
Surface area effect: Increasing surface area (grinding solids, stirring) increases contact between reactants → faster rate.
Catalyst (katalysator): Speeds up reaction without being consumed. Lowers Ea by providing alternative pathway. Does not change ΔG or equilibrium position, only the rate at which equilibrium is reached.
Key Terms & Definitions
Reaction rate (reaktionshastighet): Change in concentration per unit time (mol/(L·s)).
Order of reaction (reaktionsordning): Power of concentration in rate law; determined experimentally, not from equation coefficients.
Rate constant (hastighetskonstant), k: Proportionality constant in rate law; depends on temperature.
Homogeneous catalyst: Catalyst in same phase as reactants; often forms intermediate with reactant.
Heterogeneous catalyst: Catalyst in different phase (e.g., solid catalyst with gaseous reactants).
Key Formulas
Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n
Rate law (m and n determined experimentally)
ln(k₂/k₁) = (Ea/R) · (1/T₁ − 1/T₂)
Arrhenius equation (effect of temperature on rate constant)
t₁/₂ = (ln 2) / k = 0.693 / k
Half-life for first-order reaction

Acids, Bases & pH
Brønsted-Lowry definition, pH scale, strong vs weak acids/bases, buffers, titrations, neutralisation.
▸
Key Concepts
Brønsted-Lowry definition: Acid = proton (H⁺) donor; base = proton acceptor. Water can be both (amphiprotic). Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by one H⁺ (e.g., HCl/Cl⁻, NH₄⁺/NH₃).
pH scale: pH = −log[H⁺]. pH 7 = neutral (at 25°C), pH < 7 = acidic, pH > 7 = basic. Each unit change = 10× change in [H⁺]. pOH = −log[OH⁻]. pH + pOH = 14 (at 25°C).
Strong acids (starka syror): Completely dissociate: HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄. Examples: 0.1 mol/L HCl has pH = 1 (100% dissociation).
Weak acids (svaga syror): Partially dissociate: CH₃COOH, HF, etc. Equilibrium: HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻. Ka = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA]. Stronger weak acid = larger Ka. 0.1 mol/L CH₃COOH has pH ≈ 2.9 (partial dissociation).
Strong bases (starka baser): Completely dissociate: NaOH, KOH, etc. 0.1 mol/L NaOH has pH = 13.
Weak bases (svaga baser): Partially accept protons: NH₃, amines. Kb = [NH₄⁺][OH⁻]/[NH₃]. Stronger weak base = larger Kb.
Buffer (buffert): Solution of weak acid + conjugate base (or weak base + conjugate acid) that resists pH change. Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]). Buffer capacity depends on concentrations of acid and conjugate base.
Key Terms & Definitions
pH: Negative logarithm of hydrogen ion concentration; measure of acidity.
Ka (acid dissociation constant): Equilibrium constant for acid dissociation; larger Ka = stronger acid.
Kb (base dissociation constant): Equilibrium constant for base ionisation; larger Kb = stronger base.
Conjugate acid-base pair: Two species differing by one H⁺; related by acid-base equilibrium.
Titration (titrering): Procedure to determine concentration by reaction with known volume and concentration of another solution.
Key Formulas

[H⁺][OH⁻] = Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ (at 25°C)
Ionic product of water
Ka = [H⁺][A⁻] / [HA]
Acid dissociation constant
pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
Henderson-Hasselbalch equation (buffer pH)
n(acid) = n(base) at equivalence point (for titration)

Thermodynamics Overview
Enthalpy ΔH, entropy ΔS, Gibbs free energy ΔG, spontaneity, energy diagrams.
▸
Key Concepts
Enthalpy (entalpi), ΔH: Heat released or absorbed at constant pressure. Exothermic (ΔH < 0, heat released) vs endothermic (ΔH > 0, heat absorbed). Measured in kJ/mol. Combustion and many common reactions are exothermic.
Entropy (entropi), ΔS: Measure of disorder or randomness. ΔS > 0 for spontaneous processes (overall). Gas formation increases entropy. Dissolution usually increases entropy. Low temperature favours negative ΔS; high temperature can overcome it.
Gibbs free energy (Gibbs fri energi), ΔG: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. Predicts spontaneity: ΔG < 0 = spontaneous (forward), ΔG > 0 = non-spontaneous (reverse), ΔG = 0 = equilibrium. Temperature can change sign of ΔG for some reactions.
Spontaneity: Depends on both ΔH and ΔS (not just heat). Exothermic + entropy increase (ΔH < 0, ΔS > 0) always spontaneous. Endothermic + entropy decrease (ΔH > 0, ΔS < 0) never spontaneous. Other cases depend on temperature.
Key Terms & Definitions
Exothermic: Reaction that releases heat (ΔH < 0).
Endothermic: Reaction that absorbs heat (ΔH > 0).
Spontaneous (spontan): Reaction that can proceed without continuous external energy input (ΔG < 0).
Non-spontaneous (icke-spontan): Reaction that requires external energy input to proceed (ΔG > 0).
System (system): The part of the universe we are studying (e.g., reaction flask); surroundings are everything else.
Key Formulas
ΔG = ΔH − TΔS
Gibbs free energy at constant T and P
ΔG° = −RT ln(K)
Relation between ΔG and equilibrium constant
ΔH°_rxn = Σ ΔH°_f(products) − Σ ΔH°_f(reactants)
Hess's law using standard enthalpies of formation

Quantitative Analysis
Titrations, gravimetric analysis, volumetric methods, calculations.
▸
Key Concepts
Titration (titrering): A standard solution (titrant, of known concentration and volume) is gradually added to an unknown solution (analyte) until the equivalence point (molar equivalence). Indicator signals endpoint (close to but not exactly equivalence point). Calculation: n(acid) = n(base) at equivalence point → concentration of analyte.
Gravimetric analysis (gravimetrisk analys): Determine analyte by converting to a stable precipitate (e.g., AgCl), filtering, drying, and weighing. Calculation: mass of analyte = (mass of precipitate × molar mass of analyte) / molar mass of precipitate. Requires stoichiometry and exact molar mass knowledge.
Volumetric methods: Determine concentration from volume measurements (titrations, dilutions). Precision depends on glassware (burette > graduated cylinder). Requires calibrated equipment and careful technique (reading meniscus correctly, zero at start).
Standard solution (standardlösning): Solution of precisely known concentration, used as titrant. Made by dissolving weighed solid in solvent and diluting to mark in volumetric flask (not graduated cylinder, which has lower accuracy).

Titrant: Solution of known concentration added during titration.
Analyte: Substance being analysed (concentration unknown).
Equivalence point (ekvivalenspunkt): Point in titration when molar amounts are exactly equal (stoichiometric).
Endpoint (slutpunkt): Visual indicator of when to stop titration; usually close to but not exactly equivalence point.
Precipitate (nederbörd): Solid formed when ions combine in solution.

Molar absorptivity (molär absorptivitet), ε: Constant relating absorbance to concentration; characteristic of each molecule at given wavelength.
Molecular ion peak (molekyljon-peak), M⁺: Peak in mass spectrum corresponding to intact ionised molecule; indicates molar mass.

Vanligt fel: Att försöka identifiera varje topp i fingeravtrycksområdet. Fokusera på de karakteristiska topparna ovanför 1500 cm⁻¹ för funktionella grupper.
Chromatography
Paper chromatography, thin-layer chromatography (TLC), column chromatography, gas chromatography (GC), Rf values.
▸
Key Concepts
Paper chromatography (papperskromatografi): Sample placed on paper (stationary phase), paper dipped in solvent (mobile phase). Solvent carries components up the paper at different rates based on solubility (partition). Separated by degree of solubility in mobile phase relative to paper.
Thin-layer chromatography (TLC): Similar principle but faster and more efficient. Sample on thin layer of adsorbent on glass/plastic. Solvent front moves up. Rf = distance component moved / distance solvent moved. Same component always has same Rf under same conditions; useful for identifying substances and checking purity.
Column chromatography: Solvent flows down a column filled with adsorbent (or solid support). Compounds elute (come out) at different times based on affinity for adsorbent and solvent. Useful for separating and purifying compounds; can collect pure fractions.
Gas chromatography (GC): Vaporised sample injected into column (often capillary) carried by inert gas. Separation based on boiling point and interaction with stationary phase. Detector measures concentration. Retention time (time to elute) characteristic of each compound; area under peak proportional to amount.
Rf value (retention factor): 0 < Rf < 1. Larger Rf = more soluble in mobile phase (travels further). Same substance = same Rf; different substance = different Rf (under same conditions). Used to identify compounds and assess purity.
Key Terms & Definitions
Chromatography (kromatografi): Separation technique based on differential affinity between stationary and mobile phases.
Stationary phase (stationär fas): Medium that remains fixed (paper, adsorbent, liquid on support).
Mobile phase (mobil fas): Solvent that moves through the system (water, organic solvent).
Rf value (Rf): Ratio of distance travelled by solute to distance travelled by solvent front.
Elution (elution): Process of solute passing through and out of the chromatography system.
Exam Focus
Calculate and interpret Rf values from chromatograms; use to identify compounds or assess purity.
Explain the principle of separation in each chromatographic method (partition, adsorption, etc.).
Design a TLC experiment to separate a mixture and identify components.
Interpret GC data: identify peaks, retention times, and peak areas (proportional to amounts).

Förklara principen bakom kromatografi och skillnaden mellan stationär och mobil fas.

Princip: Ämnen separeras baserat på deras olika affinitet till en stationär fas (fast/vätska på bärare) och en mobil fas (vätska/gas som rör sig). [1]
Separation: Ämnen som attraheras mer av den stationära fasen rör sig långsammare. Ämnen som föredrar den mobila fasen rör sig snabbare. Rf-värde = avstånd ämne/avstånd front. [1]
Vanligt fel: Att blanda ihop stationär och mobil fas. Stationär = den som INTE rör sig. Mobil = den som rör sig (lösningsmedlet).

Planning & Conducting Experiments
Safety, risk assessment, experimental design, methodology, reproducibility.
▸
Key Concepts
Safety (säkerhet): Essential in any lab. Wear safety glasses, lab coat, closed shoes. Know location of emergency equipment (eyewash, shower, first aid kit). Handle hazardous chemicals carefully (read labels, use fume hood for volatiles). Never eat/drink in lab. Waste disposal follows regulations.
Risk assessment (riskbedömning): Identify hazards (chemical, physical, biological), assess likelihood and severity, implement controls (PPE, ventilation, dilution, substitution of less hazardous material). COSHH assessment (Control of Substances Hazardous to Health) required before experiment.
Experimental design: Clear research question, testable hypothesis. Identify independent variable (what you change), dependent variable (what you measure), control variables (what you keep constant). Adequate number of replicates (usually ≥3). Valid controls (blank, negative, positive as appropriate).
Methodology (metodologi): Detailed, step-by-step procedure allowing others to replicate. Record all observations (colour changes, temperature, gas evolution, etc.). Measure quantities precisely (use appropriate glassware: burettes, pipettes, volumetric flasks for accuracy).
Reproducibility (reproducerbarhet): Experiment gives consistent results when repeated. High reproducibility indicates good technique and controls. Variation between replicates indicates random error; systematic error if all consistently high/low.
Key Terms & Definitions
Safety (säkerhet): Freedom from risk or hazard; critical in laboratory work.
Hazard (fara): Property of substance or procedure that can cause harm.
Risk (risk): Probability and magnitude of harm if hazard is realised.
Control variable (kontrollvariabel): Variable kept constant to isolate effect of independent variable.
Replicate (replikat): Repeated measurement under identical conditions.
Exam Focus
Identify hazards and risks in a given experiment; suggest appropriate control measures.
Design an experiment with clear variables (independent, dependent, control) to test a hypothesis.
Write a detailed method allowing others to replicate the experiment.
Evaluate reproducibility of data; suggest improvements to reduce error.
Data Processing & Error Analysis
Uncertainty, significant figures, error propagation, graphical analysis, evaluation.
▸
Key Concepts
Uncertainty (osäkerhet): Every measurement has uncertainty. Analogue instruments: ± half the smallest division (e.g., balance to 0.01 g has uncertainty ±0.005 g). Digital instruments: ± the smallest displayed unit (e.g., digital scale reading 5.23 g has uncertainty ±0.01 g). Percentage uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty / measured value) × 100%.
Significant figures (signifikanta siffror): All known digits plus one estimated digit. Zeros: leading zeros not significant; zeros between non-zero digits significant; trailing zeros significant only if decimal point shown. Rules: in addition/subtraction, result has same decimal places as least precise measurement; in multiplication/division, result has same significant figures as least precise measurement.
Error propagation (felfortplantning): Addition/subtraction: add absolute uncertainties. Multiplication/division: add percentage uncertainties. Powers: multiply percentage uncertainty by power coefficient. Example: if measurement has 2% uncertainty and is squared, result has 4% uncertainty.
Systematic vs random error: Systematic error (e.g., calibration off, always too high) biases all measurements same way. Random error (e.g., parallax, temperature fluctuation) scatters measurements around true value. Replicates reveal random error (standard deviation); systematic error requires checking method.
Graphical analysis: Plot data with error bars showing uncertainty. Best-fit line should pass through most error bars. Calculate gradient from best-fit line; determine uncertainty in gradient from steepest and shallowest lines through extreme error bars. Linearisation (e.g., plotting 1/[A] vs time for second-order kinetics) allows determination of rate constant from gradient.
Key Terms & Definitions
Absolute uncertainty (absolut osäkerhet): Expressed in same units as measurement (e.g., ±0.1 g).
Percentage uncertainty (procentuell osäkerhet): Expressed as percentage of measured value.
Accuracy (noggrannhet): How close measurement is to true/accepted value.
Precision (precision): How close repeated measurements are to each other (reproducibility).
Standard deviation (standardavvikelse): Measure of scatter of data around mean.
Exam Focus
Calculate absolute and percentage uncertainty from instrument readings.
Apply error propagation rules to calculate final uncertainty in calculated quantities.
Express final answers to appropriate number of significant figures given uncertainties.
Draw graphs with error bars; determine gradient and its uncertainty from multiple lines.
Identify and explain sources of systematic and random error in experiment; suggest improvements.
Chemistry & Society
Environmental chemistry, sustainable development, green chemistry, impact of chemistry on society.
▸
Key Concepts
Environmental chemistry (miljökemi): Study of chemical processes in atmosphere, hydrosphere, and lithosphere. Topics: air pollution (NO₂, SO₂ from fossil fuels; particulates; ozone depletion from CFCs), water pollution (heavy metals, eutrophication from nitrogen/phosphorus), soil contamination (pesticides, mining runoff).
Climate change (klimatförändringar): CO₂ from combustion of fossil fuels is major greenhouse gas. Increased atmospheric CO₂ → enhanced greenhouse effect → global warming. Chemistry involved: combustion reactions, carbon cycle, absorption spectra of CO₂ and other greenhouse gases.
Green chemistry (grön kemi): Design of chemical products and processes to reduce hazardous substances. Principles: prevent waste (don't generate it), use renewable feedstocks, minimise energy use, minimise use of toxic/harmful chemicals, design safer chemicals with lower toxicity. Example: use of biodegradable solvents instead of VOCs.
Sustainable development (hållbar utveckling): Balancing economic growth, environmental protection, and social responsibility. Chemistry supports this through renewable energy (solar cells, biofuels), biodegradable plastics, catalysis (reducing energy use), water purification, clean production methods.
Chemistry applications in society: Pharmaceuticals (life-saving drugs), materials (alloys, ceramics, polymers), agriculture (fertilisers, pesticides but also controlled-release and sustainable alternatives), energy (batteries, hydrogen fuel cells). Benefits must be weighed against environmental and social costs.
Key Terms & Definitions
Sustainability (hållbarhet): Ability to meet present needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs.
Carbon footprint (kolfotavtryck): Total greenhouse gas emissions (especially CO₂) associated with a product or activity.
Renewable resource (förnyelsebar resurs): Natural resource that can be replenished (e.g., solar energy, plant-based feedstocks).
Biodegradable (biologiskt nedbrytbar): Capable of being decomposed by microorganisms.
Catalysis (katalys): Speeding up reaction (often lowering energy use) via catalyst without being consumed.
Exam Focus
Explain environmental problems (acid rain, ozone depletion, climate change) in terms of chemistry; discuss human activities causing them.
Describe green chemistry principles and evaluate how a chemical process could be made more sustainable.
Discuss the role of chemistry in addressing global challenges (renewable energy, clean water, waste reduction).
Evaluate trade-offs between benefits and environmental/social costs of chemical technologies (e.g., pesticides, plastics, fossil fuels).
