2D Motion in Gravitational Fields
Projectile motion: horizontal launch and angled launch. Decomposing velocity into x/y components. Trajectory equations, max height, range.
▸
Key Concepts
Independence of horizontal and vertical motion: In projectile motion, the horizontal component of velocity remains constant (no air resistance), while the vertical component is affected by gravitational acceleration g = 9.81 m/s². These two components act independently of each other.
Horizontal launch (horisontellt kast): Object launched horizontally from a height. Initial vertical velocity = 0. Horizontal distance depends on launch speed and fall time. The path is a parabola.
Angled launch (snett kast): Object launched at angle θ to the horizontal. Initial velocity is decomposed into v₀ₓ = v₀·cos θ and v₀ᵧ = v₀·sin θ. Maximum height occurs when vertical velocity = 0. Range is maximised at θ = 45° (no air resistance).
Symmetry of trajectory: For level ground, time to reach max height = time to fall back. The trajectory is symmetric about the highest point. Total flight time T = 2v₀ sinθ / g.
Key Terms & Definitions
Projectile (projektil): An object moving under the influence of gravity alone after being launched.
Trajectory (bana): The curved path followed by a projectile through space — parabolic in uniform gravity.
Range (räckvidd): The horizontal distance covered by a projectile from launch to landing.
Component (komponent): The projection of a vector along an axis (x or y). Used to break velocity into horizontal and vertical parts.
Free fall (fritt fall): Motion under gravity alone, with no air resistance. Acceleration = g downward.

Horizontal launch from a table/cliff: given height and horizontal speed, find where the object lands.
Sketch or interpret trajectory graphs: x(t) is linear, y(t) is parabolic, vᵧ(t) is linear.
2D Motion in Electric Fields
Charged particle deflection in uniform electric fields. Parabolic trajectories. Comparison with gravitational projectile motion.
▸
Key Concepts
Analogy with gravity: A charged particle entering a uniform electric field perpendicular to its velocity follows a parabolic path — exactly analogous to projectile motion, but with electric force replacing gravity.
Uniform electric field: Between parallel plates with potential difference V and separation d, the field E = V/d is uniform. The force on a charge q is F = qE, giving acceleration a = qE/m.
Deflection of charged particles: A positive charge is deflected toward the negative plate; a negative charge toward the positive plate. The horizontal velocity remains constant while the vertical velocity changes due to the electric force.
Applications: Cathode ray tubes, ink-jet printers, mass spectrometers, and particle accelerators all use electric field deflection.
Key Terms & Definitions
Electric field (elektriskt fält), E: A region where a charged particle experiences a force. Measured in N/C or V/m.
Uniform field (homogent fält): A field with the same strength and direction at every point — produced between parallel plates.
Deflection (avböjning): The change in path of a charged particle due to an electric (or magnetic) field.
Potential difference (spänning), V: The energy per unit charge between two points. Measured in volts (V).
Key Formulas

Sketch the trajectory and explain why it is parabolic between the plates and straight-line after leaving.
Circular Motion (Centralrörelse)
Centripetal acceleration and centripetal force. Uniform circular motion. Applications: orbits, curves, centrifuges. Period, frequency, angular velocity.
▸
Key Concepts
Uniform circular motion: An object moving in a circle at constant speed. The velocity direction changes continuously, so the object is accelerating even though its speed is constant.
Centripetal acceleration: Always directed toward the centre of the circle. Its magnitude is a = v²/r = ω²r. This is not a separate force — it is the net result of real forces (gravity, tension, friction, normal force, etc.).
Centripetal force: The net inward force that causes circular motion. F = mv²/r. Common providers: gravitational force (orbits), tension (swinging object), friction (car on a curve), normal force (loop-the-loop).
Angular velocity (ω): The rate of change of angle, measured in rad/s. Related to period T and frequency f by ω = 2π/T = 2πf.
Key Terms & Definitions
Centripetal (centripetal): "Centre-seeking" — describes the direction of acceleration and net force in circular motion.
Period (T, periodtid): Time for one complete revolution. Measured in seconds.
Frequency (f, frekvens): Number of revolutions per second. f = 1/T. Measured in Hz.
Angular velocity (ω, vinkelhastighet): Angle swept per unit time. ω = 2π/T. Measured in rad/s.
Centrifugal force (centrifugalkraft): A fictitious (pseudo) force that appears to push objects outward in a rotating reference frame. Not a real force in an inertial frame.
Key Formulas

Torque & Rotational Equilibrium (Vridmoment)
Torque = F × d. Clockwise vs counterclockwise moments. Conditions for static equilibrium. Lever problems, beams, and balancing.
▸
Key Concepts
Torque (vridmoment, τ): The rotational effect of a force. Depends on the force magnitude, the distance from the pivot, and the angle. τ = F · d · sinθ, where d is the distance from the pivot to the point where the force is applied.
Moment arm (momentarm): The perpendicular distance from the line of action of the force to the pivot point. When force is perpendicular to the lever arm, moment arm = lever arm length.
Conditions for static equilibrium: (1) Net force = 0 (translational equilibrium). (2) Net torque = 0 about any pivot point (rotational equilibrium). Both must hold simultaneously.
Centre of mass / gravity: The point where the entire weight of an object can be considered to act. For a uniform beam, this is the geometric centre.
Key Terms & Definitions
Torque (vridmoment, τ): The measure of a force's tendency to cause rotation about a pivot. Unit: N·m.
Pivot / fulcrum (vridpunkt): The fixed point around which rotation occurs.
Lever arm (hävarm): The distance from the pivot to the point of force application.
Static equilibrium (statisk jämvikt): A state where an object is at rest with zero net force and zero net torque.
Couple (kraftpar): Two equal and opposite parallel forces that produce pure rotation (net force = 0 but net torque ≠ 0).
Key Formulas

Ladder problems: combine force equilibrium (x and y) with torque equilibrium to find friction or normal forces.
Always state the chosen pivot point and show clockwise vs counterclockwise torques explicitly.
Numerical Simulation of 2D Motion
Euler's method for stepwise calculation. Spreadsheet or programming-based simulation of trajectories. Iterative approximation.
▸
Key Concepts
Euler's method: A numerical technique to approximate solutions to differential equations. At each time step Δt: update velocity using v_new = v_old + a·Δt, then update position using x_new = x_old + v·Δt. Smaller Δt gives better accuracy.
Why numerical methods? Many real-world problems (air resistance, variable forces) have no simple analytical solution. Numerical simulation allows us to model these situations step-by-step.
Implementation: Can be done with spreadsheets (Excel/Google Sheets with columns for t, vₓ, vᵧ, x, y) or with programming (Python, etc.). Each row represents one time step.
Accuracy vs step size: Smaller time steps produce more accurate results but require more computation. The error in Euler's method is proportional to Δt.
Key Terms & Definitions
Euler's method (Eulers metod): A first-order numerical method for solving ordinary differential equations by stepping forward in small increments.
Time step (tidssteg, Δt): The small time interval used in each iteration of the numerical simulation.
Iterative (iterativ): A process that repeats the same calculation over and over with updated values each time.
Numerical approximation: An estimated solution obtained by computation rather than exact analytical methods.
Key Formulas
v(t + Δt) = v(t) + a(t) · Δt
Velocity update step
x(t + Δt) = x(t) + v(t) · Δt
Position update step
a = F_net / m
Acceleration from Newton's second law at each step
Exam Focus

Explain why reducing Δt improves accuracy, and discuss the trade-off with computation time.
Compare a numerical simulation result with the analytical solution and explain any discrepancy.

Simple Harmonic Motion (Harmonisk Svängning)
SHM as a model for oscillation. Spring-mass systems and pendulums. Period, amplitude, frequency. Energy in SHM.
▸
Key Concepts
Definition of SHM: Oscillatory motion where the restoring force is proportional to the displacement from equilibrium and directed toward equilibrium. F = −kx (Hooke's law form). This produces sinusoidal motion.
Spring-mass system: A mass attached to a spring oscillates with period T = 2π√(m/k). Period is independent of amplitude. Increasing mass increases T; increasing spring constant decreases T.
Simple pendulum: A mass on a light string swings with period T = 2π√(L/g) for small angles. Period depends on length and gravitational acceleration but not on mass or amplitude (for small angles).
Energy in SHM: Continuous exchange between kinetic and potential energy. At maximum displacement: all energy is potential (Eₚ = ½kA²). At equilibrium: all energy is kinetic (Eₖ = ½mv²_max). Total mechanical energy E = ½kA² is constant.
Key Terms & Definitions
Amplitude (A, amplitud): Maximum displacement from the equilibrium position. Measured in metres.
Period (T, periodtid): Time for one complete oscillation (back and forth). Measured in seconds.
Frequency (f, frekvens): Number of complete oscillations per second. f = 1/T. Measured in Hz.
Restoring force (återställande kraft): The force that always acts to return the oscillating object to its equilibrium position.
Equilibrium position (jämviktsläge): The position where net force is zero and the object would remain at rest if undisturbed.
Damping (dämpning): The gradual reduction of amplitude over time due to energy losses (friction, air resistance).
Key Formulas

Reflection, Refraction & Interference
Laws of reflection and refraction (Snell's law). Interference of light, sound, and other waves. Double-slit experiment.
▸
Key Concepts
Law of reflection: Angle of incidence = angle of reflection. Both angles measured from the normal to the surface.
Refraction and Snell's law: When a wave passes from one medium to another, its speed changes, causing a change in direction. n₁ sinθ₁ = n₂ sinθ₂. Going from less dense to more dense medium, the ray bends toward the normal.
Total internal reflection: Occurs when light travels from a denser to a less dense medium and the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle θ_c. sinθ_c = n₂/n₁. Applications: optical fibres, prisms.
Superposition and interference: When two waves meet, their displacements add (superposition principle). Constructive interference: waves in phase → larger amplitude. Destructive interference: waves out of phase → reduced amplitude.
Double-slit experiment (Young's): Monochromatic light through two narrow slits produces an interference pattern on a screen — alternating bright and dark fringes. This demonstrates the wave nature of light. Path difference = nλ for bright fringes, (n+½)λ for dark fringes.
Key Terms & Definitions
Refractive index (brytningsindex, n): The ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to the speed of light in a medium. n = c/v. Always ≥ 1.
Normal (normalen): An imaginary line perpendicular to a surface at the point of incidence.
Critical angle (gränsvinkel): The angle of incidence beyond which total internal reflection occurs.
Constructive interference: When waves combine to produce a larger amplitude (path difference = whole number of wavelengths).
Destructive interference: When waves combine to produce a smaller or zero amplitude (path difference = half-integer number of wavelengths).
Path difference (vägskillnad): The difference in distance travelled by two waves arriving at the same point.
Key Formulas

Standing Waves & Resonance
Formation of standing waves. Nodes and antinodes. Resonance in everyday life and technology. Harmonics in strings and pipes.
▸
Key Concepts
Standing wave formation: A standing wave forms when two identical waves travel in opposite directions and superpose. This occurs by reflection at boundaries. The wave appears to oscillate in place — it does not transfer energy along its length.
Nodes and antinodes: Nodes are points of zero displacement (destructive interference). Antinodes are points of maximum displacement (constructive interference). Distance between adjacent nodes = λ/2.
Harmonics on strings: A string fixed at both ends has nodes at each end. Fundamental (1st harmonic): L = λ/2. 2nd harmonic: L = λ. 3rd harmonic: L = 3λ/2. General: L = nλ/2.
Harmonics in pipes: Open pipe (both ends open): antinodes at each end, all harmonics present. Closed pipe (one end closed): node at closed end, antinode at open end, only odd harmonics present.
Resonance: When a driving frequency matches a natural frequency of a system, the amplitude increases dramatically. Examples: Tacoma Narrows Bridge, breaking a wine glass with sound, musical instruments.
Key Terms & Definitions
Standing wave (stående våg): A wave pattern that oscillates in place, formed by superposition of two waves traveling in opposite directions.
Node (nod): A point on a standing wave where displacement is always zero.
Antinode (buk): A point on a standing wave where displacement is maximum.
Fundamental frequency (grundton): The lowest natural frequency of a vibrating system (1st harmonic).
Harmonic (överton): An integer multiple of the fundamental frequency.
Resonance (resonans): A phenomenon where a system oscillates with maximum amplitude when driven at its natural frequency.
Key Formulas

Sound Intensity & Doppler Effect
Decibels, loudness, sound intensity. The Doppler effect for sound — moving source and moving observer.
▸
Key Concepts
Sound intensity: Power per unit area (W/m²). Intensity decreases with distance as I ∝ 1/r² (inverse square law) for a point source in open space. Human hearing ranges from ~10⁻¹² W/m² (threshold) to ~1 W/m² (pain threshold).
Decibel scale: A logarithmic scale for comparing intensities. β = 10·log₁₀(I/I₀) where I₀ = 10⁻¹² W/m². Doubling intensity adds ~3 dB. The scale is logarithmic because human hearing perceives loudness logarithmically.
Doppler effect: The apparent change in frequency (and wavelength) of a wave due to relative motion between the source and observer. Approaching source → higher frequency (shorter λ). Receding source → lower frequency (longer λ).
Applications: Speed guns (police radar), medical ultrasound, redshift in astronomy (cosmological Doppler), ambulance sirens.
Key Terms & Definitions
Sound intensity (ljudintensitet): The power of sound per unit area perpendicular to the direction of propagation. Unit: W/m².
Decibel (dB): A logarithmic unit for measuring sound level relative to a reference intensity.
Doppler effect (dopplereffekten): The change in observed frequency of a wave caused by relative motion between source and observer.
Blueshift / Redshift: Observed frequency increase (blueshift) or decrease (redshift) due to source approaching or receding.
Key Formulas

Magnetic Fields from Current-Carrying Conductors
Magnetic field around a straight wire, solenoid. Right-hand rule. Field strength and direction.
▸
Key Concepts
Current creates magnetism: A current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field around it. This is the foundation of electromagnetism (Ørsted's discovery, 1820).
Straight wire: The magnetic field forms concentric circles around the wire. Use the right-hand rule: thumb in the direction of current (conventional), fingers curl in the direction of the field. Field strength: B = μ₀I/(2πr).
Solenoid (spole): A coil of wire that produces a nearly uniform field inside when carrying current. The field is strong and uniform inside, weak outside. B = μ₀nI where n = N/L (turns per unit length).
Force between parallel wires: Two parallel wires carrying current exert forces on each other. Same direction → attract. Opposite direction → repel. This is how the ampere was originally defined.
Key Terms & Definitions
Magnetic field (magnetfält), B: A field surrounding magnets and current-carrying conductors. Unit: tesla (T).
Right-hand rule (högerhandsregeln): A mnemonic for determining the direction of the magnetic field around a current-carrying conductor.
Solenoid (solenoid/spole): A coil of wire that produces a uniform magnetic field inside when current flows through it.
Permeability of free space (μ₀): A constant = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A that relates magnetic field to current.

Charged Particles in Magnetic Fields
Lorentz force on moving charges. Circular motion of charged particles in B-fields. Applications: mass spectrometer, cyclotron.
▸
Key Concepts
Magnetic force on moving charge: A charged particle moving through a magnetic field experiences a force: F = qvB sinθ, where θ is the angle between v and B. The force is perpendicular to both v and B (use left-hand rule for positive charges or right-hand rule and reverse for electrons).
Circular motion in B-field: Since the force is always perpendicular to velocity, a charged particle moving perpendicular to a uniform B-field follows a circular path. The magnetic force provides the centripetal force: qvB = mv²/r → r = mv/(qB).
Mass spectrometer: Ions are accelerated through a potential difference, then deflected in a magnetic field. Different masses follow different radii, allowing separation and identification of isotopes.
No work done: The magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it does no work and cannot change the speed (kinetic energy) of a particle — only its direction.
Key Terms & Definitions
Lorentz force: The total electromagnetic force on a charged particle: F = qE + qv×B.
Tesla (T): SI unit of magnetic field strength. 1 T = 1 kg/(A·s²).
Mass spectrometer (masspektrometer): An instrument that separates ions by their mass-to-charge ratio using electric and magnetic fields.
Cyclotron: A particle accelerator that uses magnetic fields to bend particles in a spiral path and electric fields to accelerate them.
Key Formulas

Electromagnetic Induction
Faraday's law, Lenz's law. Induced EMF. Applications: AC generator, transformer.
▸
Key Concepts
Electromagnetic induction: A changing magnetic flux through a conductor induces an electromotive force (EMF). This is the basis of electric generators and transformers.
Magnetic flux (Φ): The "amount" of magnetic field passing through a surface. Φ = BAcosθ, where θ is the angle between the field and the normal to the surface. Unit: weber (Wb).
Faraday's law: The induced EMF equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux: ε = −dΦ/dt. For a coil with N turns: ε = −N·dΦ/dt. EMF is induced only when flux is changing.
Lenz's law: The direction of the induced current opposes the change in flux that caused it. This is a consequence of energy conservation (the minus sign in Faraday's law).
AC generator: A coil rotating in a magnetic field experiences continuously changing flux, producing alternating EMF. The faster the rotation, the higher the frequency and peak EMF.
Transformer: Two coils sharing a magnetic core. A changing current in the primary induces EMF in the secondary. V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p. Ideal transformer: power in = power out.
Key Terms & Definitions
Magnetic flux (magnetiskt flöde, Φ): The product of magnetic field strength, area, and cosine of the angle between them. Unit: Wb (weber).
EMF (elektromotorisk kraft, ε): The potential difference induced by a changing magnetic flux. Unit: volts (V).
Faraday's law: The induced EMF equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux through a circuit.
Lenz's law: The induced current flows in a direction that opposes the change in flux producing it.
Transformer (transformator): A device that uses electromagnetic induction to change AC voltage between two coils.
Key Formulas

Wave-Particle Duality of EM Radiation
Photon model, photoelectric effect connections. Wave vs particle descriptions of light.
▸
Key Concepts
Wave-particle duality: Light (and all EM radiation) exhibits both wave properties (interference, diffraction) and particle properties (photoelectric effect, Compton scattering). Neither model alone is complete — both are needed.
Photon model: Light consists of discrete packets of energy called photons. Each photon carries energy E = hf = hc/λ, where h is Planck's constant. Photons have no rest mass but carry momentum p = h/λ.
Photoelectric effect: Light shining on a metal surface can eject electrons, but only if the photon energy (hf) exceeds the work function (φ) of the metal. Increasing intensity increases the number of electrons, not their energy. This cannot be explained by the wave model alone.
de Broglie hypothesis: Matter also has wave properties. Any particle with momentum p has an associated wavelength λ = h/p. This is significant for electrons and subatomic particles but negligible for macroscopic objects.
Key Terms & Definitions
Photon (foton): A quantum (discrete packet) of electromagnetic radiation. Massless, travels at speed c.
Planck's constant (h): A fundamental constant = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s relating energy to frequency.
Work function (utträdesarbete, φ): The minimum energy required to eject an electron from a metal surface.
Threshold frequency (gränsfrekvens): The minimum frequency of light needed to cause photoemission. f₀ = φ/h.
de Broglie wavelength: The wavelength associated with a moving particle: λ = h/p = h/(mv).
Key Formulas

E_k(max) = hf − φ

Communication & Detection Technology
Physical principles behind radio, mobile networks, radar, medical imaging. Modulation, signal transmission.
▸
Key Concepts
Electromagnetic spectrum in communication: Different frequency bands are used for different purposes: radio waves (AM/FM broadcasting), microwaves (mobile phones, WiFi, radar), infrared (remote controls, fibre optics), visible light (fibre optics).
Modulation: Information is encoded onto a carrier wave by varying its amplitude (AM) or frequency (FM). FM is more resistant to noise/interference than AM.
Radar: Radio waves are transmitted, reflected off objects, and detected on return. The time delay gives distance; frequency shift (Doppler) gives speed.
Medical imaging: X-rays (absorption by dense tissue), ultrasound (reflection at tissue boundaries, Doppler for blood flow), MRI (nuclear magnetic resonance — not covered in detail). Each technique uses different physical principles.
Optical fibres: Use total internal reflection to transmit light signals over long distances with minimal loss. Used in telecommunications and medical endoscopy.
Key Terms & Definitions
Modulation: The process of encoding information onto a carrier wave by altering its amplitude, frequency, or phase.
AM (Amplitude Modulation): Signal information encoded as variations in the wave's amplitude.
FM (Frequency Modulation): Signal information encoded as variations in the wave's frequency.
Radar: Radio Detection And Ranging — uses reflected radio waves to detect objects and determine their distance and speed.
Exam Focus
Describe the physical principles behind a specific communication or detection technology (radio, radar, ultrasound, etc.).
Explain advantages and disadvantages of AM vs FM modulation.
Describe how total internal reflection enables fibre optic communication.

Large-Scale Structure of the Universe
Models and theories of the universe's development. Big Bang, cosmic expansion, dark matter/energy.
▸
Key Concepts
The Big Bang theory: The universe began ~13.8 billion years ago from an extremely hot, dense state and has been expanding ever since. Evidence: cosmic microwave background radiation (CMB), Hubble's law (redshift of galaxies), abundance of light elements (H, He).
Hubble's law and expansion: Galaxies are receding from us with velocity proportional to their distance: v = H₀d, where H₀ is the Hubble constant. This does not mean we are at the centre — all points see other points receding (like dots on an inflating balloon).
Dark matter: Invisible matter that does not emit or absorb light but exerts gravitational effects. Evidence: galaxy rotation curves (stars orbit too fast for visible mass alone), gravitational lensing. Makes up ~27% of the universe's mass-energy.
Dark energy: A mysterious form of energy causing the expansion of the universe to accelerate. Discovered in 1998 via observations of distant supernovae. Makes up ~68% of the universe's mass-energy.
Key Terms & Definitions
Big Bang: The prevailing cosmological model describing the origin and expansion of the universe from a hot, dense initial state.
Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB): Relic radiation from ~380,000 years after the Big Bang, now observed as microwave radiation at ~2.7 K.
Hubble's constant (H₀): The proportionality constant in Hubble's law, approximately 70 km/s/Mpc. Relates recession velocity to distance.
Redshift (rödförskjutning): The increase in wavelength of light from objects moving away from the observer. Cosmological redshift indicates expansion of space.
Dark matter (mörk materia): Non-luminous matter detected by its gravitational effects.
Dark energy (mörk energi): The unknown form of energy driving the accelerating expansion of the universe.

Galaxy, Star & Planet Formation
Stellar lifecycle: nebula → main sequence → red giant → white dwarf / supernova. HR diagram. Planet formation.
▸
Key Concepts
Stellar lifecycle: Stars form from collapsing gas clouds (nebulae). Gravity compresses the gas until nuclear fusion ignites (main sequence). The star's mass determines its lifecycle: low-mass stars → red giant → planetary nebula → white dwarf. High-mass stars → supergiant → supernova → neutron star or black hole.
Main sequence: The longest phase of a star's life, where hydrogen fuses into helium in the core. Our Sun is a main-sequence star. Higher-mass stars burn hotter and faster (shorter main-sequence lifetime).
HR diagram (Hertzsprung-Russell): A plot of luminosity vs surface temperature (or spectral class). Stars are not randomly distributed — they cluster on the main sequence, giant branch, supergiant region, and white dwarf region. Position on the HR diagram reveals a star's evolutionary stage.
Planet formation: Planets form from the accretion disk around a young star. Dust and gas clump together, growing by collision and gravitational attraction. Rocky planets form closer to the star; gas giants form further out.
Key Terms & Definitions
Nebula (nebulosa): A cloud of gas and dust in space, often the birthplace of stars.
Main sequence (huvudserien): The band on the HR diagram where stars spend most of their lives fusing hydrogen.
Red giant (röd jätte): An evolved star that has expanded and cooled after exhausting hydrogen in its core.
White dwarf (vit dvärg): The dense remnant of a low-mass star after it sheds its outer layers.
Supernova: A massive explosion at the end of a high-mass star's life, briefly outshining an entire galaxy.
Neutron star: An extremely dense remnant of a supernova, composed almost entirely of neutrons.
Black hole (svart hål): A region of space where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape.
Accretion disk: A rotating disk of gas and dust around a young star (or black hole) from which material spirals inward.

Atomic Electron Structure & Spectra
Absorption and emission spectra. Energy level transitions. Identifying elements from spectral lines. Bohr model.
▸
Key Concepts
Energy levels: Electrons in atoms occupy discrete energy levels. They can only exist at specific energies, not in between (quantisation). The lowest energy state is the ground state.
Emission spectrum: When an electron drops from a higher to a lower energy level, it emits a photon with energy E = hf = E_upper − E_lower. Hot gases produce bright lines on a dark background (line emission spectrum).
Absorption spectrum: When white light passes through a cool gas, atoms absorb photons of specific frequencies (matching energy level differences). This produces dark lines on a continuous spectrum. Same wavelengths as the emission spectrum for that element.
Spectral fingerprints: Each element has a unique set of spectral lines. This allows identification of elements in distant stars and nebulae by analysing the light they emit or absorb.
Bohr model: Niels Bohr proposed that electrons orbit the nucleus at fixed radii with quantised angular momentum. While simplified, it successfully explains the hydrogen spectrum. Modern quantum mechanics replaces orbits with probability distributions (orbitals).
Key Terms & Definitions
Energy level (energinivå): A discrete, quantised energy state that an electron can occupy in an atom.
Ground state (grundtillstånd): The lowest energy level of an atom.
Excited state: Any energy level above the ground state.
Emission spectrum (emissionsspektrum): A set of bright spectral lines produced when excited atoms emit photons.
Absorption spectrum (absorptionsspektrum): Dark lines in a continuous spectrum caused by atoms absorbing specific photon energies.
Continuous spectrum: A spectrum containing all wavelengths/colours, produced by hot dense objects (e.g., the Sun's surface).

Methods of Investigating the Universe

Key Concepts
Types of telescopes: Optical telescopes (visible light — refracting and reflecting types), radio telescopes (large dishes for radio waves), X-ray and gamma-ray telescopes (must be space-based since atmosphere absorbs these wavelengths). Each reveals different information about cosmic objects.
Spectroscopy: The primary analytical tool in astronomy. Splitting light into a spectrum reveals: composition (spectral lines), temperature (continuous spectrum peak, Wien's law), motion (Doppler shift), and magnetic fields (Zeeman effect).

Standard candles: Objects of known luminosity (e.g., Cepheid variable stars, Type Ia supernovae) used to measure larger distances by comparing apparent and absolute brightness.

Spectroscopy (spektroskopi): The analysis of the spectrum of light from an object to determine its composition, temperature, and motion.
Standard candle: An astronomical object of known absolute luminosity, used for distance measurement.

EM Radiation from Stars & Interstellar Space
Blackbody radiation. Wien's law, Stefan-Boltzmann law. Cosmic microwave background.
▸
Key Concepts
Blackbody radiation: All objects with temperature above 0 K emit EM radiation. A blackbody is an idealised object that absorbs all radiation and re-emits it with a characteristic spectrum depending only on temperature. Stars approximate blackbodies.
Wien's displacement law: The peak wavelength of a blackbody's emission is inversely proportional to its temperature: λ_max = b/T, where b = 2.898 × 10⁻³ m·K. Hotter stars peak at shorter wavelengths (bluer); cooler stars peak at longer wavelengths (redder).
Stefan-Boltzmann law: The total power radiated by a blackbody is proportional to the fourth power of its temperature and its surface area: P = σAT⁴. This means a small increase in temperature causes a large increase in luminosity.
CMB radiation: The cosmic microwave background is blackbody radiation at ~2.7 K, filling the entire universe. It is the cooled remnant of the hot radiation from the early universe (~380,000 years after the Big Bang). Its discovery in 1965 was key evidence for the Big Bang.
Key Terms & Definitions
Blackbody (svartkropp): An idealised object that absorbs all incident radiation and emits radiation based solely on its temperature.
Luminosity (luminositet): The total power (energy per unit time) radiated by a star. Unit: watts (W).

Exoplanet Detection & Conditions for Life
Transit method, radial velocity method. Habitable zone. Conditions required for life.
▸
Key Concepts
Transit method: When a planet passes in front of its star (as seen from Earth), it blocks a tiny fraction of the starlight. The resulting dip in brightness reveals the planet's existence, orbital period, and size (radius). The Kepler/TESS missions used this method extensively.
Radial velocity (Doppler) method: A planet's gravity causes its star to wobble slightly. This wobble creates a periodic Doppler shift in the star's spectrum. The shift reveals the planet's orbital period and minimum mass.
Habitable zone (beboelig zon): The range of distances from a star where conditions could allow liquid water on a planet's surface. Too close = too hot (water evaporates); too far = too cold (water freezes). The habitable zone depends on the star's luminosity.
Conditions for life: Liquid water, a stable energy source, organic molecules (carbon-based chemistry), a protective atmosphere, and a magnetic field (shields from harmful radiation). These criteria guide the search for potentially habitable worlds.
Key Terms & Definitions
Exoplanet: A planet orbiting a star other than our Sun.
Transit: The event of a planet passing in front of its host star as seen from the observer's viewpoint.
Radial velocity: The component of a star's velocity along the line of sight, measurable via Doppler shift.
Habitable zone (Goldilocks zone): The orbital region around a star where surface liquid water is possible.
Light curve: A graph of a star's brightness over time, used to detect transits.

Models & Theories as Simplifications
Validity domains of models. How models evolve, get replaced, or generalised over time. Scientific paradigm shifts.
▸
Key Concepts
Models as simplifications: A model is a simplified representation of reality used to explain and predict phenomena. All models have limitations and a domain of validity (e.g., Newtonian mechanics works at low speeds but breaks down near the speed of light).
Theory evolution: Scientific theories are refined or replaced when new evidence contradicts them. Examples: geocentric → heliocentric model; Newtonian gravity → general relativity; classical physics → quantum mechanics. A new theory must explain everything the old one did, plus the new observations.
Paradigm shifts: Thomas Kuhn's concept — science occasionally undergoes revolutionary changes where the entire framework of understanding shifts (e.g., the Copernican revolution, Einstein's relativity).
Key Terms & Definitions
Model (modell): A simplified description of a system or phenomenon used to make predictions and aid understanding.
Theory (teori): A well-substantiated explanation of a natural phenomenon, supported by extensive evidence and testing.
Domain of validity: The range of conditions under which a model or theory gives accurate predictions.
Paradigm shift: A fundamental change in the basic concepts and practices of a scientific discipline.

Key Concepts
The scientific method: Observation → hypothesis → experiment → analysis → conclusion → peer review. Hypotheses must be falsifiable — capable of being proven wrong by experiment.
Controlled experiments: Change one independent variable, measure the dependent variable, keep all other variables (control variables) constant. This isolates the effect being tested.
Reproducibility: A valid experiment must be reproducible by others. Detailed methodology and transparent reporting are essential.

Key Terms & Definitions
Hypothesis (hypotes): A testable, falsifiable prediction about a natural phenomenon.
Independent variable: The variable that the experimenter deliberately changes.
Dependent variable: The variable that is measured as a response to changes in the independent variable.
Control variable: Variables kept constant to ensure a fair test.
Falsifiability: A property of a statement or hypothesis: it must be possible, in principle, to prove it wrong.

Regression analysis, graph interpretation, unit analysis, order-of-magnitude estimates. Measurement uncertainty.
▸
Key Concepts
Measurement uncertainty: Every measurement has some uncertainty. Random errors cause scatter around the true value (reduced by repeating). Systematic errors shift all measurements in one direction (e.g., a miscalibrated instrument).
Significant figures: Reflect the precision of a measurement. The final answer should have no more significant figures than the least precise input value.
Error propagation: When combining measurements: for addition/subtraction, add absolute uncertainties; for multiplication/division, add percentage uncertainties. For powers, multiply the percentage uncertainty by the power.
Regression and best-fit lines: Draw a best-fit line through data points on a graph. The slope and y-intercept can yield physical constants. Error bars show the uncertainty in each data point.

Random error (slumpmässigt fel): Unpredictable variation in measurements that can be reduced by averaging multiple readings.
Systematic error (systematiskt fel): A consistent bias in measurements due to faulty equipment or method. Cannot be reduced by averaging.
Absolute uncertainty: The uncertainty expressed in the same units as the measurement (e.g., 5.0 ± 0.1 m).
Percentage uncertainty: (Absolute uncertainty / measured value) × 100%.
Significant figures (gällande siffror): The number of meaningful digits in a measurement.

Key Terms & Definitions
Determinism: The philosophical idea that all events are completely determined by prior causes. Challenged by quantum mechanics.

S1.1 — Introduction to the Particulate Nature of Matter
Elements, compounds, mixtures. Kinetic molecular theory. States of matter and phase changes. Separation techniques.
▸
Key Concepts
Elements, compounds, mixtures: An element consists of one type of atom. A compound is two or more elements chemically bonded in fixed ratios. A mixture contains two or more substances not chemically bonded, with variable composition.
Kinetic molecular theory: All matter consists of particles in constant motion. Temperature is a measure of average kinetic energy. Higher temperature = faster particles. This explains properties of solids, liquids, and gases.
States of matter: Solids — particles vibrate in fixed positions, strong forces, fixed shape/volume. Liquids — particles can move past each other, moderate forces, fixed volume but variable shape. Gases — particles far apart, negligible forces, variable shape and volume.
Phase changes: Melting (s→l), freezing (l→s), boiling/evaporation (l→g), condensation (g→l), sublimation (s→g), deposition (g→s). During phase changes, temperature remains constant as energy is used to break/form intermolecular bonds.
Separation techniques: Filtration (insoluble solid from liquid), distillation (different boiling points), chromatography (different affinities for stationary/mobile phases), evaporation (solute from solution), fractional distillation (similar boiling points).
Key Terms & Definitions
Element: A pure substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. All atoms have the same atomic number.
Compound: A substance formed from two or more elements chemically combined in a fixed ratio.
Mixture: Two or more substances physically combined, not chemically bonded, that can be separated by physical methods.
Homogeneous mixture: A mixture with uniform composition throughout (e.g., a solution).
Heterogeneous mixture: A mixture with non-uniform composition — different parts are visible (e.g., sand and water).

S1.2 — The Nuclear Atom

Key Concepts
Subatomic particles: Protons (charge +1, mass 1 u, in nucleus), neutrons (charge 0, mass 1 u, in nucleus), electrons (charge −1, mass ~1/1836 u, in electron shells). Atomic number (Z) = number of protons. Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons.
Isotopes: Atoms of the same element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. Same chemical properties (same electron configuration) but different physical properties (mass, rate of diffusion, nuclear stability).
Mass spectrometry: An instrument that measures the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) of ions. Steps: vaporisation → ionisation → acceleration → deflection → detection. The mass spectrum shows peaks at different m/z values, with height proportional to abundance.
Relative atomic mass (Ar): The weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element, measured relative to ¹²C = 12.000. Calculated from mass spectrum data.
HL: Fragmentation: In mass spectrometry, molecules can break apart (fragment) after ionisation. The fragmentation pattern helps identify the molecular structure. The peak with the highest m/z (excluding isotope peaks) is typically the molecular ion peak (M⁺).

Atomic number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. Defines the element.
Mass number (A): The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.
Isotopes: Atoms of the same element (same Z) with different mass numbers (different number of neutrons).
Relative atomic mass (Ar): The weighted mean mass of an atom of an element relative to 1/12 the mass of a ¹²C atom.
Mass spectrum: A plot of relative abundance vs m/z ratio, showing the isotopes or fragments of a substance.
Molecular ion peak (M⁺): The peak in a mass spectrum corresponding to the intact molecule minus one electron.

S1.3 — Electron Configurations
Emission spectra, energy levels, sublevels (s, p, d, f). Orbital diagrams. HL Ionisation energies. PES.
▸
Key Concepts
Emission spectra: When atoms are excited (by heat or electricity), electrons absorb energy and jump to higher energy levels. When they fall back down, they emit photons of specific energies, producing a line emission spectrum. This proves energy levels are quantised.
Energy levels and sublevels: Main energy levels (n = 1, 2, 3...) contain sublevels: s (2e⁻), p (6e⁻), d (10e⁻), f (14e⁻). Each sublevel contains orbitals, each holding max 2 electrons with opposite spins.
Electron configuration rules: Aufbau principle (fill lowest energy first), Hund's rule (fill orbitals of the same sublevel singly before pairing), Pauli exclusion principle (max 2 electrons per orbital, opposite spins). Exception examples: Cr is [Ar]3d⁵4s¹, Cu is [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹.
HL: Successive ionisation energies: Removing electrons one at a time from an atom requires increasing energy. Large jumps in IE indicate crossing into a new (inner) energy level. Data from successive IEs reveals the number of electrons in each shell.
HL: Photoelectron spectroscopy (PES): Measures the binding energies of all electrons in an atom. Each peak corresponds to a sublevel. Peak height ∝ number of electrons in that sublevel. Provides direct experimental evidence for sublevel structure.
Key Terms & Definitions
Electron configuration: The arrangement of electrons in an atom's energy levels and sublevels (e.g., 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶).
Orbital: A region of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron. Each orbital holds max 2 electrons.
Aufbau principle: Electrons fill the lowest available energy level first.
Hund's rule: Electrons occupy orbitals of the same sublevel singly (with parallel spins) before pairing up.
Pauli exclusion principle: No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers (max 2 per orbital, opposite spins).
Ionisation energy (IE): The energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms.

Absolute zero: 0 K (−273.15°C) — the theoretical temperature at which all particle motion ceases.

S2.1 — The Ionic Model
Ionic bonding, lattice structures, properties. HL Born-Haber cycles. Lattice enthalpy. Polarisation.
▸
Key Concepts
Ionic bonding: Transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal, forming cations (+) and anions (−). The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions forms the ionic bond. Ionic compounds form giant lattice structures.
Properties of ionic compounds: High melting/boiling points (strong electrostatic forces in lattice). Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (ions free to move), but not as solids (ions fixed). Generally soluble in water (polar solvent). Brittle (like charges repel when layers shift).
HL: Born-Haber cycles: An application of Hess's law to calculate lattice enthalpy indirectly using known enthalpy changes: atomisation, ionisation, electron affinity, formation enthalpy. A higher lattice enthalpy indicates a stronger ionic bond.
HL: Polarisation: Small, highly charged cations can distort the electron cloud of large anions, introducing covalent character to the bond (Fajans' rules). This means ionic and covalent bonding exist on a continuum.
Key Terms & Definitions
Ionic bond: The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
Lattice structure: A regular, repeating 3D arrangement of ions in an ionic compound.
Lattice enthalpy: The enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic compound is formed from its gaseous ions (exothermic) — or separated into gaseous ions (endothermic, depending on definition).
Electron affinity: The enthalpy change when one mole of electrons is added to one mole of gaseous atoms.
Fajans' rules: Rules predicting when an ionic bond will have significant covalent character (small cation, large anion, high charge).

S2.2 — The Covalent Model
Lewis structures. VSEPR theory. Polarity. HL Resonance. Formal charge. Hybridisation.
▸
Key Concepts
Covalent bonding: Sharing of electron pairs between non-metal atoms. A single bond shares 1 pair, double bond shares 2 pairs, triple bond shares 3 pairs. Bond strength increases and bond length decreases as bond order increases.
Lewis (dot) structures: Diagrams showing all valence electrons, bonding pairs, and lone pairs around atoms. Follow the octet rule (8 electrons around each atom, except H which gets 2). Count total valence electrons, form bonds, distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs.
VSEPR theory: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion — electron pairs (bonding and lone) around a central atom arrange themselves to minimise repulsion. This determines molecular geometry. Lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs, compressing bond angles.
Common shapes: Linear (2 e⁻ domains, 180°), trigonal planar (3, 120°), tetrahedral (4, 109.5°), trigonal bipyramidal (5, 90°/120°), octahedral (6, 90°). With lone pairs: bent, trigonal pyramidal, T-shaped, seesaw, square planar, etc.
Polarity: A bond is polar if atoms have different electronegativities. A molecule is polar if it has polar bonds arranged asymmetrically (net dipole moment ≠ 0). Symmetric molecules (e.g., CO₂, CCl₄) are non-polar despite having polar bonds.
HL: Resonance: When a Lewis structure can be drawn in multiple valid ways (e.g., O₃, CO₃²⁻, benzene). The actual structure is a hybrid — an average of all resonance structures. Bond order is fractional.
HL: Hybridisation: The mixing of atomic orbitals to form new hybrid orbitals for bonding. sp³ (tetrahedral, 4 σ bonds), sp² (trigonal planar, 3 σ + 1 π), sp (linear, 2 σ + 2 π). The number of σ bonds + lone pairs on the central atom determines hybridisation.
Key Terms & Definitions
Covalent bond: A bond formed by the sharing of one or more pairs of electrons between atoms.
Lone pair: A pair of valence electrons not involved in bonding.
Electronegativity: The ability of an atom to attract a shared pair of electrons in a covalent bond.
VSEPR: Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion — a model for predicting molecular geometry.
Dipole moment: A measure of the polarity of a molecule, determined by the magnitude and direction of bond dipoles.
Sigma (σ) bond: A bond formed by head-on overlap of orbitals (single bonds).
Pi (π) bond: A bond formed by sideways overlap of p-orbitals (part of double/triple bonds).
Formal charge: The charge on an atom in a Lewis structure = valence e⁻ − lone pair e⁻ − ½(bonding e⁻). Preferred structures minimise formal charges.

S2.3 — The Metallic Model
Metallic bonding. Physical properties. HL Transition metals and d-electron delocalisation.
▸
Key Concepts
Metallic bonding: A lattice of positive metal cations surrounded by a "sea" of delocalised electrons. The electrostatic attraction between the cations and the delocalised electrons holds the structure together.
Properties explained: High melting points (strong attraction). Electrical conductivity (delocalised electrons can flow). Thermal conductivity (electrons transfer kinetic energy). Malleability and ductility (layers can slide past each other without breaking bonds — electrons adjust).
Factors affecting strength: More delocalised electrons per atom and smaller ionic radius → stronger metallic bonding → higher melting point. E.g., Na < Mg < Al (1, 2, 3 delocalised e⁻ respectively).
HL: Transition metals: Have partially filled d-orbitals. The d-electrons can also delocalise, contributing to very strong metallic bonding. This explains why transition metals generally have higher melting points than s-block metals.
Key Terms & Definitions
Metallic bond: The electrostatic attraction between a lattice of metal cations and delocalised electrons.
Delocalised electrons: Electrons not associated with a single atom but shared across the entire metallic lattice.
Alloy: A mixture of a metal with other metals or non-metals. Different-sized atoms disrupt regular layers, making alloys harder and less malleable.

S2.4 — From Models to Materials
Intermolecular forces: LDF, dipole-dipole, H-bonding. Allotropes of carbon. HL Polymers. Supramolecular chemistry.
▸
Key Concepts
Intermolecular forces (IMFs): Forces between molecules (NOT within them). Three types in order of increasing strength: London dispersion forces (LDF) — present in ALL molecules, stronger with more electrons/larger surface area. Dipole-dipole — between polar molecules. Hydrogen bonding — special strong dipole-dipole between H bonded to N/O/F and a lone pair on N/O/F.
Effect on physical properties: Stronger IMFs → higher boiling point, higher viscosity, lower vapour pressure. Water's unusually high BP for its size is due to extensive hydrogen bonding. Ice is less dense than liquid water (H-bonds create open lattice structure).
Allotropes of carbon: Diamond (giant covalent, each C bonded to 4 others, extremely hard, non-conductor). Graphite (layers of sp² carbon with delocalised electrons, soft/lubricant, conducts electricity). Fullerenes/graphene (molecular/2D forms with unique properties).
HL: Condensation polymers: Formed by combining monomers with the loss of small molecules (usually H₂O). Examples: polyesters (from diol + dicarboxylic acid), polyamides/nylon (from diamine + dicarboxylic acid), proteins (amino acids).
Key Terms & Definitions
London dispersion forces (LDF): Temporary, induced dipole-dipole forces caused by fluctuations in electron distribution. Present in all molecules.
Dipole-dipole forces: Attractions between the positive end of one polar molecule and the negative end of another.
Hydrogen bond: A strong intermolecular force between a hydrogen atom bonded to N, O, or F and a lone pair on another N, O, or F atom.
Allotrope: Different structural forms of the same element in the same physical state (e.g., diamond, graphite, fullerene for carbon).
Giant covalent structure: A structure where all atoms are connected by covalent bonds in a continuous network (e.g., diamond, SiO₂).

S3.1 — The Periodic Table: Classification of Elements

Key Concepts
Periodic table organisation: Arranged by increasing atomic number. Groups (columns) = same number of valence electrons = similar chemical properties. Periods (rows) = same number of occupied energy levels. Blocks: s-block (Groups 1-2), p-block (Groups 13-18), d-block (Groups 3-12), f-block (lanthanides/actinides).

HL: Transition metals (d-block): Characteristic properties include variable oxidation states, formation of coloured compounds, catalytic activity, and formation of complex ions. These arise from the partially filled d-orbitals.

Atomic radius: Half the distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms of the same element.

Electronegativity: The ability of an atom in a covalent bond to attract the shared pair of electrons toward itself (Pauling scale).
Electron affinity: The enthalpy change when an electron is added to a gaseous atom.
Transition metal: A d-block element that forms at least one ion with a partially filled d-sublevel.
Oxidation state: The charge an atom would have if all bonds were ionic. Transition metals can have multiple oxidation states.

Homologous series: A series of compounds with the same functional group and general formula, differing by CH₂. Members have similar chemical properties but gradually changing physical properties (increasing BP with chain length).

Stereoisomers: Molecules with the same structural formula but different spatial arrangements.

R1.1 — Measuring Enthalpy Changes
Exo/endothermic reactions. Energy profiles. Calorimetry. Bond enthalpies. HL Hess's law.
▸
Key Concepts
Enthalpy (H): The heat content of a system at constant pressure. We measure changes in enthalpy (ΔH), not absolute values. Exothermic: ΔH < 0 (releases heat, products lower energy). Endothermic: ΔH > 0 (absorbs heat, products higher energy).
Calorimetry: Measuring enthalpy changes experimentally using Q = mcΔT. Then ΔH = −Q/n (per mole of limiting reagent). Assumptions: no heat loss, specific heat capacity of solution = water (4.18 J/g/K), density = 1 g/cm³.
Bond enthalpies: Energy required to break one mole of a specific bond in the gaseous state (always endothermic). ΔH ≈ Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds formed). This gives only an estimate because bond enthalpies are averages.
Energy profiles: Diagrams showing energy on the y-axis and reaction progress on the x-axis. Show reactant/product energy levels, activation energy (Ea), and ΔH. The peak represents the transition state.
HL: Hess's law: The total enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the route taken. ΔH = Σ ΔH_f(products) − Σ ΔH_f(reactants), or ΔH = Σ ΔH_c(reactants) − Σ ΔH_c(products). Used to calculate ΔH for reactions that cannot be measured directly.
Key Terms & Definitions
Standard enthalpy change (ΔH°): Measured under standard conditions: 298 K, 100 kPa, 1 mol/dm³ solutions.
Enthalpy of formation (ΔH_f°): The enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states.
Enthalpy of combustion (ΔH_c°): The enthalpy change when one mole of a substance undergoes complete combustion under standard conditions.
Activation energy (Ea): The minimum energy required for a reaction to occur.
Bond enthalpy: The energy needed to break one mole of a bond in the gaseous state, averaged across different molecules.

R1.4 — Entropy & Spontaneity HL
Entropy (S). Predicting entropy changes. Gibbs free energy: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS. Spontaneity.
▸
Key Concepts
Entropy (S): A measure of the dispersal of energy and matter (the number of possible microstates). Greater disorder = higher entropy. S(gas) >> S(liquid) > S(solid). Entropy increases with temperature, number of particles, and complexity of molecules.
Predicting ΔS: Increase in number of gas molecules → positive ΔS. Phase change from solid to liquid to gas → positive ΔS. Dissolving a solid in water → usually positive ΔS. Decrease in gas moles → negative ΔS.

Key Terms & Definitions
Entropy (S): A thermodynamic quantity representing the degree of dispersal of energy and matter. Unit: J/(mol·K).
Gibbs free energy (G): The thermodynamic potential that determines whether a process is spontaneous at constant T and P.
Spontaneous reaction: A reaction that occurs without continuous external input of energy (ΔG < 0). Does not mean "fast."

Collision theory: For a reaction to occur, particles must (1) collide, (2) with sufficient energy (≥ Ea), and (3) with correct orientation. Rate depends on the frequency of successful collisions.

Activation energy (Ea): The minimum energy required for a successful collision (reaction to occur).
Catalyst: A substance that increases the rate of reaction without being consumed, by providing an alternative pathway with lower Ea.
Rate constant (k): The proportionality constant in the rate equation. Depends on temperature and Ea.
Order of reaction: The power to which a reactant's concentration is raised in the rate equation (determined experimentally).
Rate-determining step (RDS): The slowest elementary step in a reaction mechanism.

▸
R3.1 — Proton Transfer Reactions (Acids & Bases)
Brønsted-Lowry. pH scale. Strong vs weak. HL Lewis acids/bases. Ka, Kb, Kw. Buffers. pH curves.
▸
Key Concepts
Brønsted-Lowry theory: An acid is a proton (H⁺) donor; a base is a proton acceptor. In every acid-base reaction, a proton is transferred. Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by one H⁺.
pH scale: pH = −log₁₀[H⁺]. pH 7 = neutral (at 25°C), pH < 7 = acidic, pH > 7 = basic. Each unit change in pH = 10× change in [H⁺].
Strong vs weak: Strong acids/bases fully dissociate in water (HCl, NaOH). Weak acids/bases partially dissociate — equilibrium lies toward undissociated form (CH₃COOH, NH₃). Same concentration, same pH? No — weak acid has higher pH than strong acid.
HL: Ka and Kb: Ka = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA] for weak acids. Larger Ka = stronger acid. pKa = −log Ka. Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C. Ka × Kb = Kw for conjugate pairs.
HL: Buffer solutions: Resist changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. Made from a weak acid + its conjugate base (or weak base + conjugate acid). Buffer action: the weak acid neutralises added OH⁻; the conjugate base neutralises added H⁺.
HL: pH curves (titration curves): Plot pH vs volume of titrant added. Shape depends on strong/weak combinations. The equivalence point is where stoichiometric amounts have reacted. Indicator choice: pKa of indicator ≈ pH at equivalence point.
Key Terms & Definitions
Conjugate acid-base pair: Two species that differ by one proton (H⁺).
Amphiprotic / amphoteric: A substance that can act as both an acid and a base (e.g., water, amino acids).
Ka (acid dissociation constant): The equilibrium constant for the dissociation of a weak acid.
Kw (ionic product of water): [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C.
Buffer solution: A solution that resists pH changes upon addition of small amounts of acid or base.
Equivalence point: The point in a titration where the acid and base have reacted in stoichiometric proportions.

R3.2 — Electron Transfer Reactions (Redox)
Oxidation numbers. Half-equations. Electrochemical cells. HL E° values. Faraday's laws.
▸
Key Concepts
Redox: Oxidation = loss of electrons (increase in oxidation number). Reduction = gain of electrons (decrease in oxidation number). OIL RIG. They always occur together — one substance is oxidised while another is reduced.
Oxidation numbers: Rules: elements = 0, monatomic ions = charge, O usually −2, H usually +1. Sum in a compound = 0; in a polyatomic ion = ion charge. Used to track electron transfer.
Voltaic (galvanic) cell: Two different metals in solutions of their ions, connected by a wire and salt bridge. Spontaneous redox reaction generates electricity. Anode = oxidation (−), cathode = reduction (+). Salt bridge completes the circuit.
Electrolytic cell: Uses electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous reaction. Anode = oxidation (+), cathode = reduction (−). Applications: electroplating, extraction of reactive metals (e.g., aluminium), electrolysis of brine.
HL: Standard electrode potentials (E°): Measured relative to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE, E° = 0.00 V). More positive E° = stronger oxidising agent. Cell EMF: E°_cell = E°_cathode − E°_anode. Positive E°_cell → spontaneous reaction.
HL: Faraday's laws: The amount of substance deposited/dissolved at an electrode is proportional to the charge passed. Q = It, n = Q/(zF), where z = electrons transferred per ion, F = 96,485 C/mol.
Key Terms & Definitions
Oxidation: Loss of electrons / increase in oxidation number.
Reduction: Gain of electrons / decrease in oxidation number.
Oxidising agent: A substance that causes oxidation (itself gets reduced).
Reducing agent: A substance that causes reduction (itself gets oxidised).
Salt bridge: A connection between two half-cells allowing ion flow to maintain electrical neutrality.
Standard electrode potential (E°): The potential of a half-cell under standard conditions relative to the SHE.
Faraday constant (F): 96,485 C/mol — the charge carried by one mole of electrons.
Key Formulas
E°_cell = E°_cathode − E°_anode
HL: Cell EMF from standard electrode potentials
Q = I × t
HL: Charge from current and time
n = Q / (z × F)
HL: Moles deposited in electrolysis
Exam Focus
Assign oxidation numbers and identify which species is oxidised/reduced in a reaction.
Write and balance half-equations, then combine them into a full redox equation.
HL: Use E° values to predict spontaneity and calculate cell EMF. Perform Faraday's law calculations for electrolysis.
R3.3 — Electron Sharing Reactions (Radicals) HL
Radicals. Homolytic fission. Free radical substitution: initiation, propagation, termination.
▸
Key Concepts
Homolytic fission: A covalent bond breaks so that each atom gets one electron from the shared pair, forming two radicals (species with unpaired electrons). Denoted by a half-arrow (fishhook). Requires energy (UV light or heat).
Free radical substitution (e.g., CH₄ + Cl₂): Three stages: (1) Initiation: UV light causes homolytic fission of Cl₂ → 2Cl•. (2) Propagation: Cl• + CH₄ → CH₃• + HCl, then CH₃• + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + Cl• (chain reaction — radical regenerated). (3) Termination: two radicals combine (e.g., Cl• + Cl• → Cl₂).
Limitations: Free radical substitution produces a mixture of products (mono-, di-, tri-substituted) because substitution can continue. This makes it non-selective — low yield of a specific product.
Key Terms & Definitions
Radical (free radical): A species with one or more unpaired electrons. Highly reactive.
Homolytic fission: Breaking a covalent bond so each atom receives one electron, forming two radicals.
Initiation: The step that produces the first radicals (usually by UV light).
Propagation: Steps where a radical reacts with a molecule to form a product and regenerate a radical (chain continues).
Termination: Steps where two radicals combine, ending the chain.
Exam Focus
Write the three stages (initiation, propagation, termination) using correct curly arrow notation (half-arrows for radicals).
Explain why a mixture of products is formed and why this limits the usefulness of the reaction.
R3.4 — Electron-Pair Sharing Reactions HL
Nucleophiles, electrophiles. SN1, SN2 mechanisms. Electrophilic addition. Aromatic substitution. Condensation.
▸
Key Concepts
Nucleophiles and electrophiles: A nucleophile is an electron-pair donor (lone pair or π-electrons) — attacks positive/electron-deficient centres. An electrophile is an electron-pair acceptor — attacks negative/electron-rich centres.
SN2 mechanism: One-step process: nucleophile attacks the carbon simultaneously as the leaving group departs. Rate = k[substrate][nucleophile]. Favoured by primary substrates (less steric hindrance). Inversion of configuration at the carbon.
SN1 mechanism: Two-step process: (1) leaving group departs forming a carbocation, (2) nucleophile attacks the carbocation. Rate = k[substrate]. Favoured by tertiary substrates (stable carbocation). Racemisation (mixture of enantiomers).
Electrophilic addition: Alkenes react with electrophiles (HBr, Br₂, H₂O/H⁺). The π-bond attacks the electrophile, forming a carbocation intermediate, then a nucleophile attacks. Markovnikov's rule: H adds to the carbon with more H's already (more stable carbocation intermediate).
Electrophilic aromatic substitution: Benzene's delocalised π-system is attacked by an electrophile. A hydrogen is substituted (not added) to preserve aromaticity. Examples: nitration (HNO₃/H₂SO₄), halogenation (Br₂/FeBr₃ catalyst).
Condensation reactions: Two molecules join with the loss of a small molecule (usually H₂O). Examples: ester formation (alcohol + carboxylic acid → ester + H₂O), amide formation (amine + carboxylic acid → amide + H₂O).
Key Terms & Definitions
Nucleophile: A species that donates an electron pair to form a new covalent bond (e.g., OH⁻, NH₃, CN⁻, H₂O).
Electrophile: A species that accepts an electron pair to form a new covalent bond (e.g., H⁺, NO₂⁺, Br⁺).
Leaving group: An atom or group that departs with the bonding electrons during a substitution reaction.
Carbocation: A positively charged carbon intermediate. Stability: tertiary > secondary > primary.
Heterolytic fission: Breaking a bond so that both electrons go to one atom, forming a cation and an anion.
Markovnikov's rule: In HX addition to an alkene, H adds to the carbon with more hydrogens (forming the more stable carbocation).
Exam Focus
Draw full curly-arrow mechanisms for SN1, SN2, electrophilic addition, and electrophilic aromatic substitution.
Predict whether SN1 or SN2 is more likely based on the substrate (primary vs tertiary).
Apply Markovnikov's rule to predict the major product of addition to unsymmetrical alkenes.

Experimental Techniques SKILL
Titrations, calorimetry, distillation, chromatography, gravimetric analysis, spectrophotometry.
▸
Key Concepts
Titration: A volumetric technique to determine concentration. Add titrant from a burette to analyte in a flask until the equivalence point (detected by indicator colour change or pH meter). Back-titration used when the analyte is insoluble or slow-reacting.
Calorimetry: Measuring enthalpy changes using Q = mcΔT. Key: insulate to minimise heat loss. Use a graph (temperature vs time) to extrapolate the maximum temperature if heat loss occurs.
Chromatography: Separating mixtures based on different affinities for stationary and mobile phases. Rf = distance moved by spot / distance moved by solvent front. Different types: paper, TLC, column, gas chromatography.
Spectrophotometry / colorimetry: Measuring absorption of light by coloured solutions. Beer-Lambert law: A = εcl. Calibration curve method: measure absorbance of known concentrations, then read unknown concentration from the curve.
Exam Focus
Describe the procedure for a titration (including equipment, indicator choice, and how to identify the endpoint).
Use Rf values to identify substances from a chromatogram.
Explain how to use a calibration curve in spectrophotometry to find an unknown concentration.
Data Processing & Analysis SKILL
Uncertainty, error propagation, graphical analysis, significant figures.
▸
Key Concepts
Uncertainty: Every measurement has uncertainty. Analogue instruments: ± half the smallest division. Digital instruments: ± the smallest displayed digit. Percentage uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty / measured value) × 100%.
Error propagation: Addition/subtraction: add absolute uncertainties. Multiplication/division: add percentage uncertainties. Powers: multiply percentage uncertainty by the power.
Graphical analysis: Use error bars to show uncertainty. Best-fit line should pass through error bars. Max/min gradient lines give uncertainty in gradient. Linearisation of data (e.g., plotting 1/[A] vs t for second-order) allows determination of rate constants and other parameters.
Exam Focus
Propagate uncertainties through multi-step calculations correctly.
Draw graphs with error bars and determine uncertainty in gradient from max/min lines.
Express answers to the correct number of significant figures.
Scientific Investigation (IA)
Internal assessment: personal investigation. 3,000-word limit. Research question, methodology, analysis.
▸
Key Concepts
Research question: Must be focused, measurable, and answerable through primary experimental data. Should clearly define the independent and dependent variables.
Methodology: Detailed, reproducible procedure. Identify and control variables. Justify the range and number of trials. Include safety and ethical considerations.
Data analysis: Process raw data into meaningful results (tables, graphs, calculations). Include uncertainties. Use appropriate statistical tools. Relate results back to the research question with scientific reasoning.
Evaluation: Discuss strengths and weaknesses of the method. Identify systematic and random errors with specific suggestions for improvement. Assess whether the conclusion is justified by the data.
Exam Focus
The IA is worth 20% of the final grade. Focus on a clear, focused research question and thorough error analysis.
Quality of data processing and evaluation matters more than perfect results.
